    When the First Bagger, David Carr, Hit the Red Carpet

    By Cara Buckley
    February 13, 2015 9:54 am February 13, 2015 9:54 am

    It’s a dark day at The New York Times, and particularly here on the Carpetbagger beat, which the inimitable David Carr, who died last night, started a decade ago.

    Sui generis, blunt to a fault and almost like a pulp fiction character brought to life, Mr. Carr had the world’s most finely tuned radar for bull. And rather than being remotely beguiled by Hollywood’s pageantry, he took wicked delight in shaking a stick at it. “Remember,” he told the latest Bagger in her introductory video, referring to Hollywood’s stars and publicists, “they’re not your friends, they’re your fake friends.”

    Whether filming his Carpetbagger videos in his dank basement in New Jersey or annoying the hell out of Paul Thomas Anderson, who once yelled at him, “you know nothing about film!” (Mr. Carr said he replied, “I like films that tell stories, is all,”), Mr. Carr covered Hollywood’s awards season in a way bigwigs still talk about.

    He also freely shared his thoughts, sources and advice, whether welcome or not. One of his early tidbits to this Bagger included this, from a few months ago. Feeling her early coverage was a bit too starry-eyed, he told her to “hose it off a little,” adding, “They’re not that important.” Sure it stung a little, but, as he nearly always was, David was absolutely right.
