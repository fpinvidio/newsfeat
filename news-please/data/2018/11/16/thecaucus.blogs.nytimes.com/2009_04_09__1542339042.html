    A Clinton Carnival of Prizes

    April 9, 2009 6:04 pm April 9, 2009 6:04 pm

    Would you like to spend a day with Bill Clinton, followed by a free weekend in New York City? Would you like to attend the season finale of American Idol? Would you like to talk politics over lunch with James Carville and Paul Begala?

    This is not a game show, but rather three potential prizes in exchange for helping to retire the presidential campaign debt for Hillary Rodham Clinton.

    An unusual fund-raising appeal arrived in the e-mail boxes of prospective donors on Thursday afternoon. It’s the latest effort to pay down the campaign debt that Mrs. Clinton racked up during her long primary challenge with President Obama.

    “I knew it was going to take an extraordinary effort to help pay off Hillary Clinton’s campaign debt. But now, I think we can do it and have some fun at the same time!” Mr. Carville wrote in the fund-raising appeal. “I won’t spend a lot of time trying to convince you to help Hillary. I know what she means to you, and I’m sure you know how important it is for her to have her campaign pay off all its obligations.”

    As Secretary of State in the Obama administration, Mrs. Clinton cannot take part in her own fund-raising efforts. So she has turned to her loyal circle of supporters – along with her husband’s – to help pay down the bills (even if she kisses off the personal loans she made to the campaign.)

    With contributions in a slump – particularly for someone like Mrs. Clinton who is no longer in the day-to-day business of politicking – fund-raising organizers have taken to finding creative ways to raise money. Winning a day with Mr. Clinton, a ringside seat to American Idol or a lunch with two Democratic operatives is the latest volley.

    “Your contribution today not only gives you the chance at winning one of these fantastic prizes – it’ll also help our dear friend, Hillary Clinton, pay off the very last of her campaign debt,” Mr. Carville said. “This means a whole lot to Hillary, and I know she appreciates everything you do for her.”

    A spokeswoman for Mrs. Clinton said that the campaign debt was $5.9 million at the end of last year, and a new financial report is due April 15.

    So still need to think about it?

    “These amazing prizes are only being offered online and are available only for a limited time,” Mr. Carville said. “So please don’t delay in acting today.”

    DC

    You make many fair observations, but let me refute one or two:

    1) When Kennedy campaigned in1980, Carter had been President for four years and was arguably one of our most liberal presidents ever. He and Kennedy were no further apart on the issues than Hillary and Obama. Kennedy saw an opening because of Carter’s declining popularity as the summer, the economic downturn and the Iran hostage crisis dragged on, and believed he would be the more viable candidate against Reagan. We’ll never know if he was right, but it’s truly amazing, when you think about it, that Kennedy carried his fight all the way to the convention against a sitting President who was ahead by a large margin — and nobody, not the Democratic leadership, not the media, not the voters (of course, we didn’t have the blogosphere back then) saw any particular problem with that. He certainly wasn’t vilified for being a power-hungry, monomaniacally ambitious, selfish,willing-to-say-or-do-anything-to-win S.O.B. And although his family fortune dwarfs Hillary’s, I don’t believe anyone begrudged his fund-raising efforts.

    2) Chris Matthews wasn’t the only media personality with a tingle up his leg for Obama and a decidedly sexist slant on Hillsry’s candidacy. You can throw in all those middle-aged white guys at MSNBC and virtually everybody at CNN with the exception of Paul Begala (who can forget Alex Castellanis saying Hillary deserved to be called a bit**, or Jamal Simmons saying in April, when asked if he thought Hillary had a valid argument to make to the superdelegates that she would be a stronger candidate against McCain, “well sure, if my aunt grew a male appendage, she’d be my uncle, but it ain’t happenin’, Hillary!”) Like it or not, the media plays a role in shaping the narrative or an election and shaping voters’ perceptions.
    3) There were many valid objections to raise about Palin’s qualifications, but the fact of the matter is that for the first week or so after McCain announced his choice, all we heard about was her reproductive history, her fitness as a mother, her ability to juggle the demands of career and family, etc. Questioning her other qualifications was not sexist, but vilifying her for choosing to fly back to Alaska to deliver her youngest child, or choosing to expose her fetus to the risks of amniocentesis, or accepting McCain’s offer when she had a child with special needs to tend to — that was most definitely sexist.

    In the first three months of the year, Hillary Clinton paid off $3.7 million in bills left over from her failed presidential campaign, according to a report her campaign filed Wednesday afternoon with the Federal Election Commission.The report shows that Clinton has only one vendor left to pay off: pollster Mark Penn. Her campaign paid his firm $3 million in the first quarter, but still owes it $2.3 million. The filing also reveals that the campaign brought in $5.6 million between January 1 and March 31, an impressive sum given that Clinton has relied on surrogates to raise money to pay off her debts since January, when she was confirmed as secretary of state for her Democratic primary rival, President Barack Obama. Ethics rules and tradition restrict diplomats from engaging in partisan political activity, including raising cash to pay off the debt. Though Clinton had $2.6 million in the bank at the end of March, she couldn’t use that to pay off the remainder of the Penn debt because her campaign operation still has overhead costs it needs to pay. For instance, in the first quarter, the campaign paid $9,400 in salary to staffers in New York and Washington, plus $2,300 in phone services, $7,000 for website maintenance and $30,000 in travel costs.
