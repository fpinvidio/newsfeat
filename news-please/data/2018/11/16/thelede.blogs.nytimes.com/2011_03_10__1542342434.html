    March 10: Updates on Libya’s Revolt and Middle East Protests

    By J. David Goodman
    March 10, 2011 7:45 am March 10, 2011 7:45 am

    On Thursday, The Lede is following the uprising in Libya and protest movements across North Africa and the Middle East. Updates below mix alerts on breaking news with reports from bloggers and journalists on the ground. Readers can also follow some of the discussion of the region on Twitter in this blog’s right column.

    7:03 P.M. A Wrap-Up of the Day’s Developments

    Mazen Mahdi/EPA A protest in front of Bahrain’s Ministry of Education on Thursday.

    Before The Lede signs off for the night, a quick rundown of what’s going on across various hot spots in the Arab world as the region braces for another day of protests on Friday:

    LIBYA

    Rebels fled the oil town of Ras Lanuf as warplanes stretched their bombardments east to Brega.

    France became the first country to recognize the rebel leaders as the legitimate government, and Gulf Arab states, meeting in Riyadh, said that the Colonel Qaddafi had lost his legitimacy.

    But as the diplomatic reins appeared to grow tighter around the Libyan leader, his most prominent son, Seif al-Islam, promised that the government would not surrender.

    SAUDI ARABIA

    Saudi police used gunfire to disperse a crowd of more than 100 protesters in the eastern city of Al Qatif. An Interior Ministry spokesman told Bloomberg that the shots were fired over the crowd.

    Activists have called for large scale protests on Friday.

    BAHRAIN

    The Associated Press reported that on Thursday tempers boiled over in an angry sectarian confrontation that had to be broken up by police as hundreds of Sunnis and Shiites faced off in a schoolyard in the town of Saar.

    On Friday, Shiite protest organizers have planned a march on the royal court, Reuters reported. Protesters still control a central square in the capital, Manama, where they have been camping out since mid-February.

    EGYPT

    Thousands attended funerals on Thursday for eight Coptic Christians who were killed in sectarian violence in Cairo on Tuesday, posting video online.

    YEMEN

    My colleague Laura Kasinof reports that: President Ali Abdullah Saleh proposed giving more power to Parliament in an attempt to quell sustained protests that have continued for over a month.

    But a leader of the antigovernment protesters staging a sit-in in the capital, Sana, quickly rejected the offer.

    6:10 P.M. More on the Gunfire at Saudi Protests

    Reuters Protesters marched on Thursday in Saudi Arabia, demanding the release of prisoners.

    A Saudi Interior Ministry spokesperson confirmed that police had used gunfire to disperse a crowd of more than 100 protesters in the eastern city of Al Qatif on Thursday but told news agencies that the police had fired into the air above the protesters, not at them.

    The ministry spokesman, Maj. Gen. Mansour al-Turki, told Bloomberg that three people had been injured in the confrontation, including one police officer. He did not elaborate on how they were hurt.

    A Wall Street Journal reporter in Riyadh said the police had fired rubber bullets; that report could not be confirmed independently.

    Tensions are high in Saudi Arabia ahead of Friday’s planned protests, as my colleague Nada Bakri reports:

    The clash with protesters in the heavily Shiite region underscored long-standing tensions in Saudi society: A sense among its Shiite minority that it is discriminated against by a government practicing a zealous form of Sunni orthodoxy. One resident in Qatif who watched the march, Abdulwahab al-Oraid, said he it was not clear why police opened fire at what appeared to be a peaceful demonstration that started with 100 people and later grew to about 300.“There is a fear of Friday’s protests,” Mr. Oraid said. “We think this is a message, ‘Don’t protest in any Shiite areas on Friday.’”

    5:03 P.M. The Retreat From Ras Lanuf

    Tyler Hicks/The New York Times Libyan jets circled overhead in an area that had been repeatedly hit by airstrikes.

    As loyalist forces pushed rebel forces from Ras Lanuf with repeated airstrikes and shelling from tanks, many in the news media who had been covering the fighting dropped back as well. But not before capturing images in video and photographs of the battle and the ensuing flight.

    The New Yorker’s Jon Lee Anderson, writing on the magazine’s news blog, described how the scene had been transformed from several days ago, when rebel forces appeared to have the momentum, advancing westward towards Colonel Qaddafi’s stronghold at Surt:

    Gone was the festive spirit, the singing and posing with weapons that had characterized the advancing shabbab of a week ago, though they still fired their weapons in the air. Most of the young volunteer fighters seemed tense and angry, and a few were out of control. I watched one man in the distance throw a tantrum, screaming loudly in the middle of the road, apparently upset at the retreat. The remaining anti-aircraft batteries began shooting, and a panic ensued when some people thought they spotted a jet fighter. I had to dodge a flurry of large brass shell casings that spat off a heavy machine firing near me.

    Tyler Hicks/The New York Times Rebels fired anti-aircraft guns outside of Ras Lanuf on Thursday.

    Before night fell, Colonel Qaddafi’s forces had pressed the aerial bombardment eastward, striking the rebel-controlled oil town of Brega:

    Lynsey Addario for The New York Times Rebel fighters ran from a checkpoint in Brega after an airstrike as the plane circled back around.
    4:00 P.M. Son of Qaddafi is Defiant in Interview

    Here’s video from ITN News in Britain showing an interview on Thursday with Seif al-Islam Qaddafi, one of the embattled Libyan leader’s sons. In it he promises that his father’s government would “never ever surrender” and tells reporters that “Libya is not a piece of cake.”

    2:39 P.M. Reports of Gunfire at Saudi Protests

    Reports are coming out of Saudi Arabia that security forces there opened fire on small protests in the eastern part of the country. Several Western news agencies cited witnesses in reporting the incidents. A report also appeared on a Shiite news service, Rasid.

    These reports have not yet been independently confirmed. It can be extremely difficult for foreign news organizations to gather timely and accurate news from inside the kingdom.

    Reuters reported sporadic gun fire mixed with with the noise of percussion bombs at a demonstration; it gave no word on any casualties or whether security forces had fired live rounds or rubber bullets.

    In recent days, Saudi authorities have moved to ban all protests and have beefed up security forces to deal with demonstrators. If the reports of firing on Thursday are correct, the crackdown would seem intended to hammer home the message that larger protests planned for a “day of rage” on Friday would be met with a harsh response. Tens of thousands of people have signed on to a Facebook page calling for the protests.

    Video clips that appeared online rapidly — ahead even of the news agency reports — were said to show demonstrations on Thursday night in the eastern town of Al Qatif, the site of small protests last week. The video differed from those posted last week; its authenticity could not be immediately verified.

    A Twitter user, Chan’ad Bahraini, posted a link to this video and translated the protesters’ chant as: “Sunni and Shia are brothers — this homeland is not for sale.”

    Earlier in the day, there were reports of security forces pouring into the area around the protests.

    Rasid.com: Tens of military vehicles on the highway, heading to #Qatif, eastern #Saudi Arabia, in case of any demonstrationsThu Mar 10 15:07:22 via webHasan Almustafa
    halmustafa

    Before the reports of violence, Amnesty International called on Saudi Arabia to reverse its policy and allow demonstrations. “Instead of banning peaceful protests, the Saudi Arabian authorities should address the need for major human rights reform in the country,” said Philip Luther, deputy director of Amnesty International’s Middle East and North Africa program, in a statement.

    Meanwhile, a young Saudi blogger, Ahmed Al Omran, writing in The Guardian on Thursday before the reported crackdown, said he no longer had high hopes that the wave of unrest sweeping over the Middle East would result in political reform in Saudi Arabia:

    I have become very pessimistic about the prospects of reform for my country. The huge age gap between the young population and the ruling elite makes it nearly impossible for the ruled and the rulers to communicate and understand each other. We practically speak two different languages, and I don’t see how the government can keep up with our aspirations.

    2:00 P.M. Video of Funerals in Cairo for Coptic Christians

    My colleague Robert Mackey points out that a Cairene blogger and human rights activist, Sarah Carr, has posted video on YouTube and photographs on Flickr showing Thursday’s funerals for eight Coptic Christians who were killed in sectarian violence in Cairo on Tuesday.

    Writing on Flickr about the funerals in Cairo’s Manshayyet Nasser district, Ms. Carr, who is a researcher for the Egyptian Initiative for Personal Rights, reported that many of the people she spoke to there said that the victims had been shot to death and blamed Egypt’s military for their deaths.

    12:57 P.M. Loyalists Turn Eastward After Ras Lanuf

    There were signs on Thursday that Colonel Qaddafi’s forces were trying to push the fight further eastward into rebel-held strongholds following their success in wresting back the strategic oil city of Ras Lanuf from the rebels.

    Just east of Ras Lanuf, rebels in Brega braced for new attacks, and warplanes were reportedly seen overhead.

    Jet flying over al-Brega…oppostion fighters shooting in air. #LibyaThu Mar 10 16:53:47 via webbenwedeman
    bencnn

    Seif al-Islam, a son of the Libyan dictator, told Reuters in an interview on Thursday that the government was preparing an offensive. “We will never give up,” he said. “We will never surrender.”

    In Benghazi, the main city in rebel-held eastern Libya, Al Jazeera reports on the effort women there to feed and support rebel fighters.

    12:27 P.M. Report That Libya Will Release Dutch Aviators

    The three Dutch soldiers who were arrested last month by the Libyan government will reportedly be released on Thursday, news reports in the Netherlands said, citing Libyan state television.

    The Libyan state broadcaster said that the Dutch soldiers would be handed over to delegations from Greece and Malta, but it did not provide further details, news agencies reported.

    The three soldiers, members of a Dutch naval helicopter crew, were captured by loyalist forces on Feb. 27 while they were engaged in an operation to rescue European citizens fleeing the conflict in Libya, according to a spokesman for the Dutch Defense Ministry.

    The crew’s helicopter had landed in the city of Surt, Colonel Qaddafi’s hometown and one of his easternmost remaining strongholds.

    11:49 A.M. Times Photographer Describes Action at Ras Lanuf

    Tyler Hicks/The New York Times Rebels outside Ras Lanuf on Wednesday.

    Lens, the New York Times photography blog, interviewed Tyler Hicks about photographing the conflict near Ras Lanuf, Libya, where rebels appeared to be losing their hold on the town as loyalist forces escalate their assault.

    Mr. Hicks, a staff photographer for The Times who has covered conflicts in Kosovo, Chechnya, Congo, Ethiopia, Sudan, Iraq and Afghanistan, said that the fighting he witnessed on Wednesday outside the Libyan oil town may have been the most intense he had ever seen firsthand, with the “most firepower — coming and going”:

    That fighting includes bombing from jets, shooting from helicopters and incoming mortar and heavy machine gun fire. This is all happening over relatively flat terrain, with very little cover. So when you commit yourself to working out there, there’s not a whole lot you can to do protect yourself. I’m always looking for some kind of cover. But the bombs they’re dropping are huge. You can’t run from them, because you don’t know where they’re going to land. Even if you are wearing a flak jacket and a helmet, if you were hit by one of these bombs from a jet, the clothes wouldn’t do you very much good.

    11:42 A.M. Libyan Loyalists Said to Take Zawiyah

    Deborah Haynes of The Times of London said on Thursday that she had been through the city of Zawiyah and that it had been taken by Colonel Qaddafi’s forces. She said the city had suffered substantial damage in the fighting, which lasted several days. Her report could not be independently confirmed.

    Earlier on Thursday, the rebels appeared to be holding out their ground in Zawiyah as the loyalists mounted more attacks and propaganda efforts. Each side in the conflict has claimed to be in control of the town.

    11:28 A.M. Intelligence Chief Thinks Qaddafi Will Not Give Up

    Muammar el-Qaddafi “is in this for the long haul,” James Clapper, the Obama administration’s director of national intelligence, told the Senate Armed Services committee on Thursday in testimony about the Libyan conflict and the broader state of the Middle East.

    “He appears to be hunkering down for the duration,” Mr. Clapper said of the embattled Libyan strongman, according to Reuters, and that “over the longer term, that the regime will prevail.”

    My colleague Brian Knowlton writes that Mr. Clapper’s comments “may create awkwardness within the administration; President Obama has said that Colonel Qaddafi has lost legitimacy with his people and must step down.”

    Anna Stewart of CNN International noted another observation from Mr. Clapper:

    10:26 A.M. France Said to Favor Air Strikes to Aid Rebels

    Roberto Schmidt/Agence France-Presse — Getty Images A tank shell exploding near rebel fighters outside of Ras Lanuf on Thursday.

    Le Monde reports that France supports the idea of mounting selective air strikes to stop the Libyan military from bombing rebel positions in eastern Libya from the air and to help the rebels retake ground from government forces.

    Forces loyal to Colonel Qaddafi have been hitting Ras Lanuf, an important oil town in eastern Libya, and oil facilities on its outskirts forcefully from the air.

    Reports from there were mixed on Thursday. At least one rebel told news agencies that government forces, strongly aided by air attacks, had managed to drive rebels from the town. “We’ve been defeated,” a rebel who gave his name only as Osama told Agence France-Presse. “They are shelling, and we are running away.”

    As my colleagues David D. Kirkpatrick and Anthony Shadid report:

    Under a steadily escalating barrage, rebel fighters in dozens of trucks mounted with heavy weapons retreated east along the coastal road. In the chaotic scene at a checkpoint five miles east of town, the fighters shot anti-aircraft guns randomly and ineffectually into the sky while arguing whether to flee or to try to establish a new defensive front.

    The Qaddafi government severed relations with France on Thursday after France recognized the rebel leadership in Benghazi as the legitimate government of Libya.

    Le Monde also reported that Colonel Qaddafi has threatened President Sarkozy with the revelation of an “important secret.”

    The paper cited an announcement from the Libyan state news agency that the government in Tripoli had “learned of an important secret that is going to cause the fall of Sarkozy.” The state news agency said the “secret” was connected to campaign finances, but did not elaborate or give any details.

    8:08 A.M. Europe Steps Up Relations With Rebels

    The French government said on Thursday that it would recognize Libya’s rebel leadership, making it the first government to do so, and would soon exchange ambassadors as Europe appeared to inch closer to a policy for assisting rebel forces.

    As my colleagues Alan Cowell and Steven Erlanger write:

    The move was a victory for the Libyan National Council in its quest for recognition and a setback for Col. Muammar el-Qaddafi who has been seeking whatever international support he can as NATO members in Brussels began a debate about the possible imposition of a no-fly zone over Libya.

    President Nicolas Sarkozy also became the first head of state to meet with rebel leaders, sitting down in Paris on Thursday with Mahmoud Jibril and Ali Al-Esawi of the Libyan National Council.

    At the same time, the Spanish newspaper El Pais reported that a Spanish government official met with rebels in Benghazi on Saturday, in what appeared to be the first official contact between a foreign government and rebel forces within Libya.

    And a British Foreign Office spokesman told the BBC that: “The interim national council are valid interlocutors, with whom we wish to work closely.”

    Meanwhile, Russia’s foreign minister, Sergei Lavrov, said on Thursday that his country would “closely study” any policy of providing support for rebel forces but called discussion of a no-flight zone premature and military intervention “unacceptable.”

    “In the United Nations charter and other international accords, it is clearly stated that each nation has the right to decide its own future,” Mr. Lavrov said during a meeting in Moscow, Reuters reported.

    “Intervention in internal affairs, especially military interference, is unacceptable.”

    7:44 a.m. |More Journalists Missing Near Zawiyah

    A day after the BBC described the abuse of three of its journalists by forces loyal to Col. Muammar el-Qaddafi, the Guardian reports that one of its correspondents has gone missing in the same area of Libya.

    The reporter, Ghaith Abdul-Ahad, was last seen on Sunday on the outskirts of the town of Zawiyah, the town about 30 miles outside the capital, Tripoli, where rebels and government forces have fought fierce battles in recent days. It is also the same area where the BBC reporters were arrested on Monday.

    “The Guardian has been in contact with Libyan government officials in Tripoli and London and asked them to urgently give all assistance in the search for Abdul-Ahad and to establish if he is in the custody of the authorities,” the paper said. He was traveling with Andrei Netto of the Brazilian newspaper Estado, the Guardian reported, who is also missing.

    Reporters, especially those who accepted an invitation from Colonel Qaddafi to come to Tripoli, have chafed under what they see as manipulation by the Libyan government, seeking to break free of its restrictions to report on events on the ground. As Reporters Without Borders writes:

    Gaddafi’s regime uses doublespeak with regard to these journalists. He has invited them because he needs them to spread his propaganda, but his attitude to them is hateful and contemptible. He accuses them of spreading false information on what is happening in Libya and has no qualms about referring to them as “stray dogs.”

    Those who operate without government authorization are said to be considered Al Qaeda accomplices by forces loyal to Colonel Qaddafi.
