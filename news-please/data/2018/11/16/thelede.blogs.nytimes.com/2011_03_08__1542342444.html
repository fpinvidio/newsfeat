    March 8: Updates on Libya’s Revolt and Middle East Protests

    By J. David Goodman
    March 8, 2011 8:41 am March 8, 2011 8:41 am

    On Tuesday, The Lede is following the uprising in Libya and protest movements across North Africa and the Middle East. Updates below mix alerts on breaking news with reports from bloggers and journalists on the ground. Readers can also follow some of the discussion of the region on Twitter in this blog’s right column.

    6:38 P.M.Qaddafi Appears at Hotel, Shuns Waiting Press

    The gaggle of more than 100 foreign journalists waited in a Tripoli hotel on Tuesday night.

    And waited.

    And then, all of a sudden, after hours of waiting, it happened.

    Col. Muammar el-Qaddafi swooped through the hotel around midnight on Tuesday, later than promised but still keeping his date. Cameras delivered shaky pictures of the ensuing pandemonium as over a hundred journalists, who had been made to believe that the embattled Libyan leader would speak to the press, chased after his orange blur.

    In the end, he spoke only to Turkish television and departed, leaving a large number of journalists with easy access to Twitter rather unhappy.

    More than a few observers on Twitter commented that the event had the appearance of a distraction, keeping scores of foreign reporters detained far from Colonel Qaddafi’s increasingly brutal response to the rebel uprising.

    The London-based International News Safety Institute reported earlier on Tuesday that the situation in Libya was growing “increasingly precarious” for Western reporters.

    Several journalists were detained trying to get in to Zawiya, about 30 miles west of Tripoli. Some were held overnight, at least one was beaten.

    The Lede is signing off for the evening but will return to continue following the uprising in Libya and protest movements across North Africa and the Middle East on Wednesday. In the meantime, please visit the homepage of NYTimes.com to read reports from our colleagues in the region.

    6:02 P.M.Saudi Arabia Braces for Planned Protests
    In Qatar, an effort to put a cute face on state censorship.

    Other than Bahrain, where weeks of protests have threatened the government, the oil-rich states of the Persian Gulf have mostly been spared any major street protests — so far.

    But small demonstrations cropped up in Saudi Arabia for the first time on Friday, and more than a thousand people marched in Kuwait on Tuesday. And there are signs that the monarchies of the Arabian Peninsula may be worrying about what comes next.

    Would-be organizers in Saudi Arabia have set this Friday, March 11, as a day for large-scale street demonstrations. Al Jazeera, which is based in Qatar, has released a special report looking at the situation in Saudi Arabia, including efforts by the Saudi government to beef up its security forces before the end of the week. “This is Saudi Arabia’s nightmare, isn’t it?” the anchor asks, referring to the planned wave of protests.

    In what appeared to be olive branches extended in advance of the planned marches, King Abdullah, the ailing Saudi ruler, met on Tuesday with leaders of the country’s Shiite minority, and freed more than two dozen protesters who had been detained last week, Reuters reported.

    The object of their protests — a detained cleric, Sheikh Tawfic al-Amer, who has called for greater rights for Shiites — was freed on Sunday, another apparent olive branch.

    At the same time, the monarchy has moved to ban all kinds of demonstrations, leaving the country in a state of tense anticipation. As The Guardian reports:

    All this week, government agencies have been issuing statements banning protests. First it was the interior ministry that promised to take all measures necessary to prevent protests. Then the highest religious establishment, the Council of Senior Clerics, deemed protests and petitions as un-Islamic. The Shura Council, our government-appointed pretend-parliament, also threw its weight behind the interior ministry’s ban and the religious decree of prohibition.

    In contrast, the emirate of Qatar is striving to put a friendly face on its efforts to reign in antigovernment speech and other Web content that it deems unacceptable, by means of a cartoon message that pops up when a site has been blocked by the authorities.

    It’s censorship done cute. Will that matter to those who chafe under its constraints?

    5:09 P.M.A Crackdown in Yemen
    Khaled Abdullah/Reuters Women demanded the ouster of the president of Yemen outside Sana University on Tuesday. Later, the army attacked protesters at the university.

    For weeks, the government of President Ali Abdullah Saleh allowed students in Yemen to protest against his rule at a university near the center of the capital, Sana. Evidently that tolerance ran out on Tuesday.

    The Associated Press reports that the Yemeni army stormed into Sana University, firing rubber bullets and tear gas canisters at students, in an effort to break up protests and a tent encampment at the university’s main gate. Reuters reported that at least 20 people were wounded in the attack.

    Elsewhere in the country, protests calling for Mr. Saleh to resign appeared to be spreading. My colleague Laura Kasinof reports that for the first time, a large demonstration took place Tuesday in the city of Dhamar, a ruling-party stronghold south of the capital where many high-level government figures are from, including the minister of the interior and the speaker of Parliament.

    Earlier in the day, a large number of female protesters had joined the crowd of students at Sana University. It was unclear how many of them were still there when the crackdown began.

    Yemen, one of the poorest countries in the Middle East, has been showing signs of strain under the constant protests that have tied up parts of the capital and several other cities. The foreign minister appealed to the country’s rich Gulf neighbors for $6 billion in additional aid to cope with an economic crisis gripping the country.

    “The threats and conspiracies aimed at Yemen’s unity, security and stability pose a threat to the security of the region and the world,” the foreign minister, Abu Bakr Al Qirbi, told a meeting of the Gulf Cooperation Council, according to the official Saba news agency.

    4:11 P.M.Combat Video From the Streets of Zawiyah

    There were conflicting reports on Tuesday about which side controlled the Libyan city of Zawiyah. In a video report published on Tuesday, Alex Crawford of Sky News makes clear that the city has become a focal point both for rebel fighters and for the forces loyal to Colonel Qaddafi.

    Ms. Crawford, apparently the only Western journalist who has been able to file a firsthand report on the fighting in Zawiyah over the weekend, put together a compelling video of firefights in the streets in her 10-minute account.

    A large group of Libyan soldiers who defected during the unrest have organized a rebel militia in the city led by a 10-man military council, she reports, and are digging in “for a fight to the death.”

    By Sunday, roads to Martyr’s Square int he city center were littered with the detritus of war: burned-out armored vehicles, wrecked anti-aircraft guns and pickup trucks that had been destroyed in the fast urban fighting. At the conclusion of her report, the ragtag rebels maintained a tenuous hold; two days later, the situation in Zawiyah appeared to be in flux.

    3:52 P.M.Egyptian Army Said to Detain Security Chief

    Al Jazeera reports that the Egyptian army detained the head of the state security services on Tuesday. The report could not be immediately confirmed.

    The arrest may have resulted from events on Saturday at the headquarters of the hated security apparatus. Protesters stormed the building and confiscated documents that were said to show evidence of abuses.

    Some of the people who entered the building on Saturday claimed to have been previously detained there, and described their treatment in video reports that were recorded as they marched through the building’s corridors and cells, in what bloggers called “Bastille Day in Egypt.” We posted some of them here.

    In another effort to investigate the brutality committed under President Mubarak, Amnesty International released video clips on Tuesday that were recorded at a Cairo morgue in early February. The group said the videos showed evidence of abusive treatment that amounted to “horrific torture” at Al-Fayoum Prison, the largest in Egypt. The bodies shown in the video included wounds from burns and bullets as well as missing fingers and nails. (Note: The video contains disturbing images of corpses.)

    Amnesty International called for an immediate investigation. Under the Mubarak regime, the prison system was the responsibility of the minister of information, who resigned during antigovernment protests in late January.

    2:56 P.M.Sectarian Clashes in Egypt

    The police in Cairo fired shots into the air to separate groups of Coptic Christians and Muslims on Tuesday when tensions between them threatened to turn violent, the Associated Press reports.

    Thousands of Christian Egyptians — including a crowd of garbage collectors, who are predominantly Christians — gathered around the capital to demonstrate for equal rights and against the discrimination and harassment that the Christian minority, which dates back 18 centuries, has faced in recent years. The demonstrators were confronted by crowds of militant Muslims who insist on the primacy of their religion in the country.

    Hossam El-Hamalawy, a journalist and blogger, posted a video on Tuesday that was said to show Christian and Muslim Egyptians marching together to protest sectarian violence, an apparent effort to calm the waters.

    The Mubarak regime generally kept a lid on trouble between the groups, but with the relaxing of oppression in the aftermath of the revolution, pent-up tensions and grievances have come to the fore.

    1:56 P.M.After the Revolution, Gender Friction in Egypt

    A march in Cairo marking International Women’s Day apparently turned ugly on Tuesday. Several hundred women and supporters of women’s rights reported harassment from counterprotesters who taunted and, in some cases, physically intimidated them.

    Twitter reports flooded in from women who attended the march in Tahrir Square, the epicenter of antigovernment protests last month. Many said they fled after feeling menaced and unsafe.

    CNN posted a snippet of video from the event, which organizers had hoped would draw tens of thousands of women. The video shows groups of mostly young people.

    “Men are men, and women are women, and that will never change, and go home, that’s where you belong,” chanted some in the group opposing the women’s march, according to a translation by the television network.

    Another video said to be from the event and posted by Egyptocracy showed crowds of men chanting “False, false, get out, get.”

    The confrontation that began in the square continued online, as some Twitter users spewed hateful messages about women while others argued back. (A search of Twitter for some combination of #iwd, #women, #tahrir and #egypt ought to turn up some bathroom-wall-worthy samples.)

    The apparently aggressive reaction from some Egyptian men came as the United Nations human rights chief, Navi Pillay, appealed to the new governments of Egypt and Tunisia to ensure that protections for women’s rights are included in the new constitutions each country is writing.

    In a speech for International Women’s Day, she said: “The courage and determination of women in the Middle East and North Africa should be a source of inspiration for all of us, women, and men striving to achieve full respect for human rights in general, and the right to education for girls and women in particular.”

    Women played a major role in the protest movement that forcecd the resignation of President Hosni Mubarak, according to Widney Brown, the senior director of law and policy at Amnesty International, who wrote an opinion essay in the Egyptian newspaper Al Masry Al Youm on Tuesday. Since Mr. Mubarak’s departure, though, woman are at risk of being sidelined again, she writes in the newspaper essay:

    Incredibly, despite decades of discrimination and inequality, women are being denied a role in the creation of a new Egypt. They are being excluded by both the caretaker government and the international community. Most recently, a new national committee formed to write the new Egyptian constitution was composed only of men. This is not acceptable.

    12:38 P.M.More Debate Over a No-Fly Zone Over Libya

    To no-fly or not to no-fly?

    Western leaders continued to wrestle with that question on Tuesday. Britain and France are pushing the United Nations to make plans for how a no-fly zone over Libya would work, while a German politician insisted that a Muslim country be included in any such effort.

    “Were such a zone to be set up, at least one Islamic nation would have to take part,” said Ruprecht Polenz, who leads the German parliament’s Foreign Affairs committee and belongs to the same party as the chancellor, Angela Merkel.

    “It’s not enough if the U.N. Security Council gives an appropriate mandate,” Reuters quoted Mr. Polentz as saying. His remarks did not suggest any specific Muslim nation that might take part, but he did call for the Arab League to be involved.

    The British foreign secretary, William Hague, described the no-fly zone on Tuesday as “a practical possibility,” but the Obama administration has been more skeptical. “Let’s just call a spade a spade,” Robert M. Gates, the American secretary of defense, told Congress last week. “A no-fly zone begins with an attack on Libya.”

    Meanwhile, NATO has been conducting 24-hour surveillance of Libya using Awacs reconnaissance aircraft. On Tuesday, the Royal Air Force posted a video explanation of how its part of the mission, which includes three Awacs planes and some refueling jets based in Cyprus, is being organized.

    The narrator stresses that the mission of these planes is humanitarian and not related to combat.

    12:31 P.M.Reports of Protests in Iran

    Protests appear to have broken out in Tehran on Tuesday, according to a Reuters report that cites an opposition Web site:

    Iran security forces fired tear gas to disperse anti-government protesters in Tehran on Tuesday, the opposition website Kaleme reported.

    “Several teargas [canisters] were fired by the security forces in the Enqelab [revolution] Square to disperse protesters,” said Kaleme, opposition leader Mirhossein Mousavi’s website.

    The whereabouts of Mr. Moussavi and another opposition leader, Mehdi Karroubi, have been the object of speculation since protests last month. The Kaleme site has previously reported that both men and their wives were being held at the Heshmatieh prison in Tehran, though the report did not make clear when they were removed from their homes.

    12:26 P.M.The Hot Spot is Zawiyah

    Though the most powerful images to emerge from Libya so far today are of bombing runs around Ras Lanuf in the east, the ground fighting seems to be more intense and sustained in Zawiyah, the closest city to Tripoli to be controlled by the rebels.

    The Associated Press reported that the town had been recaptured by Qaddafi forces, with at least 20 people injured in the clashes, some of them seriously. Witnesses told Reuters of trapped residents seeking shelter from the onslaught.

    11:01 A.M.Rebel Leaders Organize Themselves

    Leaders of the protest-turned-rebellion in Libya are beginning to develop a more coherent public face.

    An umbrella group that was organized over the weekend, the Interim Transitional National Council, has set up a Web site, and Agence France-Presse reports that it has sent two members to address the European Parliament in Strasbourg, France, later on Tuesday. The news agency did not indicate what they plan to say.

    Mustafa Abdel-Jalil, the leader of the council, laid out terms on Tuesday for Colonel Qaddafi to step down. “If he leaves Libya immediately, during 72 hours, and stops the bombardment, we as Libyans will step back from pursuing him for crimes,” Mr. Abdel-Jalil told Al Jazeera.

    As my colleague Anthony Shadid has reported, Mr. Abdel-Jalil is working to create a functioning civil society in rebel-controlled towns even as the fighting continues to rage.

    The council’s Web site, posted in English and Arabic, says it was officially formed on Saturday.     9:00 A.M.Refugees Struggle to Flee the Conflict in Libya
    Emilio Morenatti/Associated Press A migrant worker from Bangladesh waited — along with many, many others — for a chance to depart Libya on Tuesday at the Ras Ajdir crossing on the Tunisian border.

    Human Rights Watch called on Colonel Qaddafi again on Tuesday to allow humanitarian aid into western Libya and to permit the tens of thousands of poor migrant workers who have fled there hoping to escape the violence and return to their home countries. The number of people managing to cross westward into Tunisia has fallen sharply since last week, when government loyalists took control of the area, the rights group said.

    The continuing struggle between Qaddafi loyalists and the protesters-turned-rebels who are trying to end his regime has created a sprawling refugee crisis both on the borders with Tunisia and Egypt and in the area around the international airport in Tripoli, the capital. As my colleagues David D. Kirkpatrick and Scott Sayare report, the scene outside the airport is one of grim desperation:

    The migrants — many of them illegal immigrants from Ghana and Nigeria who have long constituted an impoverished underclass in Libya — live amid piles of garbage, sleep in makeshift tents of blankets strung from fences and trees, and breathe fumes from a trench of excrement dividing their camp from the parking lot of Tripoli’s airport.

    For dinner on Monday night two men killed a scrawny, half-plucked chicken by dunking it in water boiled on a garbage fire, then hacked it apart with a dull knife and cooked it over an open fire. Some residents of the camp are as young as Essem Ighalo, 9 days old, who arrived on his second day of life and has yet to see a doctor. Many refugees said they had seen deaths from hunger and disease every night.

    8:39 a.m. |Rumors of a Parley

    As airmen loyal to Col. Muammar el-Qaddafi continued pounding rebel positions around Ras Lanuf, an important oil town, a rumor that has bounced around the Arab press for a day hardened into a question in the international media on Tuesday: Did Colonel Qaddafi send a representative to negotiate terms of his departure with rebels?

    The BBC reports on its live blog that Mohsen al-Ghariani, a member of the National Front for the Salvation of Libya, gave some clues on the BBC’s Arabic TV service. Mr. Ghariani told the BBC that though he could neither “deny or confirm that Qaddafi has expressed any intention to leave Libya,” the colonel had “sent an envoy to talk to the opposition.”

    A report of such an overture from the Qaddafi camp had earlier appeared on Monday in the pan-Arab newspaper Al Sharq al Awsat, based in London. According to the paper, “informed sources in the city of Benghazi” confirmed the presence of a negotiator who had come on behalf of Colonel Qaddafi and had presented a seemingly improbable message: “The Libyan leader declared his readiness to abandon power and leave Libya, in return for ensuring the safety of himself and his family.”

    The escalating chatter about the possibility that the colonel might be willing to let go of power drew a strong response from his embattled government on Tuesday.

    “It’s rubbish,” a Libyan official told Agence France-Presse. The official, whom the agency did not name, added that “it’s very annoying to comment” on such matters.

    Libyan state television also broadcast denials that Colonel Qaddafi was seeking to make a deal with the rebels for his departure, according to a post on Al Jazeera’s live blog.

    Rebel leaders have said that any deal with Colonel Qaddafi would have to start with his immediate departure.
