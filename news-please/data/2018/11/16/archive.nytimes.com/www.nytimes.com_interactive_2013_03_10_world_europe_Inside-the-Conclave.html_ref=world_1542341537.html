Inside the Conclave

Santa Marta

ST. PETER’S

BASILICA

Sistine Chapel

Cardinals stay at Santa Marta residence, less than a quarter-mile from the Sistine Chapel.

Cardinals sit at rows of tables along the walls.

SEATING

DOOR

Sealed for every session of the conclave.

CHIMNEY

White smoke and the ringing of bells at St. Peter’s Basilica signal that a new pope has been elected. Black smoke means that the voting continues.

STOVES

Ballots and notes are burned after the voting. The stove on the left holds the smoke canisters that produce the white or black smoke.

Voting happens in the Sistine Chapel. The last few conclaves have been brief, selecting a pope in just a few days. In 2005, Pope Benedict XVI was elected a day after the conclave began.Related: The 115 Men Who Will Choose the Next PopeTop Contenders to Lead the Roman Catholic Church

The Sistine Chapel

The Voting

When there is a winner, the new pope chooses his papal name. Minutes later, he greets the world from St. Peter’s balcony.

The cardinals burn the ballots, usually combining papers from two rounds of voting. Chemicals are added to make the smoke white if a pope has been elected, black if not.

Three cardinals check the ballots. If a candidate has received a two-thirds majority, then a new pope has been elected.

Cardinals write a name under the Latin words “Eligo in Summum Pontificem” (I elect as Supreme Pontiff) and place the ballot in an urn.

Two cardinals read the ballot and record it. A third reads it out loud. Then the ballots are threaded together through the word “Eligo.”

By HANNAH FAIRFIELD, AL GRANBERG, MIKA GRÖNDAHL and SERGIO PEÇANHA
