    Poetry Pairing | ‘To a Friend Whose Work Has Come to Nothing’

    By Katherine Schulten
    January 19, 2012 10:13 am January 19, 2012 10:13 am
    Kenny BraunStephen Harrigan. Go to related article »

    This week’s Poetry Pairing matches William Butler Yeats’s “To a Friend Whose Work Has Come to Nothing” with an article about a Texas writer and artist, “Novelist and His Hero Wonder, Will It Last?”

    Read the poem and the article excerpt below, then let us know what you think — or suggest other Times content that could be matched with the poem instead.

    Poem

    The prolific Irish poet William Butler Yeats is widely recognized as one of the greatest poets of the 20th century. He received the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1923. “To a Friend Whose Work Has Come to Nothing” appeared in Poetry magazine nine years earlier.

    — Poetry Foundation

    To a Friend Whose Work Has Come to Nothing
    By William Butler Yeats

    Now all the truth is out,
    Be secret and take defeat
    From any brazen throat,
    For how can you compete,
    Being honor bred, with one
    Who were it proved he lies
    Were neither shamed in his own
    Nor in his neighbors’ eyes;
    Bred to a harder thing
    Than Triumph, turn away
    And like a laughing string
    Whereon mad fingers play
    Amid a place of stone,
    Be secret and exult,
    Because of all things known
    That is most difficult.

    Times Selection Excerpt

    In his article “Novelist and His Hero Wonder, Will It Last?” Christopher Kelly describes his work by referring to the Yeats poem we’ve published above:

    To research his latest work of historical fiction, the post-World War I novel “Remember Ben Clayton,” the novelist and screenwriter Stephen Harrigan traveled to France twice so he could faithfully describe the town of Saint-Étienne and the battle that took place there in 1918. In Austin, where he lives, he spent hours poring over newspaper microfiche so he could write about what movies were playing at the time and what brands of chewing gum were being advertised.

    For the novel’s central character, however, Mr. Harrigan did not need to dig as deeply into history. In writing about an aging yet still madly ambitious sculptor named Francis (Gil) Gilheaney, the 62-year-old Mr. Harrigan drew upon his own joys and frustrations as a Texas artist who has never been in fashion among the New York literary set, and who wonders if any of his work will still be read after he is gone.

    “I’ve always been taken by the Yeats poem ‘To a Friend Whose Work Has Come to Nothing,’ ” Mr. Harrigan said. “I remember coming across that poem in college and thinking, ‘Oh, man, what a dire fate.’ And yet, every creative person wrestles with it. Has this been worth it? Am I going to be remembered?”

    “Remember Ben Clayton” begins with Gil helplessly trying to protect one of his finest public sculptures from being destroyed by rioters in Oklahoma.

    William Butler Yeats’s “To a Friend Whose Work Has Come to Nothing” is from Poetry (May 1914).

    Erin TES January 19, 2012 · 5:39 pm

    The poem and the article both refer to a key fear ingrained in the human mind: the fear of oblivion. it is in people’s nature to want to mark their existence, to make it seem worthy of their time, to make it matter. Leaving proof of my existence is a key component in the “point” of life, something that will last longer than my lifetime. Yeats’ poem acknowledges this obsession with wanting to extend ones’ life by leaving a trace, but interjects that any resistance to finite mortality is futile, because the most daunting task is to be content with accepting 1. death, 2. nothing is permanent, 3. mortality. The complementary article shows that Mr. Harrigan has not yet come to terms with impending oblivion and is scrambling through art to carve a scar into humanity that will stay open after his death. But a time will come where it is not only he that is dead, but the entire human race is forgotten. Shakespeare, Socrates, and the Beatles will all fade into oblivion, and our time period will not be comprised of individuals but of groups, where relevant differences of opinion today will compose one people, one mindset, in one period of time.

    Nickie TES January 19, 2012 · 10:23 pm

    There is a certain theme recurring throughout the poem and the excerpt of the article. Both the speaker in the poem and Mr.Harrigan in the article have a fear of being forgotten. Yeats’ poem suggests that work does not count for anything if one is not remembered. Their belief is that hard work should mark your name in the world and the only way to show that you have done your part is by being remembered. I don’t agree with this theory. I believe that the only thing that matters is being satisfied with your own work and knowing that you yourself have done something great. Some of the greatest things in the world are unrecognized and I find that that is what makes it special and beautiful. Hard work counts when you know you’ve done your best, recognized or not. We all have fears; that’s what makes us human, but if you know you’ve done your best you won’t be forgotten.

    People have always tried to leave their mark on the world, no matter what it took. Martin Luther King Jr. will never be forgotten for something incredible, his stand against racism. Cleopatra became famous by just being beautiful. And Brutus will never be forgotten for something terrible, betraying Julius Caesar and stabbing him on stage. However some people struggle with the idea that one is not better than the other and just leaving their mark on this world is good enough for them. In “To a Friend Whose Work Has Come to Nothing” and the article “Novelist and His Hero Wonder, Will It Last?”, we can see man’s struggle with feeling the need to leave his mark on this world before he is forgotten. Most men want to leave it doing something they love; something they feel is good, but unfortunately not all men. Just as Mr. Harrigan wonders if any of his work will still be read after he is gone, I wonder if someday I will be able to create something that is able to stand the test of time and not perish with me. But is it worth spending our whole lives trying to create something that will last longer than ourselves? After all, we will not be around to see it stay.

    In the article, Stephen Harrington implies that he believes that the poem is about being forgotten anyhow hard that is. While I understand where this interpretation might come from, I do not believe that that is the main point behind the poem.
    It seems to me that the poem instead refers to defeat and the difficulty of accepting it. The poet seeks to teach his friend to take defeat in stride and to deal with it quietly despite how hard it is. The idea that you have to be able to deal with defeat without making it a big deal seems like something very important to me because the sooner you get past a failure, the sooner you are able to move on to your next victory. When you screw up a defensive move, you need to be able to forget about it immediately or you’re likely to screw up the next one as well.
