    What America Thinks of the 2015 State of the Union Address

    By Anna North
    January 20, 2015 6:12 pm January 20, 2015 6:12 pm

    Updated at 11:11 PM. What’s unusual about this year’s State of the Union address, many have noted, is how much we know already. President Obama has revealed that he’ll call for a federal program to cover community college tuition and tax credits for middle-income Americans paid for by an increase in capital-gains taxes, among other changes. But, some say, that doesn’t mean we know everything about how Tuesday night’s speech will go. Below, watch the address and find out what observers are saying before, during and afterward — Op-Talk will be updating with reactions throughout the evening.

    Find opinion and analysis around the web:

    • At ThinkProgress, Joe Romm focuses on the president’s comments on the climate: “Obama eviscerated those who ignore the warnings from scientists and the Pentagon. He responded directly to the absurd ‘I’m not a scientist’ dodge used by many conservatives (including Iowa Sen. Joni Ernst, who delivered the GOP response).”

    “The GOP response makes clear that Obama will have to go it alone on climate action and clean energy,” Mr. Romm concludes. “His address shows he is prepared to do just that.”

    • At New York Magazine, Annie Lowrey takes up Mr. Obama’s focus on “middle-class economics.” While critics might argue that the middle class hasn’t thrived during his tenure, Ms. Lowrey writes, “the truth is that many of Obama’s proposals would or will help those aggrieved middle-class families.” And, she says, the president on Tuesday night seemed energetic once again: “We saw Obama thunder, trumpet, and staccato-shout his policies, despite the nonexistent odds they have of passage. And the fact that the economy has turned around so much seemed to give him hope that the middle class would start feeling better, even if Washington never helps.”

    • The New York Times Editorial Board writes that Mr. Obama “chose wisely to deliver a simple, dramatic message about economic fairness, about the fact that the well-off — the top earners, the big banks, Silicon Valley — have done just great, while the middle and working classes remain dead in the water.” And, the board adds, “Mr. Obama was speaking not just to the present but to the future, to the 2016 presidential elections and even beyond. By simply raising the plight of the middle class (and, looming behind it, the larger issue of economic inequality), he has firmly inserted issues of economic fairness into the political debate.”

    And predictions and other pre-speech thoughts:

    • “With so much of his agenda known,” asks Russell Berman at The Atlantic, “why bother watching Obama’s address at all?” His answer: “For starters, the tone of his speech will offer the best clue yet as to how he’ll approach the new Republican majority in Congress.” Obama’s proposal on taxes “suggests the president will be combative,” writes Mr. Berman — but he could still seek common ground with Republicans on trade and on the military campaign against ISIS.

    • Jason Johnson writes at The Root that “the State of the Union is more of a wish list than a menu at this point.” However, he adds, “that doesn’t mean there won’t be key moments when pieces of his legacy, vision and personal strength seep through the pomp and circumstance.” And he predicts that the president will gesture at the campaign to come: “Note that the first lady’s guests will be a wonderful coalition of men, women and children who resemble the voting bloc that President Obama nurtured to life in 2008. The president will mention these people as a not-so-subtle road map for potential 2016 contenders.”

    • For a more literal “state of the union,” head over to Vox, where Matthew Yglesias notes that the idea of an annual address came “from a constitutional provision directing the executive branch to provide Congress with an annual update on how things are going.” Mr. Yglesias offers his version of such an update in 33 charts depicting America’s political polarization, employment situation, income distribution and more.

    • At The New York Times, members of the editorial board discuss the issues they hope the president takes up in his speech, from immigration reform to climate change. “I hope the president mentions that 2014 was the hottest year on record,” writes Juliet Lapidos. “He should call out as hypocrites the Republicans who complain that we’re saddling our children with debt — but don’t seem to care that we’re also saddling them with environmental devastation.”
