    Tax policy puzzles

    June 16, 2008 11:44 am June 16, 2008 11:44 am

    The Tax Policy Center comparison of the candidates’ tax plans is here. Anyone who cares at all about the issues should read it.

    There’s nothing surprising in there about the McCain plan: it’s tax cuts and more tax cuts, all hugely tilted toward the top 1 percent of the income distribution.

    The surprise about the Obama plan is how much in the way of tax breaks it contains, and how much that cuts into the revenue available for other things, especially health care. And there are two pieces that really puzzle me.

    First is the income tax exemption for seniors with incomes under 50K. The Tax Policy Center explains:

    Under current law, most senior citizens do not pay income tax because only a portion of Social Security benefits are subject to tax, and only for taxpayers with incomes above a threshold. In addition, senior citizens may claim an additional standard deduction. Nobody age 65 and over whose income comes entirely or almost entirely from Social Security is subject to income tax. The proposal would actually exempt comparatively well off, though not affluent, senior citizens from tax and give them a benefit not generally available to working Americans.

    And I have to say, I really don’t like the idea that certain demographic groups get exempted from taxation. Why, exactly, is this part of the plan?

    The other is Making Work Pay, a tax credit of up to $500 a year for workers; given the formula, the great majority of workers would in fact receive a flat $500. The thing about this proposal is that it’s very, very expensive: $700 billion over 10 years, or close to half typical estimates of the cost of the subsidies needed to achieve universal health care.

    What I don’t get about this proposal is that we already have a program to help lower-income workers, the Earned Income Tax Credit. If raising wages at the bottom is a priority, why not just make the EITC a lot bigger? As it is, the Making Work Pay proposal would give most of its benefits to relatively well off workers — again, at a huge cost, large enough to pose real problems for other initiatives. What’s that about?

    ———————–
    We have an “accounting lock box.” Basically, the SS surplus is “borrowed” by the federal government to finance its deficits, with “special issue” US Treasuries in its place. Approximately, four trillion of our 9 trillion national debt is owed to the entitlement trust funds. The first date in the whole SS/Medicare timeline of note is 2010, when the SSA can no longer loan its surpluses to the Treasury; the federal government will then have to enter the public debt market to finance its deficits.

    Paul —

    In addition, the Obama exemption for over-64 households violates a prime directive in tax-transfer design — avoid notches. According to the TPC, one dollar of AGI, exempt social security and interest over 50K, you are back in regular tax schedule.

    A similar potential notch exists re capital gains tax. The TPC says Obama proposes to raise the capital gains rate only for those who are in the now restored (pre-Bush) 36 and 39.6 percent tax brackets but not for most tax filers in the current (post-Bush) 28 percent and below brackets. Is the increase in the CG rate a function of non-CG income or inclusive of it? Is it a notch or does the CG rate phase up from 15 percent to (TPC’s estimated) 25 percent? Talk about opportunities for tax accountants and lawyers to advise clients on the timing of capital gains and other income, and possibly market inefficiencies.

    The payroll tax credit is clearly designed as a talking point that Obama will have a generally applicable tax cut. Well, can’t explicitly raise tax rates to compensate lest you give Republicans a talking point.

    LRHafe June 16, 2008 · 5:34 pm

    Re: SS payroll tax donut hole —

    There’s a hidden business cost in this tactic vs simply raising the salary cap, and that is the cost of programming to stop & restart withholding at the appropriate times. Currently, virtually all payroll systems are set up to allow the salary cap to be changed with little or no programming/technical intervention. Just plug in the new number and away we go; doesn’t matter if the new number’s a chunk higher.

    However, this plan would require add’l programming to restart withholding at a certain amount & possibly restop it at yet another amount, along with the structures to maintain those amounts so they can be changed the same way as the current cap.

    It’s not rocket science, and it keeps people like me employed, but it is a tangible effort & cost for a policy that seems more interested in its own cleverness than actual usefulness.
