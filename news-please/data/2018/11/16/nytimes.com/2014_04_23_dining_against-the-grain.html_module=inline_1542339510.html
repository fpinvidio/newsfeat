    Against the Grain

    By JEFF GORDINIERAPRIL 22, 2014

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    Zachary Golper gets a strange look in his eyes when he talks about his miche.

    Mr. Golper, who oversees the ovens at Bien Cuit, a bakery in Brooklyn, is part of a starter culture of obsessive, boundary-pushing bread makers in New York City and around the country. Connoisseurs consider his miche, a French-style country loaf, something of a crown jewel. But it certainly doesn’t shine like one; bulbous and heat-bludgeoned, it looks more like something that might have been used as a shield in a Stone Age skirmish.

    Which makes sense. After all, Mr. Golper, like many comrades in the revolutionary salt-flour-water brigade, is engaged in an ancient and ceaseless battle: against the whims of working with fermenting dough whose personality can shift on a daily or even hourly basis; against the high costs of making bread in what he considers the purest manner; against decades of commercialization that have trained the American eye and palate to expect bread that is soft, gummy, pale and tasteless.

    “Most people are trying to make bread as quickly as possible,” he said. “I don’t think there’s any reason to make bread fast. I don’t think it’s healthy.”

    Instead, Mr. Golper, 36, wages a loving blitz upon the miche dough, fermenting it for up to an epic 68 hours and hardening the crust with a bake that goes on for almost double the time (at a slightly lower temperature) than you would find in the average shop. The dough itself contains six different types of flour.

    The result of all that effort and expense? A darkly crusted and deeply flavored loaf, and that wild gleam in Mr. Golper’s eyes. The process brings out “nuances that otherwise would not be obtainable if you don’t take the time,” he said.

    “It will have a more intense chew,” he added. “It will have a creaminess that’s a little more yogurt-like. It will have a trace of sweetness. It will be moist on the tongue.”

    True artisanal baking — with a fierce emphasis on the best ingredients, natural leavening and radical freshness — has been a quiet ebb-and-flow force for decades in this country. But in recent years the practice has enjoyed a marked surge.

    Small independent bakers in New York, California, Oregon, Virginia and North Carolina (and many points in between) are going to great lengths to approach an ideal of bread that is simultaneously cutting-edge and primordial. They’re hunting down heirloom grains, early forms of wheat like emmer and einkorn, and milling their own flour. Some are traveling to Washington State to meet Dr. Stephen Jones, a rogue wheat breeder who runs the Bread Lab, a Wonka-esque wonderland for crusty, airy-crumbed experimentation.

    They’re using unusually wet dough and stretching out fermentation times. They’re trying to conjure up the baker’s version of terroir, creating sourdough starter in the classic manner: simply by letting it sit, welcoming the bacteria in the air so the bread presumably tastes like the place where it was made.

    In New York, the wild frontier encompasses people like Peter Endriss at Runner & Stone, near the Gowanus Canal in Brooklyn, and Simo Kuusisto, a former cook at Aquavit whose Nordic Breads sanctuary in Long Island City, Queens, is devoted to baking almost nothing else but dark, chewy, traditionally Finnish-style whole-grain rye bread that seems like suitable nourishment for an episode of "Game of Thrones.”

    “This is very much how it’s supposed to be — how it used to be,” Mr. Kuusisto said on a recent evening at the bakery, as he buttered hunks of rye and layered them with Cheddar and cucumber slices. “I grew up with this bread. This is a staple in Finland.”

    Going back to “how it’s supposed to be” could serve as a mission statement for these bakers, many of whom have recent family roots in the Old World. It’s not uncommon to hear them swoon over carbohydrate epiphanies in France, Denmark or Italy. They talk about reclaiming a mythic moment in human history when the staff of life had some genuine funk to it.

    The almond croissant at Runner & Stone has a following, but its gnarled, mahogany-hued form doesn’t have much in common with the puffy, golden grease-sponge at the convenience store. The dough in the former has fermented for over 24 hours; it’s made with a local, high-fat butter.

    “We get asked frequently, ‘Is this supposed to look like this?' ” said Mr. Endriss, a Per Se veteran whose palate was influenced when he was young by trips to Germany to visit his father’s family. “They get it when they eat it.”

    What they get, he went on, is “depth of flavor — layers of flavor that keep coming while you’re eating it.”

    Mr. Endriss could easily bake bread that’s conventionally blond and pretty. But, he said, “I want to make something that tastes different than your quote-unquote regular bakery.”

    “For me,” he added, “it’s just a personal integrity thing.”

    Whether because of the strange hours they keep, or their hands-on engagement with one of the cornerstones of human civilization, bakers tend to be an intense, soulful lot. Uri Scheft, who opened Breads Bakery near Union Square in Manhattan in 2013 after achieving legendary status in Israel, is so insistent on offering his goods fresh and warm from the oven that he has brushed away offers to sell wholesale. He also refuses to eschew gluten. “You will not find a single piece of gluten-free anything here,” said Gadi Peleg, his business partner. “That’s a trend. We’re not in the trend business.”

    For Evrim Dogu, a quest (“searching for that timeless aspect,” he said) led him to dive more deeply into an understanding of Abruzzi rye and Turkey Red wheat. “Heirloom grains smell, taste, feel better,” he said. “For the baker, and for the consumer.” Mr. Dogu, whose family hails from Turkey, mills his own wheat, spelt, rye and corn and aims to make “bread that tastes like the grain itself” at Sub Rosa, his bakery in Richmond, Va.

    But it’s not always easy. Artisanal bakers get used to hearing satisfied sighs from customers, but also an array of complaints, like “the crust tears up my mouth” or “the mayonnaise keeps seeping through the big holes.”

    For bakers, the bubble-holes, known as alvéoles, can be a badge of honor, a sign of a well-mixed, well-hydrated, well-proofed dough. “I tell them we put a lot of work into making sure those holes are in there,” said Evan Andres at Columbia City Bakery in Seattle.

    Meticulousness doesn’t come cheap. At Bien Cuit, the Brooklyn bakery, Mr. Golper’s passion for prime ingredients and snail-paced dough-cultivating puts financial pressure on Kate Wheatcroft, who oversees the books, and happens to be his wife. (She started falling in love with him one day when she sampled his raisin-walnut bread and wound up devouring an entire loaf.)

    “Zach’s an artist through and through, and I’m trying to run a business, and those two things don’t necessarily go together,” she said.

    Many bakers who first absorbed the artisanal credo during one of its last big waves, in the 1990s, would agree. “I knew somebody who made einkorn bread, and he had to sell it at $15 a loaf,” said Jonathan Stevens, the head baker at Hungry Ghost Bread, a fixture in Northampton, Mass. “We’re just not into that.”

    Mr. Stevens has grown wary of the deification of hotshot bakers. Making bread “has to be about what people are going to be able to afford and eat,” he said.

    Continue reading the main story

    “It’s not supposed to be a luxury food,” he added.

    Still, this latest American bread boom has created a constellation of baking stars, among them David Bauer at Farm & Sparrow near Asheville, N.C.; Tim Healea at Little T Baker in Portland, Ore.; and Melissa Weller, a veteran of Per Se and Roberta’s who plans to open a downtown bagel shop and bakery with the Major Food Group. These bakers, in turn, cite a pantheon of pioneers and mentors, including Amy Scherber, Paula Oland, Nancy Silverton, Jim Lahey, Maury Rubin, Daniel Leader, Dave Miller, Richard Bourdon and Lionel Poilâne.

    But if the artisanal miche niche has a spiritual godfather right now, it’s Chad Robertson, in San Francisco, whose fragrant, custardy loaves (he and his team make only about 200 to 240 a day) compel hungry followers to line up outside Tartine Bakery every evening. On the phone, he expounded on “the demystification of sourdough,” and in the first of three influential cookbooks, he sought to foster that by presenting a basic, broken-down-into-small-bites bread recipe that stretched on for a David Foster Wallace-ish 38 pages. “My publisher thought I was crazy for making that first recipe so long,” he said. “But I could go on for another 30 pages.”

    So much for demystification. As with faith, love and poetry, it’s the mystery that imbues great bread with power over those who bake it or eat it. In the bakery there are “hundreds of little nuances that change the process throughout the day,” Mr. Robertson said.

    “It’s not the way we want it all the time,” he added. “We push it. We push the fermentation, we push the hydration. It makes for a very precarious baking situation.”

    When it comes together, though, the results reveal why artisanal baking is worth all those risks. Mr. Endriss, of Runner & Stone in Brooklyn, caught a glimpse the first time he ate bread from Tartine: “It’s got the kind of flavor that you don’t notice, except that you know you want more of it.”
