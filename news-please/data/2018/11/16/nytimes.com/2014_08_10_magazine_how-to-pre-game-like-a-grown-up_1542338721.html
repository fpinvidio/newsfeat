How to Pre-Game Like a Grown-Up

By Rosie Schaap

  • Aug. 7, 2014

Editors’ note: We’re resurfacing this 2014 Drink column for Smarter Living so you can enjoy the sophisticated pleasures of a pre-dinner aperitif cocktail all year long.

Aperitifs don’t often come into your life with much ceremony, the way a first martini or manhattan might. They’re not the sort of thing you get wasted on at a college party or default to on a bender. Instead, aperitifs turn up when you’re good and ready for them. When I was a kid and watched my mother drink Lillet or Dubonnet before summer dinners, I never tried to sneak a sip, as I did with beer or wine or fruity drinks like piña coladas. My instincts told me that I’d have to wait until I was older.

My first aperitif — and I doubt I’m alone here — was a Campari and soda, to which my reaction was much the same as my initial response to cilantro: I recoiled, and then wanted more. Now, when I have friends over for dinner in the summer, I usually start things off by muddling a basil leaf with a couple hits of citrus bitters in an old-fashioned glass; adding a handful of ice, about a half-ounce of Campari and two ounces of Lillet (blanc or, even better, rosé); and topping it off with club soda. I plonk a thick slice of grapefruit into the drink to be used as a stirrer (and then eaten, if one likes, and one usually does).

I’ve had the occasional aperitif by myself, but I think of this as an inherently social drink. The unwieldy word, which always seems one syllable too long, comes from the Latin aperire, “to open.” And that’s what it does: An aperitif puts people at ease and signals that an occasion has begun. It opens the proceedings in a way that’s elegant and faintly formal, but also congenial and serene.

And perhaps owing to the unwieldiness of the word, aperitifs are misunderstood and often overlooked. Some of the customers at the bar where I work look at me skeptically when I suggest a wet martini (in which the vermouth can be clearly tasted) rather than a dry one (in which it barely exists). They’d probably be even warier if I recommended vermouth — just vermouth — on the rocks with a lemon twist and a splash of soda. If you haven’t tried good vermouth as a thing in itself, you’re in for a treat. And no aperitif could be easier when you’ve got the grill fired up and company you want to keep.

But it’s also worthwhile to take a few extra uncomplicated steps to make pre-dinner aperitif cocktails. I’ve asked two terrific New York bartenders to share recipes for their favorites. They come together very quickly and are everything an aperitif ought to be: bright and refreshing, and capable of stimulating the appetite and lively conversation. I’ll always associate aperitifs with summertime, but I want to drink these year-round.
