    Cooking With Dexter: Not in My Backyard

    By PETE WELLSOCT. 20, 2010

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    The backyard-chicken movement may be a great thing for poultry fanatics, but for parents it’s a nightmare. A few years ago, it was easy enough to say, “Oh, honey, we’d love to have a pet chicken, but unfortunately you aren’t allowed to keep hens in Hamden, Conn. [or Evanston, Ill., or Madison, Wis., or Salem, Ore.].” No more. Egg-gathering activists in those communities successfully lobbied for legalization. Naturally you can try lying to your children, but if they are old enough and resourceful enough they will eventually navigate their way to the backyardchickens.com forum on local laws and ordinances, and the jig will be up.

    I don’t even have that excuse to fall back on. In Brooklyn, hens are perfectly legal, and while it’s a stretch to say there’s a coop in every backyard, there is one on a front lawn not far from where we live. I used to walk my boys, Dexter and Elliot, to see it, in the days when this seemed like a harmless diversion. I’ve stopped doing that now that Dexter wants to turn our house into a farm. I’ve also hidden my copy of “My Empire of Dirt,” in which Manny Howard writes about his attempts to feed himself almost entirely with plants and animals raised on his property in Brooklyn. Now that we’ve moved into our new house, with a hundredth of an acre of prime Kings County topsoil out back, urban farming is no longer an amusing oddity. It’s an all-too-real danger, one that must be resisted every single day.

    I admit that I instigated Dexter’s farming mania. In the spring, when we were renting a duplex with a huge yard, I encouraged him to choose eggplant, tomato, kale, lettuce and pepper seedlings, which we planted in big pots. By the Fourth of July, we were enjoying our first Bed-Stuy-grown salad. But our lettuce withered under the sun in the hottest summer on record, and our tomatoes were wantonly picked and scattered to the winds by a neighbor’s 2-year-old, who wiped out our harvest faster than a plague of locusts.

    My farming ambitions died with the tomato plants. But not Dexter’s. Right about then, my wife, Susan, took him to visit a friend’s small farm on Long Island, where he fell in love with a chicken named Sergeant Pepper. Our friend helpfully suggested a configuration of coops that would allow us to keep four birds in our backyard. But she also warned us that chickens will eat anything in sight. Give them enough room to scratch around, and our 20-by-30-foot plot of arable land would be a dust bowl. When I told Dexter that a chicken would lay waste to our peppers and eggplants, he immediately had visions of a verdant rooftop garden. I had visions of hauling hundreds of pounds of compost up three flights of stairs. When I told him there would be no rooftop garden, he had his counteroffer ready: rooftop beehives.

    Once again, I couldn’t simply say, “We can’t do that in Brooklyn.” In March, the Department of Health and Mental Hygiene rescinded a longstanding ban that put honeybees in the category of animals too dangerous to be kept as pets, along with elephants, hyenas and cobras. But I could say — and have said — that we can’t do that in our house. It’s even true. Susan is profoundly allergic to bee stings, just as Dexter has life-threatening reactions to nuts.

    You might think “Mommy could die” would be a fairly effective argument-ender, but Dexter still asks me sometimes how we could find a queen for our colony. Long ago I learned that when he has a fixed idea in his head, stalling maneuvers are the best defense. My favorite tactic in the face of his relentless pro-farming agitation is teaching him to make sausage. Because, yes, Dexter wants to raise hogs in our backyard too. But before we can have pigs, I tell him, we need to learn how to handle the fresh raw meat.

    He doesn’t blink at this. I’ve been talking to him about the ultimate fate of food animals for years, and now that he’s 6, he’s used to the concept. In fact, the prospect of hog-butchering at home is just the kind of daunting project he loves. In our basement is an old darkroom, which could be converted to an abattoir without much trouble. We could cut up the pig there and grind the scraps into glorious sausages. But first we have to practice.

    Of course, there is a hidden agenda. I’ve been making sausages at home for a decade and always enjoy myself. But lately I’ve been watching with rapt interest as some Brooklyn butchers apply real creativity to their sausage craft. In the hands of Greene Grape Provisions or Marlow & Daughters or especially the Meat Hook, stuffing a hog intestine looks like sport. The meat manipulators at the Meat Hook have shaken all my preconceptions with their banh mi sausages (stuffed with cilantro as well as jalapeños and carrots). Another tastes exactly like a bacon cheeseburger, a magic trick in a tube (and, like a good magic trick, it incorporates some trade secrets). They’ve come up with a fresh L.A.-style chorizo the likes of which New York has never seen, vivid orange with guajillo chilies, and they make something they call the ’90s Man, the first chicken-apple sausage flavorful enough to keep up with venerable, no-nonsense links like cotechino.

    Tom Mylan, an owner of the Meat Hook, gave me two recipes to start. When Dexter and I master them, I’ll ask for another. If all goes well, we’ll still be practicing when he goes off to college, where he can petition the dean for a campus farm while my wife and I drink vinho verde on a beach in Portugal. And if that fails, I’ve got one card left to play. That health code that used to prohibit honeybees? It still bans pigs.

    1.If you are making sausage links, thoroughly rinse the casing of salt and soak it in a large pot of cold water overnight. Thoroughly rinse the casing again and place it in fresh, cold water until ready to use.

    2.Attach the knife and grinding-plate attachments to a stand mixer. Run the pork and beef through the grinder into a large bowl, followed by a slice of bread to push out any bits of meat stuck inside the works. Discard any ground bread. Add the remaining ingredients and mix together with your hands. Knead the meat until it seizes up and holds together, about 2 minutes. At this point you can shape the sausage mixture into patties.

    3.If you are making links, replace the grinding plate with a stuffer attachment with a ¾-inch opening. Select a length of casing and run water from the tap through it in order to clear out any excess salt and spot any holes, which you should trim out. Cut a length of casing at least 3 to 4 feet long; tie it off. Run the casing between your fingers to remove excess water. Stretch the open end of the casing around the attachment’s nozzle and gradually slide the rest of the casing onto the nozzle, an inch or 2 at a time. Leave about 3 inches at the knotted end dangling free. Put the sausage in the hopper of the grinder and turn it on (Speed 4 tends to work best). Hold the dangling casing with one hand, and with the other, feed the sausage mixture into the hopper with a wooden pestle at an even pace. When 2 inches of casing are empty at both the knotted and free ends, stop stuffing and slowly pull on the casing to free it from the stuffer nozzle. Run your fingers over the casing to distribute the sausage evenly. Pinch the middle of the sausage and gently twist it to form two long links. Keep pinching and twisting to form links 4 or 5 inches long. Repeat this process until all of the sausage meat has been used, pushing out the last bits of meat with another slice or two of bread.

    4.Whether you’ve made patties or links, refrigerating the sausage overnight, wrapped in wax paper, will help it hold together as it cooks. To cook, snip off a single length containing as many individual sausages as you like. Don’t separate the links until they have been cooked (in an oiled skillet, on the grill or under the broiler). Any uncooked remainders will freeze well. Makes 14 sausages. Adapted from Tom Mylan and Sara Bigelow at the Meat Hook in Brooklyn.

    Note: Sausage casing can be bought from most sausage-making butchers or ordered online at Amazon.com.
