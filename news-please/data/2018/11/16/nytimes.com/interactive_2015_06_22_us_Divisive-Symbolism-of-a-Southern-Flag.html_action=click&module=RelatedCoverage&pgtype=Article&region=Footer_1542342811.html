    Divisive Symbolism of a Southern Flag

    By THE NEW YORK TIMES JUNE 22, 2015

    1. Since the Charleston church shooting, pressure has mounted on South Carolina to stop displaying the Confederate battle flag on the grounds of its State Capitol in Columbia, S.C. On Monday, Gov. Nikki R. Haley and Senator Lindsey Graham of South Carolina called for the flag's removal.

      Supporters of the flag have said they view the shooting and the flag as unrelated. And a Winthrop University poll last fall found that 62 percent of South Carolina residents had positive or neutral feelings toward the flag. But Cornell William Brooks, the national president of the N.A.A.C.P., has called it an emblem of hate. “That symbol must be removed from our state capital,” he said last week.


    2. Confederate Battle Flag

      First used in northern Virginia battlefields in 1861, this flag was never officially adopted as the flag of the Confederate States.

      Beginning in 1962, South Carolina flew the flag over the dome of the State House in observance of the Civil War Centennial. But it was moved to a memorial nearby in 2000 after the N.A.A.C.P. announced a boycott of the state and the presidential candidates weighed in, with the Democratic contenders calling for the flag's removal and Republicans declining to take a position.

    3. Confederate National Flag

      The official flag of the Confederacy evolved through the Civil War. The earliest version came to be known as the Stars and Bars. Later flags incorporated the battle flag as a canton, in the upper left corner.

    4. South Carolina State Flag

      The crescent moon of the current flag of South Carolina was first used for South Carolina Revolutionary War troops beginning in 1775. A flag known as the Sovereignty flag, a blue cross with stars on a red field, incorporating the crescent and palmetto tree and designed in 1860, was never officially recognized.

    5. Enduring Confederate Symbolism

      Mississippi is the only state with a flag, shown here, that incorporates the Confederate battle flag. A 2001 referendum to redesign the flag failed.

      Other Southern states' flags retain some characteristics of the battle flag. Alabama and Florida flags are white and display a wide red X. The flags of Tennessee and Arkansas contain stars on a red field. The design of the North Carolina flag is based on the earliest Confederate national flag.

      Georgia's flag incorporated the Confederate battle flag in a design flown between 1956 until 2001.

      Motorists in Alabama, Georgia, Louisiana, Maryland, Mississippi, North Carolina, South Carolina, Tennessee and Virginia can request a license plate with a Sons of Confederate Veterans logo, based on the Confederate battle flag. Last week the Supreme Court ruled that Texas could refuse to issue such plates.



    Correction: June 23, 2015

    An earlier version of this article incorrectly included West Virginia in a list of states in which vehicle owners can request vanity license plates carrying the Sons of Confederate Veterans logo.
