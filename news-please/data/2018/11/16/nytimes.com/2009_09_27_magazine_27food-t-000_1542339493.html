    Raising Steaks

    By CHRISTINE MUHLKESEPT. 24, 2009

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    In 1950, the editor Judith Jones rescued Anne Frank’s diary from the reject pile. Ten years later, she championed a cookbook no other publisher would touch and named it “Mastering the Art of French Cooking,” the first of many seminal culinary titles that she shepherded. At 85, she still works as a senior editor and vice president of Knopf. But every girl needs a hobby. So three years ago, she started raising cattle.

    Now when her writers visit her summer home in northern Vermont to test recipes, they can cook with Bryn Teg farm’s grass-fed beef.

    “The only time I had a little twinge was the other day and the cows licked our hands,” Jones said, recounting when she was hosting the food writer Joan Nathan, who was there to work on her next Jewish cookbook. “You get that rough texture of the tongue. And the next day, we were peeling it off!” she said with a shuddering laugh. The brisket proved less troubling.

    On a rainy late-August evening, Jones was improvising an elegant little supper for two (Bryn Teg burgers, buttered potatoes, salad and cheese course — always a cheese course) in her lived-in kitchen. Dressed in slim white jeans and a tidy striped shirt, she glugged some red wine into a skillet of hamburger-juice-soaked shallots, then tucked a stuffed beef heart into the oven to braise indefinitely. Even at the stove, her clipped-and-drawled speech evokes a diminished East Coast aristocracy, which clings to certain words in order to underscore their hierarchy in the sentence. “I just don’t understand people who don’t love to cook, do you?” she asked as she zipped around the kitchen, lithe and spry from yoga and twice-daily pond swims.

    She not only loves to cook; Jones helped America discover it loves cooking, too. The kitchen windowsill is lined with a sliver of her life’s work: cookbooks from the likes of James Beard and Jacques Pépin, as well as the three titles she wrote with her husband, Evan Jones, who died in 1996. There are many authoritative works among them: Marcella Hazan’s Italian fundamentals, Madhur Jaffrey’s Indian food, Middle Eastern fare from Claudia Roden, Edna Lewis’s seasonal Southern menus.

    Candles lighted, napkins removed from family rings, Jones paused above her plate. “Now. Shall we see how our cows taste?”

    The flavorful, juicy patties on our china were courtesy of a Black Angus that had grazed on the picturesque hills surrounding her modest home. “It has that character of just beefiness!” she said. “I think we’ve forgotten that. I really had come to never buy hamburger meat because I didn’t think it was that good. Every cut has been good. Some have been a little chewy,” she admitted, noting the characteristic associated with grass-fed beef. “The chewiness goes with the flavor, you know. I like those extra chews!”

    Jones’s Yankee pragmatism and culinary creativity are being drawn upon now that her country and city freezers are filled with random cuts from the 300 pounds of beef she split with her partners, her cousin John Reynolds and her stepdaughter Bronwyn Dunne last year. Talking about what to make with kidneys and stew meat and that giant heart makes her eyes shine.

    Thrift and enjoyment in the kitchen are also the guiding principles behind the recipes in her second book in two years, “The Pleasures of Cooking for One.” Almost every dish begets several sensual meals, none of which have the cat-lady stigma of single suppers thanks to Jones’s wisdom and gracious cheer. (“This is apt to be a messy-looking pancake,” she writes in one recipe introduction. “But who cares? It’s just for you, and it’s delicious.”) Attention Julie Powell: leftover boeuf bourguignon makes an excellent beef-and-kidney pie on Day 2, not to mention a meaty pasta sauce when thinned with three or four San Marzano tomatoes.

    The six pregnant cows that Jones bought for $900 apiece three years ago have multiplied into a herd of 20, which Reynolds moves between fenced pastures, feeding them hay during winters that drag into May. Last year he added Belted Galloways, a heritage breed chosen for its ability to winter better as well as fatten solely on grass.

    Reynolds, who lives on a farm a Subaru-wagon’s throw away, has watched the farms around him disappear over the decades. “The profit center has moved from the farm to the processor,” he said. Reynolds had just given up on his heifer business before Jones proposed her scheme. “We thought originally, We’ll just sell the heifer calves. And thennnn,” he turned to Jones and laughed, “you kind of wanted to go for the beef.”

    Jones sees her family’s project as more of a hobby than a business. They could make more by selling the meat themselves, but the Bryn Teg partners prefer to let someone else do the butchering and delivery.

    Sample business discussion:

    Jones: I think we might break even in another year or two, don’t you?

    Reynolds: . . . .

    Jones: Well, we’ll see.

    And so Dunne, who lives in Burlington, Vt., brought in Michael Gourlay, a grass-fed-beef specialist. He packages, sells and distributes their meat regionally under the Hardwick Beef label. (Gourlay noted that they have experienced a 50 percent increase in the availability of grass-fed beef in the last year alone.) Dunne sees potential in creating an online source for their beef and other Vermont products. “Supporting a local economy is beneficial to everybody,” she said. It also resonates beyond the quaint trendiness of the farmers’ market: “It may be the future of food security in the world.”

    Watching the cows work their way across the field creates its own sense of security. For Jones, it’s knowing that her family is restoring the land and providing what she believes are happy lives for cows. “I don’t mean to sound sentimental,” she said, “but you can see: This is a contented herd.”

    They’ve also provided a return to the true beef flavor that she hadn’t experienced since she was in Paris in her 20s. The first time she tasted Bryn Teg’s meat last fall, Jones said, she wasn’t near tears out of guilt. “If anything I felt a sort of gratitude,” she recalled. “I felt, this is what meat should taste like.”
