    In Impassioned Debate on South Carolina Confederate Flag, Three Voices Emerge

    By RICHARD PÉREZ-PEÑA and ALAN BLINDER JULY 9, 2015

    1. After hours of emotional debate that ended early Thursday morning, members of the South Carolina House voted to remove the Confederate battle flag from the State House. Gov. Nikki R. Haley, who called for the measure, signed it into law on Thursday.

      Before the vote, lawmakers delivered speeches for and against taking down the flag. These three distinctive voices emerged.

    2. Michael A. Pitts
      Republican of Laurens County

      At the center of the tumult for much of the evening stood a Republican representative from the most conservative part of South Carolina, an avid advocate of gun rights and small government, with a particularly white, Southern take on history.

      That man, Michael A. Pitts, seemed to do everything he could to derail, scuttle or otherwise alter the plan to force down the battle flag. He introduced dozens of amendments, all of them unsuccessful, intended to slow the bill’s race to passage — including one to erect a monument to a Cherokee chief who became a Confederate general, and one dictating that yellow jessamine, the state flower, be planted where the flagpole stands.

      “I grew up holding that flag in reverence because of the stories of my ancestors carrying that flag into battle and serving under it,” he said.

      Mr. Pitts, 59, a retired police officer with a gray mustache and a bald pate, lives where he was born, in Laurens, a town of about 9,200 people in the Upstate region. “Some call it the War Between the States, some call it a Civil War,” he said during the debate. “Growing up, my family, it was called the War of Northern Aggression. It’s where the Yankees attacked the South.”

      He was one of a few lawmakers who drew a parallel between the Confederates' cause and what conservatives like him fight for today.

      “One of the main reasons for that great tragedy of four years was states’ rights — the ability for the states to govern themselves,” he said. “There’s a huge argument which still exists today over which could control its destiny, the sovereignty of the states or the rule of the federal government.”

      Mr. Pitts voted against the flag removal proposal on a preliminary vote and abstained during the final roll call. Nonetheless, on Thursday morning, he insisted that he supported removing the battle flag, but that he had wanted the flag’s critics to broker an agreement to honor South Carolina’s history. Instead, he found “an absolute unwillingness to compromise.”

      “I’m willing to remove that flag from the pole at some point if it causes a twinge in the heart of Lonnie Hosey or Bill Clyburn, my friends,” he said during the House debate, referring to two black Democratic colleagues, but “I’ll ask for something in return.”

      He pointed to ways the state had changed since 2000, when a legislative compromise stripped the State House’s dome of the battle flag, which was moved to a spot near the Confederate Soldier Monument.

      “We have an Indian-American governor,” he said on Thursday. “We have a black U.S. senator.”

      “The rest of the nation,” he added, “instead of always looking down their nose at the state and trying to portray us to be a bunch of rednecks, could take a look and see what this state actually and really is.”

      “The battle is over,” he said. “My side lost. We’ll come back together. The state will move on.”

      But, he acknowledged, there was perhaps a reason that the 30-foot pole that has for years held the battle flag over the State House grounds did not have an easy mechanism to lower the Confederate battle standard.

      “They thought it was never going to come down,” he said.

    3. Jenny Anderson Horne
      Republican of Dorchester County

      Jenny Anderson Horne sat quietly for hours while others had their say about the Confederate battle flag, but when she finally took the podium Wednesday night and spoke against the flag for just four minutes, she gave the debate’s most powerful display of raw emotion.

      Fearing that the votes to remove the flag were slipping away as the debate droned on, Ms. Horne marched to the well of the House, not quite sure what she would say. And in a political culture that prides itself on a certain reserve, she gestured emphatically, tears sprang to her eyes and her voice trembled, choked and then rose to a shout as she spoke about the flag in terms that would once have been unthinkable for a conservative white South Carolinian.

      “I cannot believe that we do not have the heart, in this body, to do something meaningful such as take a symbol of hate off these grounds on Friday,” said Ms. Horne, a Republican from Dorchester County, near Charleston. “For the widow of Senator Pinckney and his two young daughters, that would be adding insult to injury. And I will not be a part of it.”

      Her impassioned speech, lawmakers from both parties said on Thursday, helped spur representatives toward a deal that broke a legislative logjam and led to the early-morning vote. It became the video clip posted online by dozens of news organizations and thousands of social media users.

      “I am sorry; I have heard enough about heritage,” she said. “I am a descendant of Jefferson Davis, O.K.? But that does not matter.”

      Ms. Horne, 42, said in an interview on Thursday morning that as debate wore on, she feared that the bill’s supporters were losing momentum.

      “I was just frustrated because I felt like some people were losing sight of the victims of the Charleston shooting,” Ms. Horne, a lawyer, said in her office near the State House.

      She has deep roots in the House, which she joined as a member in 2009. She was a page in the chamber while she was an undergraduate at the University of South Carolina, and met the Rev. Clementa C. Pinckney, the future state senator who was Emanuel African Methodist Episcopal Church’s senior pastor when he was killed in the massacre there last month.

      Those longstanding ties, she said, likely factored into the emotions that coursed through her speech.

      Some of her Republican colleagues were less than impressed. Mr. Pitts described her in an interview on Thursday as “self-serving and a little unbalanced.”

      But Democrats said the speech had galvanized wavering members.

      “When you have a person like that who is intelligent and well thought of and doesn’t speak much, when they speak, people listen,” said Representative Cezar E. McKnight, who sat next to Ms. Horne at Mr. Pinckney’s funeral last month.

      As her office phone rang almost constantly and her Facebook page filled with a dizzying array of praises and critiques, Ms. Horne said she was stunned by the attention. “I never really imagined that this would be far-reaching,” she said. “I was just trying to remove the flag from the State House grounds. That’s all I was trying to do.”

    4. Wendell G. Gilliard
      Democrat of Charleston

      As he spoke from the well of the House chamber, standing not far from a portrait of a Confederate general, Robert E. Lee, Representative Wendell G. Gilliard, a Democrat and African-American from Charleston, read out the names of the nine people killed in the church massacre, noting that afterward, the victims’ relatives “shocked the world” with their public words of forgiveness for the man suspected in the shooting, Dylann Roof — an act, Mr. Gilliard said, that averted “a calamity on top of a tragedy.”

      “And now we see what needs to be done,” he said. “The right thing to do is what we call the healing thing. The gentle laying down of the past and a hopeful road to the future.”

      Picking up on the theme used by many Republicans here — that their state had been a model of racial peace, even unity — Mr. Gilliard said, “We are showing the world, as one, we can achieve anything.”

      He has sometimes adopted causes his colleagues either have not noticed or do not want to touch. As a Charleston City Council member, Mr. Gilliard, 61, first drew widespread attention for trying to close a pornography shop, and to limit the amount of skin shown by young women sunbathing in public areas.

      He was calling for a ban on texting while driving, and for video cameras in high-crime areas of Charleston well before those concepts became reality. When he proposed last year that police officers be required to wear body cameras, the idea did not gain much support, but this year it swiftly became law, after a video emerged of an officer in North Charleston shooting an unarmed man in the back as he ran away. That bill put Mr. Gilliard in opposition to Mr. Pitts, who questioned whether the state was moving too quickly in response to the shooting. But Mr. Pitts voted in favor of the bill.

      Mr. Gilliard, a former chemical plant operator who was president of a union local for 15 years, knows how to be confrontational, but on Wednesday, he reminded his fellow lawmakers what had prompted the latest calls to remove the flag. He gave a somber tribute to the people killed at the church.

      “We cannot ever add to what was their sacrifice and their example,” he said. “They gave their last level of devotion to their church, and in doing so, stand as an example of Christian love.”

      “This has brought forth a new understanding and a new way of peace,” he added. “The scales have been shed from our eyes.”
