    France's What-If Campaign

    By IAN BREMMERFEB. 16, 2012

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    French voters head to the polls in April for the first round of a presidential election, and unlike the looming vote in Russia the outcome in France remains in doubt. Fear is rising in some quarters that the Socialist Party candidate, François Hollande, will unseat President Nicolas Sarkozy, that his government will drive France hard to the left, and that he will fatally undermine the work that Sarkozy has done with Chancellor Angela Merkel of Germany to restore confidence in the euro zone.

    Not so fast. First, Sarkozy will make this a more interesting race before he’s finished. Second, a win for Hollande wouldn’t mean dramatic change for France’s economic policy orientation or for crisis management in the euro zone.

    It’s hardly surprising that the challenger leads handsomely in the polls. France’s unemployment levels are stuck at about 10 percent — their worst level in more than a decade. In January, Standard & Poor’s downgraded France’s credit rating. About 65 percent of respondents have a dim view of Sarkozy’s performance as president, the highest for any incumbent on the eve of elections since Charles de Gaulle ushered in France’s Fifth Republic in 1958.

    But this is not Sarkozy’s first fight, and we can expect him to wage a relentless re-election campaign. Incumbency will boost his chances; when asked who can most effectively defend France’s interests abroad, voters still give Sarkozy the edge. The polls will tighten in the next two months, and the president’s battle for his political life will drive the media coverage. About one in five voters is undecided, and half of those who have a preference say they might still change their minds.

    But what happens if, as expected, Hollande wins? Market sentiment has been negative, particularly since he kicked off his campaign in January with a virtual declaration of war on bankers. Here, too, the conventional wisdom deserves scrutiny.

    The toughness of his tone on the financial sector has much to do with the Socialist Party’s recent history of failure. In 2002, the Socialist standard-bearer, Prime Minister Lionel Jospin, suffered the humiliation of first-round elimination when smaller parties on the left essentially abandoned him, and the charismatic icon of the far right, Jean-Marie Le Pen, and his National Front advanced. Jacques Chirac then won big.

    Ten years later, Hollande needed to open his campaign with an appeal to what the former U.S. presidential candidate Howard Dean might call the socialist wing of the Socialist Party. But having rallied the left, Hollande will travel at the end of this month to London, home to some 200,000 French citizens, many of whom work in the financial industry. Hollande has assured this audience ahead of time that, though regulatory changes must be made, his banking proposals won’t go “overboard.”

    Campaigning is one thing, but there’s no need to wait until the votes are counted to remember that governing is quite another.

    It’s no secret that, whatever adjectives he uses to describe his approach, austerity is part of Hollande’s plan. He pledges to reduce France’s budget deficit to 3 percent of G.D.P. by 2013 and to eliminate it by 2017. Yes, he means to roll back the retirement age from 62 to 60, but only for those who have worked at least 41 years, an adjustment that is consistent with the “long careers” early retirement envisioned during France’s post-crisis pension reform two years ago.

    A Hollande victory probably means tax hikes for high-income earners and capital gains generally — and new regulations for bankers. But he cannot and will not, as some fear, reinvent the role of government in France’s economy.

    Finally, some say a Hollande victory will undermine the battle to bolster confidence in the euro zone. He heightened these concerns late last year by promising to reopen the European fiscal pact that Sarkozy has endorsed before the French Parliament can ratify it. Hollande insists the agreement imposes too much austerity and does too little to spur growth.

    Yet, he’ll approach this agreement carefully, if at all, because he knows well that renunciation of the agreement could generate market turmoil throughout Europe and subject the French economy to considerable market pressure. Here again, there is simply less to the campaign rhetoric than meets the eye.

    That Merkel is actively supporting Sarkozy’s re-election bid — an extraordinary step — won’t help Europe’s most important bilateral relationship if Hollande wins.

    But Hollande cannot and will not lead France to play a fundamentally different role in the euro zone. A former protégé of Jacques Delors, three-term president of the European Commission and arguably the most pro-European political official in France, Hollande will not become the man who fatally undermines the euro, the most tangible symbol of Europe’s integration.

    In fact, Franco-German cooperation has become a key element of Hollande’s campaign. He will propose a new Franco-German treaty to increase integration between the two countries. He’ll have to work with Merkel to accomplish that.

    “Merkollande” won’t roll off the tongue like “Merkozy,” but it would hardly signal the end of the euro zone.

    Ian Bremmer is president of Eurasia Group, the political risk consultancy, and author of the forthcoming “Every Nation For Itself: Winners and Losers in a G-Zero World.”
