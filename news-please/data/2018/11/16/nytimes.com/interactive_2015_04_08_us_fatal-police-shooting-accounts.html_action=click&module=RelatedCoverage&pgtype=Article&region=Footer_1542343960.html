    Fatal Police Shootings: Accounts Since Ferguson

    By RICHARD PÉREZ-PEÑA APRIL 8, 2015

    Nobody knows just how many people are killed by the police nationwide. The F.B.I. keeps an official tally of law enforcement officers killed in the line of duty — an average of 69 per year since 1980 — but there is no comparable accounting of lives taken by officers.

    Yet a detailed review of news reports, official statements and websites that try to keep track of such things indicates that in the year since an officer fatally shot Michael Brown in Ferguson, Mo., on Aug. 9, 2014, 1,000 or more people died at the hands of law enforcement officers acting in the line of duty.

    That review reveals some expected patterns, like the disproportionate presence of African-Americans, people with mental illnesses, and young men among the dead.

    More surprising is how often the people killed seemed to be committing “suicide by cop,” either indicating in advance that they wanted to die, asking officers to shoot them, or simply preferring death to prison. In dozens of cases, a person was killed after brandishing some kind of toy gun, even pointing it at officers, a sure invitation to gunfire.

    Dozens of people, at least, died after being shocked with electric stun guns, some from compression or choking, and some from causes that remain mysterious. But most, by far, were shot.

    Another clear conclusion is that a great majority of the people killed were reported to be armed, and very often were attacking officers. Sometimes, there is only the officers’ word for that, but in most cases, their accounts are supported by witnesses or physical evidence.

    At times, the killing of an armed person leads to protests and is called unjustified, or the killing of an unarmed person is considered justified.

    But the presence or absence of a weapon is often, like video, a definitive factor in determining how the police, prosecutors and the public react. It is the fatal shootings, like those of Mr. Brown, Tamir Rice and Samuel DuBose, that prompt the greatest outrage.

    Here are some recent cases that drew the most attention or raised the most serious questions about the officers’ conduct.

    1. Christian Taylor
      Aug. 7, 2015 — Arlington, Tex.

      A white rookie police officer shot and killed an unarmed black college football player who the police said had broken into a car dealership in Arlington, a Dallas suburb. The police said the man, Christian Taylor, 19 — an Arlington native who was a football player and student at Angelo State University in San Angelo, a city in western Texas — was shot at around 1 a.m. on Aug. 7 as he was confronted by officers who had been sent to the dealership after reports of a burglary. The officer, Brad Miller, 49, was fired Aug. 11 for “inappropriate judgment” in his handling of the situation, officials said. The Arlington police chief, Will D. Johnson, said that Officer Miller's mistakes included entering the building without his more experienced partner. There were other officers at the scene, Chief Johnson told reporters, including Officer Miller’s partner, who tried to use a stun gun to subdue Mr. Taylor.

    2. Darius Graves
      Aug. 4, 2015 — Rantoul, Ill.

      A suspect in an armed robbery was fatally shot after a standoff at a motel, during which, officials said, the man told the police he had guns and threatened to shoot. Two days earlier, a man had held up a restaurant at gunpoint. From images taken by surveillance cameras, investigators tentatively identified him as Darius Graves, 31, and received a tip that he was at a Days Inn motel. Police officers from Rantoul and Champaign, Ill., spent nearly seven hours trying to get him to leave the room, but said he refused and repeatedly made threats. Finally, officers fired tear gas into the room, and Mr. Graves, who was black, crawled out, holding an object that officials said they thought was a weapon. Sgt. Colby Oleson, a Champaign SWAT team leader, fired a single shot from about 30 feet away, killing Mr. Graves. Officials said that Sergeant Oleson, who is white, had followed the plan laid out by commanders and that the shooting was justified. The object in Mr. Graves’s hand turned out to be a metal sink trap. There were no weapons on him or in the room, and officers did not find the money and valuables taken in the robbery.

    3. Allan F. White III
      July 28, 2015 — Cleveland, Tenn.

      A Bradley County sheriff's deputy fatally shot a man who she said had attacked her outside her home. The deputy, Tiffany Oakley, was working the night shift and had gone home for her break. Officials said Allan F. White III, a man she did not know, stepped out of the shadows and refused her command to stop. A fight ensued, and Deputy Oakley shot him. She was treated for what were described as minor injuries.

    4. Jean Paul Falgout
      July 27, 2015 — Houma, La.

      Law enforcement officers fatally shot a man who they said had pointed a realistic-looking pellet gun at them. The man, Jean Paul Falgout, 45, who was wanted on nonviolent criminal charges, led officers on a car chase until he was cornered, and was shot after he got out of his car. Eight officers from three police agencies fired at him; it is not clear how many of the shots hit him. Mr. Falgout was white. The races of the officers have not been made public.

    5. Derek Wolfsteller
      July 23, 2015 — Plymouth, Minn.

      A distraught man whose family had tried unsuccessfully to have him hospitalized for mental health problems caused a disturbance in a restaurant, where an officer fatally shot him. The day before, the police had been called to the home of the man's grandparents because of an unspecified crisis he was experiencing. On the day he died, the man, Derek Wolfsteller, 31, went to an Arby’s, where, his father said, he called the police and asked to be taken to the hospital. An Arby’s employee called 911 and reported a man causing a disturbance. The authorities said that on her way to the restaurant, a Plymouth police officer, Amy Therkelsen, was told that the man might be armed. When she arrived, customers were trying to subdue Mr. Wolfsteller, and the officer did, as well. Officials said he tried to take her gun, and she shot him. Mr. Wolfsteller was white, as is Officer Therkelsen.

    6. Samuel DuBose
      July 19, 2015 — Cincinnati

      A campus police officer fatally shot a motorist during a traffic stop for a missing license plate, resulting in what prosecutors said was the first murder charge ever filed in Hamilton County against an officer for actions taken on duty. The officer, Ray Tensing, of the University of Cincinnati police force, was patrolling away from the campus when he stopped the motorist, Samuel DuBose, 43. Video from a body camera worn by Officer Tensing showed that he tried to get Mr. DuBose out of the car and reached in; that Mr. DuBose began to drive away; and that the officer fired one shot, hitting Mr. DuBose in the head. The county prosecutor, Joseph T. Deters, called the shooting "asinine" and said Mr. DuBose had not acted aggressively or posed a threat to Officer Tensing. The officer contended that he was dragged by the car. The murder charge is punishable by life in prison.

    7. Darrius Stewart
      July 17, 2015 — Memphis

      After a routine traffic stop, a Memphis police officer tried to arrest a man on outstanding warrants, fought with the man, and fatally shot him. The officer, Connor Schilling, pulled over a car with a broken headlight. He gave the driver a ticket and asked the passenger for identification; the passenger gave his name, Darrius Stewart, but said he did not have identification with him. The driver of the car, Rickey Taylor, left on foot to retrieve Mr. Stewart’s driver’s license from his home nearby, and the officer put Mr. Stewart, 19, into the back seat of his patrol car. A dispatcher told Officer Schilling that there were two warrants for Mr. Stewart in other states, both issued when he was 13. According to the police, the officer then tried to get Mr. Stewart out of the car in order to handcuff and arrest him. They said Mr. Stewart kicked the car door open, took the handcuffs, hit Officer Schilling with them and ran. The officer caught Mr. Stewart, and a physical struggle followed, partly captured on video. Mr. Stewart was black. Officer Schilling is white.

    8. Albert J. Davis
      July 17, 2015 — Orlando, Fla.

      An officer fatally shot a man who was involved in a disturbance and resisted arrest, the police said. The officer, Kelvin Vidro, responded to a report of a fight at an apartment, but when he tried to arrest the man, Albert J. Davis, Mr. Davis fled. The officer chased him, and there was what officials described as a violent struggle. Officer Vidro shot Mr. Davis, 23, once in the chest. Mr. Davis was black. Officer Vidro’s race is not publicly known.

    9. Salvado Ellswood
      July 12, 2015 — Plantation, Fla.

      An officer shot and killed a homeless man who had assaulted him, officials said. The man, Salvado Ellswood, 36, had recently completed a prison term for battery on a police officer, one of several convictions on his record. Officials said that when a Plantation police officer, Erik Carlton, found him behind a medical office building and told him to leave, Mr. Ellswood punched the officer in the face. They said that Officer Carlton used a stun gun, to no effect, and that when Mr. Ellswood continued to be aggressive, the officer shot him. Mr. Ellswood was black. Officer Carlton’s race was not disclosed.

    10. Johnny Ray Anderson
      July 5, 2015 — Hawaiian Gardens, Calif.

      A deputy investigating a call about a possible burglar fatally shot a homeless man under disputed circumstances. Los Angeles County sheriff’s deputies went to the neighborhood after a report of a prowler going door to door, asking to be let in. Johnny Ray Anderson, 43, and his wife, Kathleen, had been squatting illegally in a vacant house. His wife said he had been in the driveway fixing a bicycle when he saw the deputies and decided to run, jumping fences into neighboring yards. A short time later, officials said, Mr. Anderson surprised one deputy and tried to take his gun, and the deputy shot him once in the chest. A lawyer for his family said that when Mr. Anderson was shot, he was too far away to pose any threat, much less take the gun. Mr. Anderson was white. The deputy’s name and race have not been made public.

    11. John Deming Jr.
      July 5, 2015 — Pleasanton, Calif.

      An officer shot and killed a man who had broken into an exotic car dealership and then fought with the officer, the police said. The family of the man, John Deming Jr., 19, has disputed the official account of the confrontation, which is under investigation. Pleasanton police officers responded to a burglary call and could see Mr. Deming inside the dealership, jumping on cars, officials said. They said when officers entered, Mr. Deming went to a back room and out a window, and then turned and fought with Officer Daniel Kunkel, beating the officer almost to unconsciousness. Officer Kunkel used his stun gun to no avail, they said, and then shot Mr. Deming three times. Mr. Deming’s family contends that police radio traffic shows that only 22 seconds passed from when officers entered the dealership to when Mr. Deming was shot — not enough time for all of that to happen. They have also questioned the severity of the injuries sustained by Officer Kunkel, who spent less than a day in the hospital. Mr. Deming was white, as is Officer Kunkel.

    12. Richard Munroe
      July 5, 2015 — Austin, Tex.

      Officers fatally shot a man who had pointed a BB gun at them, officials said. A 911 dispatcher told the police of a call from the man, Richard Munroe, 25, and said that he sounded distraught and possibly suicidal. Three officers who went to his home said they had fired after he pulled a pistol from his waistband. It turned out to be a BB gun. Mr. Munroe was white. The races of the officers — Stephen Johnson, Matthew Murphy and John Nelson — were not released.

    13. Victo Larosa III
      July 2, 2015 — Jacksonville, Fla.

      A detective shot and killed a driver who had fled on foot after hitting another officer with a car. The man, Victo Larosa III, 23, had been driving a stolen car and was about to be arrested for selling drugs in a sting operation by the Jacksonville Sheriff’s Office. The authorities said that when he saw officers approaching on foot, he tried to drive away and struck one of them, a sergeant, who was taken to a hospital and released the next day. He also hit several other cars before crashing into an apartment house, and then got out of the car and ran. Detective Mike Boree caught up with him a few blocks away. Officials said Mr. Larosa reached for his waistband and Detective Boree fired eight shots, five of which hit Mr. Larosa, who was unarmed. Mr. Larosa was black. Detective Boree’s race was not released.

    14. Douglas M. Buckley
      July 2, 2015 — Brockton, Mass.

      A man was shot and killed by officers after brandishing realistic-looking BB guns. Two Brockton police officers went to the home of the man, Douglas M. Buckley, 45, after a late-night call from his wife reporting that he was drunk and making threats. Family members said later that he was suicidal. His wife said she told the officers repeatedly that Mr. Buckley had BB guns, not real weapons. According to officials, the officers found Mr. Buckley in his yard holding a rifle, and he dropped it but then drew a pistol and pointed it at them. The two officers, who have not been publicly identified, began firing. The state attorney general has opened an investigation. Mr. Buckley was white. Witnesses said the officers were also white.

    15. Kevin L. Judson
      July 1, 2015 — McMinnville, Ore.

      A Yamhill County sheriff's deputy fatally shot a man who was trying to drive off in the deputy’s patrol car. The episode began when a deputy made a routine traffic stop of a car with three people in it. One of the passengers, Kevin L. Judson, fled on foot; officials said he was later found to be in possession of methamphetamine. Soon after Mr. Judson ran off, a second deputy, Richard Broyles, arrived in another patrol car and chased him down. In the struggle that followed, partly captured on video, Mr. Judson, 24, got into the driver’s seat of Deputy Broyles’s car. The deputy’s arm got caught in the door, and he fired twice at close range. The deputy broke free, and Mr. Judson began to drive off in reverse. As he pulled away and backed into a utility pole, the deputy fired twice more from several yards away. The district attorney ruled that Deputy Broyles’s actions were justified. Mr. Judson was black, and Deputy Broyles is white.

    16. Allan L. Bellew
      June 28, 2015 — Portland, Ore.

      Officers fatally shot a man who they said had pointed what turned out to be a realistic-looking starter’s pistol. Late at night, two Portland police officers approached three people in the parking lot of a grocery store and questioned them about a fight that had occurred at the store. One of the three, Allan L. Bellew, 29, who was wanted on a drug charge, reached into a parked car and drew the starter’s pistol. The police said he pointed it at the officers and the other civilians. Both officers, Michael Currier and Dominic Lovato, fired. A grand jury cleared them of wrongdoing.

    17. Spencer L. McCain
      June 25, 2015 — Owings Mills, Md.

      Officers responding to a domestic disturbance shot and killed an unarmed man. The Baltimore County police were called to a condominium unit that they had visited several times for domestic disturbances. A woman lived there with her three children and, at times, the children’s father, Spencer L. McCain, 41. Three officers heard screams, forced their way in and fired 19 shots at Mr. McCain. Officials said his movements made the officers think he was armed, but they did not elaborate. The woman had swelling and scratches on her face. Mr. McCain was black, as was one of the officers, according to a local N.A.A.C.P. official. The officers’ full names were not released.

    18. Joshua Dyer
      June 23, 2015 — Indianapolis

      An Indianapolis officer fatally shot a passenger in a car that had been involved in a police pursuit. An officer tried to question Mathew Cole, the driver of a car that was illegally parked, and Joshua Dyer, 34, the passenger. But Mr. Cole drove off with officers in pursuit, crashing through fences before coming to a stop, officials said. They said Mr. Cole got out of the car, but then got back in and drove in reverse toward officers who were on foot. One of them fired, striking Mr. Dyer. The officer’s name and race have not been released. Mr. Dyer was white.

    19. Adrian Simental
      June 21, 2015 — Azusa, Calif.

      An officer shot and killed a man who was already blood-spattered and who had reportedly been acting bizarrely, broken into a neighbor’s house and asked the police to shoot him. The family of the man, Adrian Simental, called the police to report that he had hurt himself and was behaving strangely. He then broke into the house next door, where no one was home. An Azusa police officer confronted Mr. Simental, who was bloodied, behind that house. A witness said Mr. Simental told the officer to shoot him and then ran toward other houses. Officials have not said what prompted the officer to fire. Mr. Simental was Hispanic. The officer's name and race have not been released.

    20. Kris Jackson
      June 15, 2015 — South Lake Tahoe, Calif.

      An unarmed man who climbed out of a motel window to evade the police was shot and killed by an officer responding to a 911 call of a woman crying and screaming. The man, Kris Jackson, 22, was at the hotel with his girlfriend, who reportedly told his family that he fled for fear of being arrested on an outstanding drug charge. Officer Joshua Klinge radioed that he saw someone going out a back window of the motel, and 18 seconds later, he radioed that he had shot the man. Officials said Officer Klinge told them he had “perceived a deadly threat,” but they have not elaborated. Mr. Jackson was mixed race. Officer Klinge is white.

    21. Ryan K. Bolinger
      June 9, 2015 — Des Moines

      An officer shot through the closed window of a police car, killing a man who had been acting strangely and was approaching the vehicle. The Des Moines police said that when one of their officers made a routine traffic stop, the man, Ryan K. Bolinger, who was not involved in the stop, pulled up alongside the police car, so close that the officer could not open his door. Mr. Bolinger, 28, stepped out of his car and moved around in the street, almost as if dancing, and then got back in and drove away, but not at high speed. Later, officers managed to box him between two police cars. He got out again and “walked with a purpose” toward the car driven by Officer Vanessa Miller, a police spokesman said. She fired once at him through the window. The police have not said there was any reason to think he was armed. Mr. Bolinger was white, as is Officer Miller.

    22. Rudy Baca
      June 3, 2015 — Los Chavez, N.M.

      A deputy fatally shot a man who had fought with him and had reportedly said he was determined not to go to jail. The mother of the man, identified as Rudy Baca, 36, asked the Valencia County Sheriff’s Department to check on him because he had been drinking. She told a local television station, KOAT, that her son, who had spent time in jail, punched the deputy and said, “You’re not taking me back.” After the deputy drew his gun and told Mr. Baca to calm down, she said, he took a step toward the deputy, who fired. The deputy’s name and race were not released. Mr. Baca was Hispanic.

    23. James D. Bushey
      May 31, 2015 — Palestine, Tex.

      Officers shot and killed a suspect in a theft who had pointed a realistic-looking BB gun at them. Sgt. Gabriel Green and Officer Kaylynn Griffin of the Palestine police responded to a report that a man had stolen beer from a Walmart store and then gone to an Applebee’s restaurant. They found a suspect, James D. Bushey, in a restaurant bathroom and escorted him outside. As they were trying to identify him, he drew the pellet gun and pointed it at the officers. The episode was captured on video from body cameras worn by the officers. Mr. Bushey was white, as are both officers.

    24. Ebin Proctor
      May 30, 2015 — Cottonwood, Ariz.

      A deputy fatally shot a man who had fled on foot after a traffic stop and then reportedly fought with the deputy. There was a warrant out for the arrest of the man, Ebin Proctor, 18, for violating his probation on a previous conviction. When a car he was in was pulled over before dawn by a Yavapai County sheriff’s deputy, Steven Gorman, Mr. Proctor gave the deputy a false name and then ran. The deputy caught Mr. Proctor, who the sheriff’s office said fought when the deputy tried to subdue him. A stun gun and pepper spray were ineffective, the office said, and Mr. Proctor tried to take Deputy Gorman’s gun, so the deputy shot him. Mr. Proctor was white, as is Deputy Gorman.

    25. Feras Morad
      May 27, 2015 — Long Beach, Calif.

      An officer shot and killed a college student who officials said had fought with the officer. The student, Feras Morad, 20, a highly ranked debater in high school and college, fell or jumped from a second-story window; a family member said he had taken hallucinogenic mushrooms. A 911 caller reported that he was bloodied, acting strangely and “a little bit violent.” A Long Beach police officer, Matthew Hernandez, arrived, and Mr. Morad advanced on him, refusing commands to stop, even after the officer used a stun gun. After a physical struggle, Officer Hernandez shot him. Mr. Morad was of Middle Eastern descent. Officer Hernandez is Hispanic.

    26. Michael Lowrey
      May 22, 2015 — Somerset, Pa.

      An officer fatally shot a burglar who the authorities said was holding a realistic-looking BB gun. State troopers responded to a burglar alarm at a grocery store during the night and found a rear door forced open. Inside, one trooper confronted Michael Lowrey. Officials said Mr. Lowrey ignored orders to drop the gun, and the officer shot him. Mr. Lowrey, who was suspected in a string of burglaries, was white. The trooper’s name and race have not been released.

    27. Matthew Coates
      May 15, 2015 — Sacramento

      A Sacramento police officer looking into a domestic disturbance complaint shot and killed a man who was holding a realistic-looking BB gun. The girlfriend of the man, Matthew Coates, 42, said she had told the officer before he fired that the gun was a toy. She also said that Mr. Coates had bipolar disorder. Mr. Coates was white. The police did not release the name or race of the officer.

    28. Brendon K. Glenn
      May 5, 2015 — Los Angeles

      An officer shot and killed a homeless panhandler after a brief scuffle near the beach boardwalk in the Venice neighborhood of Los Angeles. Video of the altercation has not been made public, but officials who saw it, including Police Chief Charlie Beck, have said it raises questions about whether the shooting was justified. Two officers responded to a call that the panhandler, Brendon K. Glenn, was harassing customers outside a restaurant, and shooed him away. A short time later, they saw him in a physical struggle with someone outside another restaurant, officials said. The officers tried to subdue Mr. Glenn, and one of them shot him.

    29. Terrance Kellom
      April 27, 2015 — Detroit

      An officer fatally shot a suspect in an armed robbery who he said had lunged at him with a hammer inside the suspect’s home. Family members of the suspect, Terrance Kellom, 20, dispute the officer's account, saying Mr. Kellom was empty-handed and posed no threat when he was killed. Mitchell Quinn, a federal Immigration and Customs Enforcement officer, was part of a multiagency team serving an arrest warrant on Mr. Kellom, who reportedly fired seven rounds. Officials have so far refused to release an autopsy report or video taken by cameras inside the house. Mr. Kellom was black, as is Officer Quinn.


    30. Billy Joe Patrick
      April 26, 2015 — Adair County, Okla.

      A man who was wanted by the police and reportedly tried to drown a state wildlife officer was shot and killed by the officer. The officer approached three anglers and asked them to produce fishing licenses. In checking their backgrounds, the officer learned that one of the men, Billy Joe Patrick, 29, was wanted in Arkansas as a parole absconder. When the officer tried to arrest him, Mr. Patrick fought, pushed the officer’s head under water and tried to hold him there, officials said. The officer was able to surface and shot Mr. Patrick once. Mr. Patrick was white. The officer's name and race were not made public.

    31. William Chapman
      April 22, 2015 — Portsmouth, Va.

      A Portsmouth police officer shot and killed a shoplifting suspect in the parking lot of a Walmart store after the suspect, William Chapman, 22, reportedly resisted arrest. The officer, Stephen Rankin, had killed another unarmed person in 2011 and had not been put back on patrol duty for almost three years. After the earlier episode, he was found to have posted disturbing messages online that seemed to indicate a willingness to use violence. Mr. Chapman was black, and Officer Rankin is white.

    32. Daniel Covarrubias
      April 21, 2015 — Lakewood, Wash.

      Lakewood police officers fatally shot a man who was behaving strangely, saying they mistook a cellphone he drew from his pocket for a gun. The man, Daniel Covarrubias, 37, had been having hallucinations, and officials said he had told a social worker at a hospital where he had gone for help that he had been using methamphetamine and had not slept in days. Callers to 911 reported seeing him running and speculated that he was fleeing the police; he was not. Mr. Covarrubias was found in a lumber yard, where two officers, David Butts and Ryan Hamilton, confronted him. They shot him five times. The Pierce County prosecutor decided that the shooting was justified. Mr. Covarubbias was Hispanic. The officers are white.

    33. Frank Shephard III
      April 15, 2015 — Houston

      An unarmed man who had led Houston police officers on a car chase, stopping after he had slammed into other vehicles, was shot and killed after he stepped out of his car. The officers tried to make a routine traffic stop, but the man — Frank Shephard III, known as Trey, who had an outstanding arrest warrant — sped away. His mother has said that during the pursuit, he called her and told her he loved her, and refused to follow her advice to stop the car. He also called 911 to say that there was a child in the car, which was untrue. Video of the last minutes of the chase shows him coming to a stop after ramming a small car, whose driver was injured, and a sport-utility vehicle. Mr. Shephard, 41, can be seen getting out of the car and then starting to duck back into it. Law enforcement officials said his move back into the car prompted officers to open fire. Mr. Shephard was black. No gun was found in the car. The officers have not been identified, and their race or ethnicity is not clear in the video.

    34. Ernesto Flores
      April 15, 2015 — Montclair, Calif.

      Deputies shot and killed an apparently disturbed man who was holding what turned out to be a pellet gun. The daughter of the man, Ernesto Flores, 52, called 911, saying that her father was acting erratically and breaking things, and seeking help to get him under control. She later said she had told the dispatcher that the gun he was holding was a toy. Family members said Mr. Flores was unarmed, and witnesses said he exited his house with his hands in the air. But the San Bernardino County Sheriff’s Department said he emerged holding both the pellet gun and a knife. After deputies knocked him down by firing “bean bag” rounds, the department said, he pointed the gun their way, and deputies shot him several times. It is not clear whether he was holding a knife at that point. Mr. Flores was Latino. The deputies have not been identified.

    35. Celin Nunez
      April 14, 2015 — Houston

      An off-duty Houston police officer shot and killed an unarmed man who the police said had pointed what turned out to be a welding gun at the officer. The man, Celin Nunez, 24, was at a gas station and behaving erratically — talking to himself, getting in and out of other people’s cars and hitting one customer with a rock. Witnesses said it seemed he either had a mental illness or was trying to rob people. When customers tried to subdue him, Mr. Nunez climbed into a pickup truck with tinted windows. An off-duty officer who was working as a security guard, David Sudderth, saw the commotion and stopped at the gas station. Looking into the truck, officials said, he thought Mr. Nunez was pointing a shotgun at him, and opened fire.

    36. Walter L. Scott
      April 4, 2015 — North Charleston, S.C.

      A police officer fatally shot an unarmed man in the back as he tried to run away after a scuffle. A bystander’s video showed Officer Michael T. Slager of the North Charleston police firing eight times, with several of the shots striking the man, Walter L. Scott, 50. Mr. Scott had been driving when Officer Slager pulled him over for a broken taillight. It is not clear why Mr. Scott might have resisted or why he fled. Officer Slager said at the scene that Mr. Scott had tried to take his stun gun. Mr. Scott was black, and Officer Slager, who has been charged with murder, is white.

    37. Eric C. Harris
      April 2, 2015 — Tulsa, Okla.

      A volunteer reserve deputy shot and killed an unarmed man, apparently firing his pistol by mistake when he intended to use a stun gun. Tulsa County sheriff’s deputies were about to arrest the man, Eric Courtney Harris, 44, on a charge of illegal gun sale, but Mr. Harris, who had an extensive criminal history, fled on foot. Among those who chased him down was the reserve deputy, Robert Charles Bates, 73, who is white. A deputy wearing a body camera knocked Mr. Harris, who was black, to the ground, but officials say he continued to resist as he lay face-down in the street. In a video of the episode, a voice, apparently that of Mr. Bates, can be heard saying “Taser,” as if to warn other deputies that he is about to shock the suspect. Then there is a single gunshot, and a voice says, “Oh, I shot him, I’m sorry.” Mr. Harris can be heard repeatedly shouting, “He shot me,” while a deputy kneels on his head and others yell at him to stop struggling. It later emerged that Mr. Bates might not have had the training required for his post, and questions were raised about whether his qualifications had been falsified. Critics said he had been allowed to hold the position only because he was a friend of the sheriff's and had donated expensive equipment to the department. Mr. Bates has been charged with second-degree manslaughter.

    38. Nicholas Thomas
      March 24, 2015 — Cobb County, Ga.

      A Smyrna police officer fatally shot a driver who was trying to evade arrest. Officers went to an auto repair shop where the driver, Nicholas Thomas, 23, worked to arrest him for a probation violation, and Mr. Thomas tried to flee in a customer’s Maserati. A vehicle might be considered a weapon, depending on how it is used; otherwise, Mr. Thomas, who was black, was unarmed. Officers said he circled the building several times at high speed. The police and prosecutors said Mr. Thomas threatened to run the officers down, and that the officer, Sgt. Kenneth Owens, was justified in shooting him. Mr. Thomas's family disputed that conclusion, saying that Sergeant Owens shot into the car from the side, not the front, and that the fatal bullet hit Mr. Thomas in the back.

    39. Jamison Childress
      March 19, 2015 — Sumas, Wash.

      An unarmed Canadian man was shot and killed by a Border Patrol agent just after illegally crossing the international border on foot in a rural area. Two agents confronted the man, Jamison E. Childress, 20, who faced several criminal charges in British Columbia and was wanted there in a homicide case. The agents said he behaved aggressively, refused commands to stop, told the agents to shoot him and sprayed one of them with bear spray. The agent who was sprayed shot Mr. Childress once, in the head, a shooting that prosecutors said was justified. Mr. Childress was white. The agent who shot him has not been publicly identified.

    40. Brandon Jones
      March 19, 2015 — Cleveland

      A Cleveland police officer fatally shot an unarmed man who had broken into a grocery store to rob it. The man, Brandon Jones, 18, broke into the store when it was closed for the night, and two officers confronted him as he emerged with cigarettes and coins he had taken. The police said a struggle followed, and Officer Alan Buford shot Mr. Jones once at close range. Mr. Jones was black, as is Officer Buford.

    41. Bobby Gross
      March 12, 2015 — Washington

      A female Metro Transit Police Department officer fatally shot a partly undressed man who was trespassing in areas of a Metro station that are off-limits to the public. The man, Bobby Gross, 35, was shot in a tunnel at the Potomac Avenue station. A large stick or branch was found in the tunnel, but officials have given conflicting accounts on whether Mr. Gross, who was black, was using it as a weapon. He was otherwise unarmed. The officer was not publicly identified.

    42. Anthony Hill
      March 9, 2015 — Chamblee, Ga.

      A police officer shot and killed a naked, unarmed man who was knocking on doors and running and crawling through an apartment complex. The officer, Robert Olsen of the DeKalb County Police, is white; the dead man, Anthony Hill, 27, who had bipolar disorder, was black. The police said that Mr. Hill had refused Officer Olsen’s commands to stop, but that it was not clear whether he had been charging at the officer. A grand jury is expected to review the results of an investigation into the shooting.

    43. Tony Robinson
      March 6, 2015 — Madison, Wis.

      An officer shot and killed an unarmed man after responding to a report that the man, Tony Robinson, 19, had assaulted two people and had been jumping in front of moving cars. The police and prosecutors said that Mr. Robinson, who was black, fought with and injured the police officer, Matthew Kenny, who is white, and that the officer's actions were justified.

    44. Naeschylus Vinzant
      March 6, 2015 — Aurora, Colo.

      An unarmed man was fatally shot as officers who had been searching for him tried to arrest him, the police said. The man, Naeschylus Vinzant, 37, had a long criminal history and was wanted in a kidnapping and robbery, and for cutting off the electronic parole monitor on his ankle, according to the police. They have not identified the officer who fired or said why he fired. Mr. Vinzant was black.

    45. Sergio A. Navas
      March 5, 2015 — Burbank, Calif.

      A Los Angeles police officer shot and killed an unarmed motorist who had tried to elude officers trying to pull him over. Officers tried to stop the motorist, Sergio A. Navas, 35, but he drove into the neighboring city of Burbank, where the police S.U.V. and the car Mr. Navas was driving collided in what officials described as an accident. At some point, the officer in the passenger’s seat of the police vehicle, who has not been publicly identified, fired several shots into the car, killing Mr. Navas, who was Latino. The officers later learned that the car was stolen.

    46. Derek Cruice
      March 4, 2015 — Deltona, Fla.

      A sheriff’s deputy fatally shot an unarmed man in his home while serving a search warrant. Officials said Volusia County SWAT team members taking part in a drug investigation “met with resistance” on entering the house, an account that occupants of the house said was false. Deputy Todd Raible shot the man, Derek Cruice, 26, who was white, once in the face just inside the front door. The team reported finding several ounces of marijuana.

    47. Thomas L. Allen Jr.
      March 2, 2015 — Wellston, Mo.

      A Wellston police officer fatally shot an unarmed motorist who was trying to drive away from the scene of a traffic stop while a 5-year-old girl sat in the back seat. According to the St. Louis County Police Department, the officer, after pulling the car over, told the driver and two adult passengers to get out. After searching the car and one of the passengers, Thomas L. Allen Jr., and finding nothing suspicious, the officer allowed Mr. Allen, 34, to get back into the car and continued talking with the other two adults. After a second officer arrived, officials said, Mr. Allen climbed from the back seat into the front and drove off. One of the officers jumped into the front passenger seat of the moving car, struggled with Mr. Allen for control of the vehicle and fired his gun three times. Wounded, Mr. Allen crashed the car. He died the next day. Mr. Allen was black. The officer has not been publicly identified.

    48. Charly Leundeu Keunang
      March 1, 2015 — Los Angeles

      Los Angeles police officers shot and killed an unarmed homeless man who, the police said, had tried to take an officer’s gun during a scuffle. Multiple videos of the episode on Skid Row show the man, Charly Leundeu Keunang, 43, who had a history of mental illness, fighting with a group of officers. The videos show four officers forcing him to the ground. The clicking of a stun gun can be heard, followed by at least five gunshots. The authorities have not said how many officers fired, and the Los Angeles Police Department has not released video from the officers' body cameras. But a journalist who viewed at least some of that footage wrote in GQ magazine that it contradicted accounts the officers had given to investigators. Mr. Keunang, who was from Cameroon and was known as Africa, was black. The officers, who have not been officially identified, are of various ethnicities.

    49. Deven Guilford
      Feb. 28, 2015 — Roxand Township, Mich.

      A sheriff’s sergeant shot and killed an unarmed teenager who had been uncooperative and fought with him during a traffic stop. The sergeant, Jonathan Frost of the Eaton County Sheriff’s Office, stopped the teenager, Deven Guilford, 17, for flashing his high beams. Video taken by the sergeant's body camera and Mr. Guilford's cellphone shows Mr. Guilford arguing at length with Sergeant Frost, refusing to hand over his driver's license, resisting getting out of his car and, after lying on the ground, refusing to put his hands behind his back. Sergeant Frost called for backup and used a stun gun on Mr. Guilford. At this point, the videos stop. Sergeant Frost said Mr. Guilford got on top of him and punched him repeatedly, and officials later released photos of the sergeant's bloodied face. He shot Mr. Guilford seven times. Mr. Guilford was white, as is Sergeant Frost.

    50. Ernesto J. Canepa Díaz
      Feb. 27, 2015 — Santa Ana, Calif.

      Police officers fatally shot an unarmed man they suspected of committing a robbery. Santa Ana police officers approached Ernesto J. Canepa Díaz, 27, a native of Mexico, because he was sitting in a vehicle that matched that of a man who had taken a gold chain from a woman, officials have said. But they have not said what prompted the officers to open fire, and they have not publicly identified the officers. Family members say that Mr. Canepa who was Latino, was unarmed, which the authorities have not refuted. Officials have said that there was a replica pistol – possibly a BB gun – in the vehicle, but have not said whether he was holding it, or whether it played any part in the shooting or the earlier robbery.

    51. Daniel Elrod
      Feb. 23, 2015 — Omaha

      An unarmed man suspected of having just robbed a store was shot twice in the back and killed by an Omaha police officer. Four officers responding to reports of the robbery confronted Daniel Elrod, 39, two blocks away. The police said he had climbed onto a car, ignored commands to get down on the ground and repeatedly reached toward his waistband. Officers and witnesses said he had dared the police to shoot him. When Mr. Elrod turned and grabbed a fence, Officer Alvin Lugod fired at him. Mr. Elrod had an extensive criminal history, and Officer Lugod had arrested him on a domestic violence charge a month earlier. He had been released from jail a few hours before the shooting. There was also long trail of evidence that he was distraught and suicidal. A grand jury found no criminal wrongdoing by Officer Lugod, who quit the police force a short time later. Mr. Elrod was white; Officer Lugod is Asian-American.

    52. Rubén García Villalpando
      Feb. 20, 2015 — Euless, Tex.

      A Grapevine police officer fatally shot an unarmed man who had led the officer on a chase but had then stopped. The man, Rubén García Villalpando, raised his hands and got out of his car in response to commands from the officer, Robert W. Clark. But video video taken by a dashboard camera in Officer Clark's patrol car also shows Mr. García repeatedly defying commands to keep his hands up and to stay by his own car. Instead, he repeatedly steps toward the officer, ignoring orders to back up. Officer Clark fired two shots into his chest, which can be heard on the recording but not seen. Tests later showed that Mr. García, 31, was legally drunk. Officer Clark had responded to a burglar alarm at a closed business and had seen a car in the parking lot that drove off as he approached. He pursued the car with his lights and sirens on. The pursuit ended in nearby Euless, where Mr. García, a Mexican national, stopped on the shoulder of a highway. A grand jury cleared the officer of wrongdoing.

    53. Dillon Taylor
      Aug. 11, 2014 — Salt Lake City

      A Salt Lake City officer responding to a report of young men brandishing a gun shot and killed an unarmed man who failed to comply with commands and appeared to reach toward his waist. Outside a convenience store, officers confronted the man, Dillon Taylor, 20, and his brother and cousin, who the police said fit the description in a 911 call of the three men flashing a gun. The other two men stopped and raised their hands, but Mr. Taylor kept walking away from the officers. He was wearing earbuds, and his family members have said he could not hear the police commands. Video taken by a body camera worn by Officer Bron Cruz shows Mr. Taylor raising his hands to his waist and the officer, his gun drawn, yelling, “Get your hands out now!” Mr. Taylor turns to face Officer Cruz and, walking backward, continues to move away from him. He appears to lift his T-shirt with one hand, and Officer Cruz fires. The police said Mr. Taylor’s gestures had suggested that he was drawing a gun; his brother said he had been hiking up his pants. Neither Mr. Taylor nor his companions were armed. It later emerged that Mr. Taylor was drunk at the time and that there was an outstanding warrant for his arrest. The district attorney called the shooting justified. Mr. Taylor was white; Officer Cruz's race and ethnicity have not been released.

    54. Michael S. Ireland
      Feb. 17, 2015 — Springfield, Mo.

      A police officer shot and killed an unarmed man after a short foot chase. Officers responded to a report of “suspicious activity” and approached the man, Michael S. Ireland, who ran away, the Springfield Police Department said. Mr. Ireland had a felony record and was on parole. The department said Officer Andrew Bath ran after Mr. Ireland and caught him in a backyard a few blocks away, where there was some kind of confrontation; officials have not elaborated on what happened. Officer Bath shot Mr. Ireland, 31, once in the chest. Mr. Ireland was white, as is Mr. Bath, who has since left the police force.

    55. Cody Evans
      Feb. 15, 2015 — Provo, Utah

      Officers shot and killed an unarmed man who had brandished a realistic-looking toy gun. The wife of the man, Cody Evans, 24, had called 911, saying that her husband had committed domestic violence and that he wanted the police to kill him, and warning that he had a realistic-looking BB gun. Officials said information about the toy gun had been relayed to officers responding to the call. They said Mr. Evans refused to surrender, threatened officers, displayed what looked like an AR-15 assault rifle and then drove off. Officers found him again a short time later, and rather than surrender, officials said, Mr. Evans ran to his vehicle, retrieved the pellet gun and pointed it at the officers. Officer Zach Lazenby of the Provo Police Department and Deputy Chris Bagley of the Utah County Sheriff’s Office fired a total of three shots. Mr. Evans was white, as are the officers.

    56. Antonio Zambrano-Montes
      Feb. 10, 2015 — Pasco, Wash.

      Three Pasco officers fired 17 shots at a man who had thrown rocks at people, killing him. The officers went to the scene in response to a report of a man throwing rocks at passing cars, and the police say the man, Antonio Zambrano-Montes, hit some officers with rocks. There have been conflicting statements about whether Mr. Zambrano-Montes had a mental illness. A video taken by a bystander shows him standing near the officers, throwing an object at them or their car, and then moving away from them across a busy street. Two officers can be seen firing at him a few times as he retreats, and then all three give chase. Seconds later, he turns to face them while lifting an object in his hand, and all three officers open fire. Mr. Zambrano-Montes, who was Hispanic, drops the object and falls to the sidewalk, his body turning away from the officers as he falls; a couple of shots can be heard after he is down. In all, as many as seven bullets hit him. His family has said two rounds struck him in the back, while the police say none did; each side has cited its own autopsy. The officers were identified as Ryan Flanagan, Adam Wright and Adrian Alaniz.

    57. John Sawyer
      Feb. 5, 2015 — Calimesa, Calif.

      Deputies shot and killed an unarmed man who they said had displayed a realistic-looking toy gun. The Riverside County Sheriff’s Department said two of its deputies approached two men believed to have been involved in a drug deal. The department said one suspect, John Sawyer, 36, drew a gun from his pocket that was later determined to be a “replica handgun,” prompting the shooting. An assault rifle was found in Mr. Sawyer's car, officials said.

    58. Jeremy Lett
      Feb. 4, 2015 — Tallahassee, Fla.

      A Tallahassee police officer investigating a report of a possible burglary at an apartment complex shot and killed an unarmed man who fought with him. The officer, David Stith, confronted the man, Jeremy Lett, 28, who lived in the complex, and a fight ensued. Some witnesses said Mr. Lett pinned the officer to the ground. Officer Stith shot him five times. A grand jury cleared the officer of wrongdoing. Mr. Lett was black, as is Officer Stith.

    59. David Kassick
      Feb. 2, 2015 — Hummelstown, Pa.

      As an unarmed suspect lay face-down on the ground, an officer fatally shot him in the back, saying later that she thought he was reaching for a weapon. The episode began with an attempted traffic stop in Hummelstown, just east of Harrisburg, for expired inspection stickers. Officer Lisa Mearkle of the Hummelstown Police Department tried to pull over the suspect, David Kassick, 59, but he sped away to his sister’s house, where he had been living, and then ran around to the back of the house with Officer Mearkle chasing him. Though it is not clear why he fled, Mr. Kassick had a history of drug addiction and criminal convictions, and officials said a syringe was found near his body. Officer Mearkle’s lawyer said Mr. Kassick had refused repeated commands to stop and raise his hands. The officer fired a stun gun, causing Mr. Kassick to fall to the snow-covered ground, but she said he kept reaching beneath him, as if for a weapon, despite commands to show his hands. She shot him twice in the back. The district attorney has charged Officer Mearkle with homicide, saying that a video, not yet made public, shows that Mr. Kassick’s moves were not threatening. Mr. Kassick was white, as is Officer Mearkle.

    60. Ralph Willis III
      Jan. 29, 2015 — Stillwater, Okla.

      A Stillwater police officer fatally shot an unarmed man suspected of murder and arson who officials say wanted the police to kill him. The police suspected the man, Ralph Willis III, 42, of stabbing a neighbor to death and setting fire to the victim’s home. Officers spotted Mr. Willis and his wife hours later and tried to arrest them. They said Mr. Willis screamed at the officers to kill him, refused orders to show his hands and get on the ground, and made a movement as if to draw a gun. Detective Cody Manuel shot him once in the chest. Officials said that his wife, questioned after the shooting, said that her husband, who had a criminal record, had told her that he would not return to prison and would rather force the police to kill him. Mr. Willis was white.

    61. Joshua O. Garcia
      Jan. 26, 2015 — Lynn County, Tex.

      Sheriff's deputies fatally shot an unarmed, handcuffed man when he tried to drive away in their sport-utility vehicle. The deputies tried to pull him over, but the man, Joshua O. Garcia, refused to stop, leading the deputies on a chase. Officials said that after Mr. Garcia crashed his car into a Lynn County Sheriff’s Office vehicle, he was arrested, handcuffed behind his back and placed in the S.U.V. while the deputies searched his car. They said he somehow managed to move his cuffed hands to the front of his body and take control of the vehicle, but he drove only a few feet before being shot. Mr. Garcia was Hispanic; the deputies have not been identified publicly.

    62. Joseph Caffarello
      Jan. 7, 2015 — Rosemont, Ill.

      An off-duty Rosemont police officer shot and killed his unarmed brother-in-law after the brother-in-law rammed his car into the officer’s and reportedly threatened to kill him. The officer, Rick Drehobl, and another off-duty officer went to the home of Officer Drehobl’s sister, where they had an altercation with her husband, Joseph Caffarello. Officer Drehobl tried to drive away with his sister and her son, but Mr. Caffarello crashed into their car with his own. Both men got out of their cars, and Officer Drebohl said Mr. Caffarello advanced on him and threatened to kill him. He shot Mr. Caffarello once in the chest. Prosecutors decided not to file charges. Mr. Caffarello was white, as is Officer Drehobl.

    63. Autumn Steele
      Jan. 6, 2015 — Burlington, Iowa

      An officer who fired at a dog he said was attacking him proceeded to fatally shoot one of the animal’s owners, who was unarmed. Gabriel Steele called 911 to report that his wife, Autumn, was assaulting him at their home. She had been arrested the day before after allegedly assaulting him, and a court had ordered her to have no contact with him. According to a police investigation, Officer Jesse Hill responded to the 911 call, and saw Mr. Steele leaving the home with the couple's 4-year-old child in his arms while Ms. Steele followed, hitting him. Investigators reported that Officer Hill was trying separate them when the family dog charged him and bit him. In a brief, jumbled video, he can be heard saying, “Hey, get your dog,” before firing two shots. One bullet grazed Ms. Steele, 34, and the other struck her in the chest. She was white, as is the officer, who was cleared of wrongdoing.

    64. Leslie Sapp
      Jan. 6, 2015 — Knoxville, Pa.

      Members of a United States Marshals Service task force shot and killed Leslie Sapp, who they said had approached them holding a realistic-looking pellet gun. The task force had gone to the home of Mr. Sapp, who was a fugitive wanted on a charge of raping a child, to arrest him. One task force member, an Allegheny County sheriff’s deputy, tested positive for cocaine and was fired. The district attorney found the shooting justified. Mr. Sapp was black. The deputy was white; the identities and races of the other task force members have not been made public.

    65. Matthew Hoffman
      Jan. 4, 2015 — San Francisco

      Officers fatally shot a man with a BB gun who officials said had wanted the police to kill him. The man, Matthew Hoffman, 32, had written suicide notes on his phone, apologizing to the officers involved, according to the San Francisco Police Department. Mr. Hoffman walked into the parking lot of a police station, an area that is off-limits to the public, and when confronted by three sergeants, he drew a BB gun, the department said. Two of the sergeants fired, hitting him three times.

    66. Dennis Grigsby
      Dec. 15, 2014 — Texarkana, Tex.

      While investigating a report of a possible burglary, a Texarkana police officer shot and killed an unarmed man who he said had advanced on him while holding a metal object, which turned out to be a spoon. Residents whose home had been burglarized before heard noises in their garage. They say that they called out to the intruder to leave but that he began banging on a window, so they called the police. The man turned out to be Dennis Grigsby, 35, who was living across the street with his mother and had a history of mental illness; his mother said he had been barefoot and wearing pajamas when he died. The officer, Brent Lawing, who found him in the dimly lit garage, said Mr. Grigsby had moved toward him aggressively and refused orders to stop. He fired once, striking Mr. Grigsby in the chest. Mr. Grigsby was black; the race of Officer Lawing, who was cleared of wrongdoing, was not disclosed.

    67. Rumain Brisbon
      Dec. 2, 2014 — Phoenix

      An unarmed man bringing food home from McDonald’s for his family was fatally shot by a Phoenix police officer, who may have mistaken a pill bottle he felt in the man’s pocket for the handle of a gun. The police said that the officer, Mark Rine, had been investigating a report of drug dealing when he approached the man — Rumain Brisbon, 34, who was sitting in his S.U.V. — and that Mr. Brisbon had tried to run away, toward his apartment. Officials said that the men struggled and that when Mr. Brisbon put a hand in his pocket, Officer Rine put his own hand over Mr. Brisbon’s and felt a hard, cylindrical object. Fearing that it was a gun and unable to keep his grip, officials said, he shot Mr. Brisbon twice. Officer Rine, who was cleared of wrongdoing, is white; Mr. Brisbon was black. Searches of the apartment and the vehicle later turned up a stash of oxycodone, a stolen handgun and a large amount of cash.

    68. Tamir Rice
      Nov. 22, 2014 — Cleveland

      A rookie officer shot an unarmed 12-year-old boy who had been pointing a toy gun at people in a park. A 911 caller had complained about the boy, Tamir Rice, but had said twice that the gun was probably not real. A video released by the Cleveland Police Department showed a police car pulling to a stop a few feet away from Tamir, who was black. Within two seconds, the officer in the passenger seat, Timothy Loehmann, who is white, had fired. For several minutes, neither Officer Loehmann nor the driver, Officer Frank Garmback, who is also white, administered first aid. When Tamir's 14-year-old sister tried to go to his aid, the officers forced her to the ground and handcuffed her. It later emerged that Officer Loehmann had resigned from another police department after a supervisor found him emotionally unfit for law enforcement work. A judge later found probable cause to charge Officer Loehmann with manslaughter and Officer Garmback with negligent homicide. The case is being presented to a grand jury.

    69. Akai Gurley
      Nov. 20, 2014 — New York City

      A New York City police officer fatally shot an unarmed man in the unlit stairwell of a high-rise housing project in Brooklyn. The man, Akai Gurley, 28, was hit once in the chest by a bullet that apparently ricocheted off a wall; a lawyer for the officer, Peter Liang, has said he fired by accident. Officials said Mr. Gurley was not suspected of any crime and did not pose a threat, and it was not clear why Officer Liang had his gun drawn. Mr. Gurley was black; Officer Liang, who has been charged with manslaughter, is Asian-American.

    70. Carlos M. Perez Jr.
      Nov. 12, 2014 — Indian Springs, Nev.

      A correction officer fatally shot an unarmed, handcuffed inmate and wounded another, who was also unarmed and handcuffed. Prison officials said the officer opened fire after the inmate who was killed, Carlos M. Perez Jr., 28, and the other inmate, Andrew J. Arevalo, got into a fight at High Desert State Prison. Both men had their hands cuffed behind their backs. Prison officials have said little about what happened, but the Clark County coroner reported that Mr. Perez had been shot several times, in the head, neck, chest and arms. Mr. Arevalo was also shot, but he survived. The state attorney general's office is looking into the episode. Mr. Perez was Hispanic. The officer’s name and ethnicity have not been made public.

    71. Jack Jacquez
      Oct. 12, 2014 — Rocky Ford, Colo.

      An unarmed man who had just entered his home was fatally shot in the back by a Rocky Ford police officer who later said he thought the man, Jack Jacquez, 27, was a burglar. The killing led to a second-degree murder charge against the officer, James Ashby, whose trial is pending. It was the first time in two decades that a law enforcement officer in Colorado faced such a serious charge for an on-duty killing. Officer Ashby told investigators that he thought Mr. Jacquez had broken into the house and was going to attack him with a baseball bat. But investigators said that he never identified himself as an officer and that Mr. Jacquez was never a threat to him. Mr. Jacquez was Hispanic. Mr. Ashby is white.

    72. Cameron Tillman
      Sept. 23, 2014 — Terrebonne Parish, La.

      A sheriff’s deputy fatally shot an unarmed 14-year-old boy who he said was holding a realistic-looking BB gun. The boy, Cameron Tillman, 14, was playing with friends in a vacant house just outside Houma when the deputy, Preston Norman, went to the house, responding to a 911 call reporting armed men there. Deputy Norman knocked, Cameron opened the door, and the deputy shot him four times. Officials said that Deputy Norman identified himself when he knocked and that Cameron answered the door holding a BB gun, which was found at the scene. Cameron’s friends have said that they did not hear the deputy identify himself and that the boy was not holding the gun. Cameron was black, as is Deputy Norman.

    73. Ezell Ford
      Aug. 11, 2014 — Los Angeles

      Two officers shot and killed an unarmed man who they said had tried to take a gun from one of them. The man, Ezell Ford, 25, who was described by family and neighbors as mentally ill, was walking on a sidewalk when two officers in a police car stopped to question him. A report issued by the Los Angeles Police Department said Mr. Ford “continued walking and made suspicious movements, including attempting to conceal his hands.” When Officer Sharlton Wampler tried to detain him, Mr. Ford fought, forced the officer to the ground and tried to take his gun, officials said. Officer Wampler and Officer Antonio Villegas both fired at Mr. Ford, hitting him three times. The Police Department called the shooting justified, and neither officer was disciplined. But the city's civilian Police Commission found fault with Officer Wampler, saying he had no justification for detaining Mr. Ford. Mr. Ford was black; Officer Wampler is Asian-American, and Officer Villegas is Hispanic.
