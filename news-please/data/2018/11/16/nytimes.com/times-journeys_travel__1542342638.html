Journey to nine distinctive regions of Japan in its most beautiful season, cherry blossom time, as you learn about United States-Asian relations from a New York Times journalist and team of foreign affairs specialists. The many splendors of Japan will delight your senses, and your exclusive learning experience will delight your brain. This 15-day cruise aboard the Holland America Line’s recently reimagined Westerdam is a journey unlike others.

Spring in Japan is magical: peach blossoms near Tokyo, peonies in Osaka, camellias in Kochi, azaleas everywhere, and, of course, the ubiquitous cherries. Japan’s history and culture will draw your attention, and you will also get deep insight into regional international affairs with a Times journalists, experts, and scholars. A wide range of optional shore excursions, combined with exclusive access to seminars and receptions with your experts, combine the best of cruising on a midsize ship and the intimacy of a journey with like-minded travelers.

Your journey includes:

  • On Tour Team: Four New York Times Experts and two professional tour leaders
  • Accommodations: 14 nights onboard the Holland America Westerdam
  • Food: All food (excluding optional meals in the private restaurants), including 24-hour room service; tea, coffee, a half-dozen juices, and milk
  • Taxes and Fees: Port charges, government taxes and fees, non-refundable booking service fee, tour leader gratuities
  • Activities: Sixteen seminars delivered by TImes experts; eight receptions; use of an expansive, fully-equipped gym
  • Daily copy of The New York Times Digest
  • Every evening, Holland America provides top-flight Las Vegas-style entertainment in their large show lounge

Journey highlights include:

  • Attend sixteen exclusive seminars with a Times journalist and noted experts to learn about international and regional affairs, and mingle at exclusive receptions.
  • Sample many of Japan’s local crafts and cuisines, and marvel in the beauty of a country showing off its floral best.
  • Learn more about the complicated interplay among China, Japan, Russia, the Koreas and the United States from people deeply immersed in their pasts and presents.
  • Stop in Vladivostok, and see why it is sometimes more closely related to China and Japan than it is to Russia.
  • Combine your love of travel with your love of learning on a newly reimagined ship designed for 21st-century casual comfort.

Scheduled Experts

  • Susan Shirk

    Apr 14–28 departure
    Susan Shirk, Ph.D. is the Chair of the 21st Century China Center and Research Professor at the School of Global Policy and Strategy at the University of California - San Diego. She is also director emeritus of the University of California’s Institute on Global Conflict and Cooperation (IGCC). Susan Shirk first visited China in 1971 and has been teaching, researching, and engaging China diplomatically ever since.

  • Sheila Smith

    Apr 14–28 departure
    Sheila A. Smith, Ph.D., an expert on Japanese politics and foreign policy, is senior fellow for Japan studies at the Council on Foreign Relations (CFR). She is the author of Intimate Rivals: Japanese Domestic Politics and a Rising China (Columbia University Press, 2015) and Japan’s New Politics and the U.S.-Japan Alliance (Council on Foreign Relations, June 2014). Her current research focuses on how geostrategic change in Asia is shaping Japan’s strategic choices. In the fall of 2014, Smith began a project on Northeast Asian Nationalisms and Alliance Management.

  • Steven Lee Myers

    Apr 14–28 departure
    Steven Lee Myers is a veteran diplomatic and national security correspondent, now based in the Beijing bureau of The Times. In 2013 and 2014 he worked as The Times Moscow bureau chief. He previously served as a correspondent and bureau chief there from 2002 to 2007, covering Russia and the other former Soviet republics. He is the author of the biography of Vladimir Putin, entitled The New Tsar: The Rise and Reign of Vladimir Putin, published by Alfred A. Knopf Books in September 2015.  

  • Bonnie Glaser

    Apr 14–28 departure
    Bonnie S. Glaser is a senior adviser for Asia and the director of the China Power Project at CSIS, where she works on issues related to Asia-Pacific security with a focus on Chinese foreign and security policy. She is concomitantly a nonresident fellow with the Lowy Institute in Sydney, Australia, and a senior associate with the CSIS Pacific Forum. Ms. Glaser has worked for more than three decades at the intersection of Asia-Pacific geopolitics and U.S. policy.

Yokohama: Tokyo’s Neighbor

Arrive in Yokohama, about an hour outside Tokyo, in time to board the Westerdam. If you have time, wander the city, where East has met West for 150 years. Once on board, settle in and meet your fellow Times Journeys travelers and experts at an exclusive bon voyage cocktail party this evening.

Day 2

Shimizu: Mount Fuji and Friends

Spend about half the day in Shimizu. Among optional excursions, you can visit Nihondaira Park Outlook to take in views of Suruga Bay and magnificent Mount Fuji (weather permitting), and ride a cable car to the Toshogu Shrine, dedicated to Shogun Tokugawa Ieyasu (1543–1616), who brought peace to Japan after a long period of civil war. If you like your natural beauty virtual, the Tokaido Hiroshige Art Museum displays 19th-century woodblock landscapes called ukiyo-e prints, for a sampling of Japan’s abundance of signature countryside and seascapes. Back on the Westerdam, attend a reception.

Day 3

Osaka: A Cluster of Treasures

Osaka is new by Japanese standards, having been rebuilt after World War II, and you’ll have most of the day to explore it. You’ll be an hour from the ancient capitals of Kyoto and Nara, with their classic streetscapes, thousand-year-old temples and deep heritage as centers of art and literature. You can visit Nara and see the world’s largest statue of Buddha, or go to nearby Kyoto to see the Kinkaku-ji Temple, originally built in 1397 and faithfully reconstructed in 1955, down to the gold foil that honors its name, the Golden Pavilion. If you’re a brewing and distillery buff, you can head for nearby Fushimi, home to three-dozen of Japan’s most famous sake breweries.

Day 4

Kochi: Blessed With Nature

Kochi is called “Hometown, Japan,” for its low-key charms. The city has a strong historical legacy and craft heritage of tosa washi paper, which you can observe on an optional tour, but it also boasts beautiful natural scenes. You can explore one of eight “fine views” in Japan, Cape Muroto, jutting out over the Pacific Ocean. Its name means “blessed with great nature.” You might decide to visit Ryugado Cave on the slopes of Mount Sampo, or stay nearer to Kochi and visit its famed Kochi Castle, with commanding views of the city.

Day 5

Fukuoka: Traditional Craft

Many threads of Japanese culture, old and new, converge around Fukuoka. You have a long day here, and you can even have dinner on your own in town. Nearby towns offer local craft specialties, a scenic canal system, moss gardens and natural beauty. Japan is known for its bullet trains, and you can take one on a 20-minute ride to a 17th-century castle with its gardens (and many stairs). Or visit the Dazaifu shrine, dedicated to the ninth-century scholar Sugawara no Michizane, particularly popular with Japanese students hoping to do well on their exams. Or take a donko-bune, a gondola-like boat, to explore the canals around Yanagawa. Fukuoka itself offers urban bustle, a folk museum, baseball stadium and a Pokémon store.

Day 6

At Sea: Through the Genkai Sea

Settle in for a full day of exclusive sessions with your Times journalist and experts as the Westerdam sails the Genkai Sea, part of the Sea of Japan, known for its valuable fisheries. Among the possible topics are a look at the history of Chinese leadership and politics, how U.S.-Chinese relations have changed over the years, and how journalists operate in China, where surveillance is a given and detention a real possibility. Since you’re sailing close to the Korean Peninsula, you might also discuss the complicated relationships between the Koreas, China, Japan, Russia and the United States. At a reception, mingle with your experts for more insight.

Day 7

Kanazawa: Footsteps of Samurai

You’ll have a long day in Kanazawa, nestled between the Sea of Japan on the west and the Japanese Alps on the east. Its name means “marsh of gold” because, legend has it, a peasant digging for potatoes in the marsh discovered gold flakes. Kanazawa is also one of Japan’s few remaining castle towns, and you can explore the castle: Kenrokuen, one of the castle’s gardens, is considered to be one of Japan’s three finest. Kanazawa’s labyrinthine old town has a notable samurai district, and the adventuresome may want to make a reservation and tour the mysterious Temple of the Ninja (Myoryuji Temple) with its hidden rooms, trap doors and strategic architecture. The area is also known for its handicrafts, particularly kutani ware, delicately painted porcelains with intricate, vivid designs. Consider having dinner on your own in one of Kanazawa’s many restaurants.

Day 9

Vladivostok: Russia’s Rustic East

Vladivostok’s remoteness from most of Russia has not prevented it from being a strategic spot for Russia since the 19th century. Home of the Soviet Pacific fleet from 1958 to 1974, the city was literally closed to foreigners and nonresident Soviet citizens. Consider visiting the old-school natural history museum to learn about the Russian Far East’s peoples, folkways and flora and fauna. Wrap the day up by sampling the region’s local/crossroads cuisine for an authentic experience of Russia’s rustic Far East outpost. Keep your eye out for tigers: the Siberian tiger, also known as the Amur tiger, is Vladivostok’s official symbol, and it appears in statues, on buildings and occasionally even in person.

Perhaps you will hear how United States relations with China are evolving, or gain insight into what many are calling the “new Cold War,” as so many nations seem to be rattling their sabers.

Day 11

Otaru: Sushi Town

Sure, Japan is known for sushi, but Otaru is to sushi as New York or Chicago is to pizza. You could alternate exploring local seafood specialties with strolls along its famous canals, doubling back for more sushi sampling among its 100 sushi restaurants. Otaru also has a great music box museum (more than 3,400 boxes) with a studio where you can make your own. Otaru also has a reputation for glassmaking, and you can visit its oldest operation, Kitaichi Glass. You can also head to nearby Sapporo, the home of miso ramen: Ganso Sapporo Ramen Yokocho is an alley with many ramen shops. There’s also a Sapporo beer museum. Return to the Westerdam for an evening reception.

Day 12

Hakodate: Historic Portal to the West

Hakodate, on the island of Hokkaido, opened to Western visitors and commerce in 1859. It is one of the few places in Japan where a non-Japanese ethnic group, the Ainu, thrives. You can visit the Hakodate Morning Market, with about 280 stores, followed by a visit to the Goryokaku Tower for a view of its graceful pentagonal structure. If you’re in the mood for outdoor activity, you can take an hour’s drive to Onuma Quasi-National Park to cruise and hike along a trail winding among and across the small islands studding Lake Onuma. The Westerdam departs late, so maybe you’ll have dinner on our own in town.

Day 13

Aomori: Cherry Blossom Time

Aomori is at the northern tip of Japan’s largest island, and its beautiful forests, the Hakkoda Mountains and lakes draw outdoor enthusiasts. Though most of the city had to be rebuilt after World War II, you can tour its modern vibe plus some archaeological ruins nearby that date more than 4,000 years. Foodies know the city as the source of fabulous local tuna from the Tsuguru Strait, seafood soups and apple cider. Or take a short trip to Hirosake to view its castle, cherry blossoms and apple trees, and a nearby pagoda and assembly of 33 Zen temples.

Day 14

At Sea: Through the Tsugaru Strait

As the ship threads its way through the Tsugaru Strait, between Hokkaido and Honshu on its way back to Yokohama, spend your last day with your Times journalist and experts. Perhaps you’ll want to fill in some gaps, or learn more about territorial claims that have embroiled China, Japan and other Asian nations.
