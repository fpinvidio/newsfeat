Go deep into the CERN facilities in Geneva on this six-day tour, learning from expert researchers about the critical work being performed there, including the search for the elusive Higgs boson particle. Explore the history and culture of southwestern Switzerland, taking in the highlights of Geneva, the Observatoire de Genève, and the wine-producing region of Lavaux. The combination of Swiss science, heritage and cuisine make this exclusive trip an unforgettable one.

Geneva is a city of elegant buildings, financial power and international diplomacy as well as the site of CERN’s famous Large Hadron Collider. CERN, the European Organization for Nuclear Research, conducts high-energy particle physics research with the help of over 13,000 staff and scientists.

This unique tour includes the history and importance of the search for the Higgs boson, referred to by some as the “God particle.” CERN has other colliders as well, all designed to make particles collide at close to the speed of light, to give insights into the fundamental laws of nature. It is also here that the World Wide Web was first developed.

*CERN does not permit access to the Large Hadron Collider.

Day 2

Meyrin: Introduction to CERN

Visit the Geneva headquarters of CERN, established in 1954 as an international particle physics laboratory. Its experiments have resulted in many significant discoveries, and at least three Nobel Prizes.

Begin with an overview of its history and an introduction to particle physics. Take an optional walking tour of Geneva’s highlights, including the Flower Clock, Jet d’Eau and Palais des Nations, the European home of the United Nations.

Day 3

Meyrin: The Large Hadron Collider and Carouge

Return to CERN for an in-depth discussion of Large Hadron Collider experiments that take place in its 17-mile underground racetrack. The Large Hadron Collider is not the only collider at CERN, merely the newest and largest, and its track runs under both France and Switzerland.

Take an exclusive guided tour of Carouge, known as the Greenwich Village of Geneva. In the 18th century, Carouge — intended as a trading rival to Geneva — became a refuge for people who felt persecuted elsewhere in Switzerland, including Catholics, Protestants and Jews. Its architecture is reminiscent of its Sardinian roots. Visit the workshop of a local watchmaker for a closer look at one of Switzerland’s iconic industries. Continue to the chocolatier Philippe Pascoët, whose confections include passion fruit, black currant and saffron flavors.

Return to the hotel to freshen up, and depart on foot for dinner at a nearby restaurant serving traditional Swiss cuisine.

Day 4

Versoix: Observatoire de Genève and Lavaux

Travel to the village of Versoix to visit the Observatoire de Genève, where the first “exoplanet” was discovered in 1995, the first planet to be detected outside our solar system. The observatory’s Leonhard Euler telescope measures the Doppler effect of a star’s electromagnetic spectrum, to see if it is affected by nearby planets.

Discover the terraced vineyards of Lavaux with an official Unesco World Heritage guide, learning about the history of these vineyards, begun in the 11th century on the slopes above Lake Geneva. Enjoy a wine tasting before returning to Geneva for an evening at leisure.

Day 5

Meyrin: CERN and the Birth of the Internet

This morning, visit CERN a final time to explore the links between particle physics and cosmology, then view the world’s first web server. The web began as a project called Enquire, initiated by Tim Berners-Lee in 1980 as a way all the computers at CERN could share information. Berners-Lee and Robert Cailliau refined the idea in 1989 and 1990, and the web was made available to the public in 1993.

After lunch at CERN, take part in a final Q&A session with CERN scientists. Return to Geneva for some time at leisure to relax or explore. This evening, enjoy a festive farewell dinner.
