For more than 2,000 years, travelers have come to Egypt seeking traces of the powerful dynasties swept away by the sands of time. On this eight-day journey into the past, including a cruise on the mighty Nile, New York Times experts will help you piece together the life and times of a mighty ancient civilization and share the vision for the country’s future.

Egypt has long held an important place in the world, first as a cradle of civilization, and more recently for its on again, off again alliances with the United States and other Western nations. Visit its ancient sites, including the tombs of many of its kings and pharaohs, and see how it rescued its past from the modernization that the Aswan Dam project in the 1960s threatened. Gain insight with special talks and exclusive access to experts and experiences to help put this enigmatic nation into perspective, even as you enjoy modern amenities and luxury accommodations.

During the trip, your expert’s lecture topics may include:

  • Egypt and Israeli/Palestinian Peace: Vignettes from what was once called the Peace Process
  • Egypt’s Economic Challenges and Promise: it’s all about Human Development
  • Egypt and the United States: A legacy relationship in search of a purpose
  • How did the Arab Spring unfurl in Egypt and what does it mean today?

Day 2

The Egyptian Museum and the Pyramids

This morning, meet your Times expert, your fellow Times travelers and your Egyptologist for a tour of the Egyptian Museum. This museum highlights 7,000 years of history, with the largest collection of ancient Egyptian artifacts in the world. Don’t miss the treasures of the “boy-king” Tutankhamen, including his gold mask, the solid gold sarcophagus that held his mummy and his beautiful throne.

After a traditional lunch at a local restaurant, explore the Pyramids of Giza and the Sphinx. The Great Pyramids of Giza are the only survivors of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World, built about 4,500 years ago as giant tombs for the mummies of the pharaohs Khufu, Khafre and Menkaure. In the ancient Egyptian religion, the sides of the pyramids were likened to sunrays on which the pharaoh ascended to heaven. The pyramids are monumental in scale, with the largest, Khufu’s, made of over two million blocks. Despite what you may have heard, the pyramids were not built by slaves but by Egyptian peasants who paid their taxes to the pharaoh through this labor, and were fed, clothed and housed by him.

A few at a time, you can go inside one of the pyramids to get a sense of how they were built and what they are like from the inside.

Nearby sits the enigmatic Sphinx with the body of a lion and the face of a man wearing a royal head cloth, which workers may have based on King Khafre to guard his enormous funerary monument. About a thousand years after the Sphinx was built, it was covered in sand until a young prince had a dream in which the Sphinx told him that if he cleared the sand away, he would become pharaoh. This story is told on the “Dream Stela” that was placed between the Sphinx’s paws by King Thutmose IV.

Tonight, gather with your fellow travelers for cocktails and a welcome dinner with an opportunity to talk to a local expert about the country’s recent history and Egypt’s vision for the future regarding development and human rights.

Day 3

Rescued Temples in Abu Simbel, Aswan and the Nile

Fly to Abu Simbel, site of the magnificent temple of Ramses II. There are in fact two temples: the Great Temple of Ra-Harakhte, fronted by the four colossal statues of Ramses II, and the Temple of Hathor, dedicated to Ramses’ beloved wife Queen Nefertari. Impressive in themselves, these temples are further remarkable in that they were cut into pieces, raised up the hillside from their old location and then reassembled around a concrete dome on the current site when they were threatened by floodwaters from Lake Nasser and the Aswan High Dam project.

Later, fly to Aswan and transfer to your Nile cruise ship, Sanctuary Sun Boat IV. After lunch onboard, your Egyptologist will take you to the granite quarries that supplied the ancient Egyptians with most of the hard stone used in pyramids and temples. Here you can see the enormous Unfinished Obelisk, which weighs 1,200 tons and is a fine example of the extraordinary skills and techniques used by the ancient Egyptian stonemasons in producing these enormous monoliths.

This evening, cocktails and canapés will be served before dinner.

Day 4

Gods, Gold and Crocodiles

This morning, take a short boat ride to the exquisite Temple of Philae on the island of Agilika. Moved from the island of Philae before it was submerged by the waters from the Aswan Dam in the 1960s, the temple was reconstructed stone by stone on Agilika and is a Unesco World Heritage site. It was dedicated to the goddess Isis and decorated with the image of her alter ego, Hathor, and is one of the last strongholds of the ancient Egyptian religion.

Return to Sanctuary Sun Boat IV for a cooking lesson from your onboard chef. If you choose not to cook, you can relax and enjoy the lovely scenery on your way to Kom Ombo (“hill of gold”). Visit the site of a lovely Greco-Roman temple, dedicated to Haroeris (a sky god) and Sobek (the crocodile god). The temple, built in the third century B.C., stands at a bend in the Nile where, in ancient times, sacred crocodiles basked in the sun on the riverbank. Several of their mummies are on display here.

Return to your yacht in time for afternoon tea as you sail for Edfu. Visit the beautifully preserved Temple of Horus, built in 230 B.C. by Ptolemy III. This well-preserved structure makes it easy to imagine what temple worship in ancient Egypt would have been like.

Back on board, dinner will be an Egyptian Night costume party for all guests, with a chance to dress up in traditional Egyptian galabeyas. Dinner features Egyptian specialties, followed by Arabic music and dancing for everyone. Dock in Esna.

Day 5

The Greco-Roman Influence

This morning explore one of the largest and most completely preserved Pharaonic temples in Egypt — albeit Greek-built: the extraordinary Temple of Horus at Edfu. Started in 230 B.C. by Ptolemy III and finished by Ptolemy XII in 57 B.C., this well-preserved structure makes it easy to imagine what temple worship in ancient Egypt would have been like.

Return to the boat and continue to Esna. This afternoon your Egyptologist will guide you round the Greco-Roman Temple of Khnum at Esna. The beautifully preserved Great Hypostyle Hall was built during the reign of the Roman Emperor Claudius; it was excavated from the silt that had accumulated through centuries of annual Nile floods and is about 29 feet below present-day street level. Return to the Sun Boat IV and continue toward Luxor.

Day 6

Luxor and the Valley of the Kings

Tour the east bank of Luxor, starting with the strikingly graceful Temple of Luxor, built by Amenhotep III, Tutankhamen, Horemheb and Ramses II. Along the front of the temple pylons, three of six large statues of Ramses II remain, while behind the East Tower, inside the Courtyard of Ramses II, is a mosque built over a portion of the temple. The Colonnade of Amenhotep III contains 14 papyrus-headed columns, some 50 feet in height and 32 feet in circumference, leading to the Court of Amenhotep III, also surrounded by gigantic columns.

Proceed to the Temple of Karnak, a huge complex 2,000 years in the making. It is here that nearly all the pharaohs wished to record their names and deeds for posterity. Karnak Temple is a temple within a temple, a shrine within a shrine. The Karnak Temple, the Seat of Amun, the king of the gods, is focused on the awesome Hypostyle Hall. Covering 50,000 square feet (an area larger than the whole of Notre Dame Cathedral) are 134 great columns that once supported an enormous roof, an extraordinary engineering feat for the 12th century B.C. There are also superb statues of Ramses II and Tutankhamun, in addition to the fine Obelisks of Queen Hatshepsut and Thutmosis I.

In the afternoon cross the Nile to the west bank and tour the imposing Valley of the Kings, the Necropolis of Thebes and a vast city of the dead and the burial ground of the pharaohs of the New Kingdom. Over 60 tombs have been discovered here to date and the hieroglyphs and paintings that cover their walls are as vivid as if they were painted yesterday.

Explore the tomb of Tutankhamen, rediscovered in 1922 with many of its riches (and King Tut himself) still intact. Then meet an archaeologist to learn about the recent excavations on Luxor’s west bank.

Rising out of the desert plain in a series of terraces, the Temple of Queen Hatshepsut (ancient Egypt's first female pharaoh) merges with the sheer limestone cliffs that surround it. Explore the temple and learn about Hatshepsut’s reign as a woman ruling over the powerful empire of Egypt and her successful expedition to the land of Punt (current day Somalia), depicted on the temple’s walls.

On the way back to the river Nile, pass the famed Colossi of Memnon, known in ancient Greek times for their haunting voices at dawn.

Tonight’s farewell dinner on board will be followed by a belly dancer.

Day 7

Historical Cairo, Mosques and Bazaars

After breakfast bid farewell to your Nile cruise ship’s crew and fly back to Cairo. Drive to the heart of Islamic Cairo and the Mosque of Sultan Hassan. With its grand, soaring architecture and rich decoration, the mosque, completed in 1363, is one of the finest in Egypt. In recesses around its open courtyard, each of the four schools of Sunni Islam was taught to students, and in its mausoleum, Sultan Hassan’s sons are buried.

Then tour a residential neighborhood in Islamic Cairo, from Bab al-Futuh, where locals shop, to Khan el-Khalili bazaar. This unique tour takes you from one end to the other of the ancient thoroughfare of the Khan el-Khalili, once the heart of the old walled city of Cairo. The street itself, which leads you along some of the most traditional parts of the bazaar where life has hardly changed for centuries, runs from the massive fortified Futuh Gate to the beautifully designed Bab Zuweila gate, and has more listed buildings per meter than any other stretch of road in the world.

This part of the bazaar, where Egyptians go to bargain for everyday items, will thrill your senses, and you can see and learn about the licorice-tea sellers, spice markets with obscure roots and magical powders, black-swathed women, wicker baskets of birds and brightly painted donkey carts full of watermelon and mangoes.

Tonight gather with your fellow Times Journeys travelers for a farewell cocktail reception. Dinner is on your own.
