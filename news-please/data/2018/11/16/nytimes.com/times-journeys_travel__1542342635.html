During World War ll, German forces operated a vast system to seize Jewish art collections and other works essentially from private owners in occupied France. Spend six days in Paris exploring the systematic plunder of art that offers a universal lesson today about international conflicts. With a New York Times expert, hear the stories these looted works have to tell.

In war, art theft is about dominance and destruction of identity. Almost 70,000 homes of Jews and French war resistors were emptied during World War II — 38,000 of them in Paris alone. Repatriated after the war, about 2,000 works are scattered among French museums, unclaimed until the descendants of the owners can resurrect family history and documents to make legal claims. See these museums and their works in a new light, through the eyes of their proud prewar owners, with exclusive access and insightful commentary from The New York Times.

Day 2

A Looted Renoir and a Restricted Auction House

This morning, take a guided tour of the Musée Nissim de Camondo. Through World War I and II, the family that inhabited the sumptuous manor confronted dramas, scandal and tragedy. A looted Renoir painting of a young girl — the lady of the house — hung in an opulent salon there and became a trading chip in the organized network of looting, swapping and selling between France, Germany and Switzerland.

This Rue de Monceau neighborhood was the hub of 19th-century banking families and business magnates, including the Ephrussi family, wealthy bankers whose lives were upended in the war. Edmund de Waal described the Ephrussis’ history and lost art legacy in “The Hare with Amber Eyes.”

Stop for lunch on your own before visiting the Drouot auction house. Tour the Drouot (with a quick lesson in bidding technique), a fabled institution that was the international hub of art commerce during the war and has a dark history of its own when Jews were banned from entering. Today more than 5,000 people enter daily for auctions in 16 showrooms, with items on sale ranging from art and the silver of a Russian princess to vintage Hermès scarves and stuffed owls. The evening is on your own.

Day 3

Impressionism and Pissarro on the Oise

The green River Oise was inspiration for Impressionist and post-Impressionist artists who followed the train line from Paris to a region of luminous light. Chase the same inspirations on a boat tour of the river that inspired many of Camille Pissarro’s landscapes. Sail from Pontoise to Auvers-sur-Oise and back while indulging in a sparkling aperitif, snacks and a guided commentary focusing on Impressionism. Then visit a small museum beyond the city’s medieval ramparts that is devoted to Pissarro. Pissarro descended from a family of Dutch Jews in the Antilles, yet his works were much coveted by German officers. Most recently a Pissarro landscape was found in the Gurlitt stash in Germany and was returned to a French family that had been looking for it for almost 70 years. Another Pissarro cityscape of Paris is part of a tug-of-war between the Thyssen-Bornemisza Museum in Madrid and Los Angeles descendants of a woman forced to give up the painting in 1939 for $360 in exchange for exit visas to flee Nazi Germany for England. Return to your hotel for an evening at leisure.

Day 4

A War Heroine and Orphaned Art

Tour the Jeu de Paume to trace the footsteps of Rose Valland, the unlikely French heroine who helped saved France’s art patrimony as an assistant curator at this museum during the war. The Nazis used the museum as a storage center for stolen art, and the Louvre posted Valland there as its spy. Unaware that Valland spoke German, military officers spoke openly in front of her while she made notes and gathered critical information about the movement of art, which she relayed to the French resistance.

Stop for lunch on your own before going to the Musée d’Orsay for a tour focusing on the orphaned art that is exhibited there, awaiting the descendants of the owners of the art to make claims. More than 2,000 such orphaned works are scattered among French museums, among them works by Courbet, Renoir, Degas and Dürer. They are all marked M.N.R. for Musées Nationaux Récupération, meaning it was recovered from the Nazis and allocated to museums. After the tour the rest of the day is yours.

Day 5

The Left Bank and Returned Art

Take a walking tour of the Left Bank with a local expert. During the war the creations of artists took a dark turn. When the German army occupied France in 1939, Picasso remained defiant despite being the leading “degenerate” artist in the world. German officers called at his Left Bank studio and interrogated him about his activities and rumors that he was Jewish. But mysteriously he was never arrested. He protested the Nazi bombing of a Basque town with his mural-sized oil painting “Guernica,” created at his home on Rue des Grands Augustins. Later, in the 1940s, a Nazi officer visited Picasso’s studio. “Did you do that?” he supposedly asked while studying a photograph of the painting. “No,” Picasso replied. “You did.”

Have lunch at the Hôtel Lutetia. This early-20th-century hotel was the headquarters for the Abwehr, the German military intelligence service. Later it was a refuge for returning concentration camp prisoners, and it remains an old soul of the Left Bank. Alain Monteagle, a retired French schoolteacher, will tell you about his successful efforts to recover his family’s artworks that had been sold in a forced sale in Nice. Working alone as an amateur sleuth, he has recovered almost a dozen paintings, including some from the Louvre and the Kimbell Art Museum in Texas.

The rest of the day is on your own, followed by a cocktail reception and farewell dinner as a group at a traditional restaurant.
