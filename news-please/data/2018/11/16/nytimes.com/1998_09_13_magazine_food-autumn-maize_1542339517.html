    Food; Autumn Maize

    By MOLLY O'NEILLSEPT. 13, 1998

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    Corn is a way of telling time. its different incarnations, from fresh to dried to ground, correspond not only to nature's imperative but to the seasonality of human appetite as well.

    The flavor of fresh corn is like a milky sun on a summer morning -- gentle, buttery, light, full of possibility. When sweet corn is dried, it delivers nearly the same flavor. But rare is the farmer still willing to dry and rehydrate it. Today it is far more economical to grow starchier corn for drying and milling. It's as if the god of commerce cut a deal with the god of light.

    When corn is dried and ground and then cooked with water to make a mush (or its more cosmopolitan cousin, polenta), its flavor still retains a hint of summer, although the predominant taste is nutty. It is the same flavor that gives solace to humans as well as to squirrels. A nut in winter indemnifies. It also announces the triumph of effort, the benefit of hoarding. A humble cornmeal porridge tastes like a safe childhood.

    When baked -- in bread, muffins, griddle cakes or even spoon bread -- cornmeal assumes a huskier flavor, a flavor that barely echoes a ripe cornfield. The meal's sweet tones get a roasted, faintly caramelized character, which, in turn, is both exaggerated and mitigated by the sugar that is typically used when making corn bread, muffins, sticks or pone.

    Of course, the taste of corn bread owes as much to human ingenuity as it does to fire. Indeed, the more that corn is processed -- and with the passing of each week after harvest, the more necessary it is to dry or otherwise preserve it -- the less its flavor will reflect a capricious weather god.

    When dried corn is treated with an alkali like wood ash, lime or lye, it is called hominy or slaked corn. Hominy tastes something like popcorn. Its nutty, buttery, starchy comfort is as distantly related to fresh corn as corn is to cornflakes.

    When the hominy is cracked into a coarse meal, it becomes hominy grits, a favorite breakfast food in the Deep South. When it is ground fine, it becomes masa harina, the flour used to make tortillas or occasionally to thicken soups. And when it is made from white corn and reconstituted with water, it is called posole, which is also the name of a hearty hominy-rich stew that's popular in the Southwest. You can also turn hominy into a snack -- fry it with corn and salt it to make the ''nuts'' in the recipe that follows.

    So in other words, when corn is ground into its most versatile and long-lasting form, it adds a kind of solidity and substance to a dish that only age can provide. Hominy has the warmth of wisdom. Which should be enough to get you through until the next season.

    Acorn Squash With Polenta Souffle And Pulled Pork

    (Adapted from the Cookhouse, New Milford, Conn.)

    For the pulled pork:

    2 tablespoons mild paprika

    1 tablespoon black pepper

    1 tablespoon chili powder

    1/2 tablespoon garlic powder

    1/2 tablespoon cumin powder

    1/2 tablespoon sugar

    1 tablespoon salt

    1 Boston butt (pork-shoulder roast with bone, 6 to 7 pounds)

    2 cups hickory, apple or cherry wood chips

    For the acorn squash:

    6 small acorn squash

    4 tablespoons butter

    For the polenta souffle:

    4 tablespoons butter

    1/4 cup masa harina (corn flour)

    1/4 cup yellow cornmeal

    Advertisement

    Continue reading the main story

    1/2 cup milk

    3 eggs, separated

    1 teaspoon salt

    1 teaspoon freshly ground pepper.

    1. To make the pulled pork, combine the spices and sugar, rub them all over the pork roast and refrigerate overnight.

    2. Soak the wood chips in water 20 minutes before cooking. When ready to cook, partly fill a metal bowl with water. In the bottom of a charcoal grill, surround the bowl with charcoals. Ignite the coals, and when they are white, place the roast, fat side up, over the bowl and add some wood chips to the fire. Cook, covered, 4 to 6 hours, adding wood chips and charcoal every hour as needed, until the roast has reached an internal temperature of 185 degrees. Allow it to cool for an hour before removing the fat and pulling the pork into pieces.

    3. Meanwhile, make the acorn squash. Preheat oven to 350 degrees. Slice off the tops of the squash and enough off the bottoms to make them sit flat. Scoop out the seeds and rub about 2 teaspoons

    of butter inside each. Roast for 40 minutes and keep warm.

    4. To make the polenta souffle, melt the butter in a medium pan. Stir in the corn flour and cornmeal and cook over medium heat for 2 minutes. Mix in the milk and 1 cup water and cook, stirring, 5 minutes.

    5. Remove from heat. Beat 2 tablespoons water into egg yolks, then stir thoroughly into the flour mixture. Add the salt and pepper.

    6. Meanwhile, whisk the egg whites until they form stiff but not dry peaks; gently mix them into the polenta, using a wooden spoon.

    7. Place about cup of the pulled pork in each squash, and over that, spoon in 1/4 to 1/2 cup of the souffle mixture. Bake until the souffle has become puffy and brown, 30 to 40 minutes. Serve immediately alongside additional pulled pork.

    Yield: 6 servings.

    1 teaspoon ground coriander

    2 tablespoons tomato paste

    1/2 cup pine nuts.

    1. To make the pudding, preheat oven to 300 degrees. Heat the milk in a saucepan over medium heat until very hot. In the top of a double boiler that has been placed over boiling water, add the cornmeal and very slowly whisk in the milk. Cook, stirring, 15 minutes. Stir in the remaining pudding ingredients.

    2. To make the stuffing, combine all the ingredients. Line the bottom of an 8- by 8-inch cake pan with parchment paper and spread the stuffing evenly. Spread the pudding over the stuffing, and bake until set, 2 1/2 to 3 hours. Slice and serve.

    Yield: 8 servings.

    Hominy Nuts

    1 pint corn oil

    1 pound dry hominy, washed and thoroughly dried

    Salt

    Lime juice (optional).

    Heat the oil in a skillet to 375 degrees. Fry the hominy in 1/2-cup batches until golden, about 1 to 2 minutes per batch. Remove from the skillet and drain on paper towels. Add salt to taste and lime juice if desired. Allow to cool.

    Yield: Serves 6 as a snack.

    Cornmeal Beignets With Roasted Apples

    1/2 teaspoon cinnamon

    2 tablespoons unsalted butter, softened

    2 Granny Smith apples, peeled and cored

    For the beignet batter:

    1/4 cup milk

    6 tablespoons unsalted butter

    1/2 cup maple syrup

    1/2 teaspoon salt

    Advertisement

    Continue reading the main story

    1 1/2 to 1 2/3 cups masa harina (corn flour)

    5 eggs

    3 cups corn oil

    Maple sugar or additional syrup.

    1. Preheat oven to 350 degrees. Knead the cinnamon into the butter and rub the mixture all over the apples. In a cake pan, bake the apples 45 minutes and remove. When cool enough to handle, chop enough of them to make 1 cup.

    2. Meanwhile, make the batter. Combine 1/2 cup water with the milk, butter, syrup and salt in a saucepan. Bring to a boil and cook over medium heat until the butter melts. Stir in the corn flour and continue to stir until the dough forms a ball, 1 to 2 minutes.

    3. Remove the dough to a mixing bowl and mix in the eggs, one at a time. Beat until the dough is shiny, about 10 minutes.

    4. Heat the oil in a skillet or fryer to 375 degrees. Drop the beignets in, 1 heaping tablespoon at a time, and fry until golden, turning once, 2 to 3 minutes. Top with maple sugar or drizzle with additional syrup.

    Yield: About 30 beignets.

    Veal-Shank Posole

    For the soup:

    1/2 pound bacon, chopped

    2 medium carrots, chopped

    5 cloves garlic, chopped

    1 medium onion, chopped

    1 rib celery, chopped

    Salt and freshly ground black pepper

    3 pounds veal shanks, sliced into 1 1/2-inch-thick disks

    1/3 cup cornmeal

    4 quarts chicken stock

    2 1-quart cans hominy

    For the garnish:

    Raw corn cut from 2 ears

    4 ripe avocados, sliced

    1 head iceberg lettuce, chopped

    1 cup chopped cilantro

    4 to 8 jalapeno chilies, thinly sliced

    Cayenne pepper to taste.

    Advertisement

    Continue reading the main story

    1. For the soup, fry the bacon in a large pot over medium heat 3 minutes, then add the carrots, garlic, onion and celery. Cook, stirring until softened, about 5 minutes.

    2. Salt and pepper the veal to taste, then dust with cornmeal. With a slotted spoon, remove the vegetables and set them aside. Add the meat and brown on all sides, about 10 minutes.

    3. Return the vegetables to the pot along with a quart of water, the stock and hominy, including the liquid from one of the cans. Bring to a boil over high heat. Simmer and reduce for about 2 hours.

    4. For the garnish, toast the corn in a dry cast-iron skillet over medium heat for 5 minutes. Mound the corn in a large serving platter surrounded by the avocados, lettuce, cilantro and chilies.

    5. When veal is tender, remove it from heat and let cool. Meanwhile, skim the fat from the soup. When meat is cool enough to handle, separate it from the bones; reserve marrow. Chop meat into chunks and return it and marrow to the pot. Adjust seasoning and serve in bowls. Heap each bowl with the garnish.

    Yield: 8 servings.
