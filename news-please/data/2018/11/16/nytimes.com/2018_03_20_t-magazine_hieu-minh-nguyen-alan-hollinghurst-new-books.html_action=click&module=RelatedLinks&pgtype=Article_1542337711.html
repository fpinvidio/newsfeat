Inside the Season’s New Books

What happens on Page 76 in four of this season’s books, as envisioned by the artist Sam McKinniss.

Not Here
Poems by Hieu Minh Nguyen

Who needs a good reason to leave the party before things get weird? So maybe that’s hope. Maybe hope is stopping the story before it’s over, before the inevitable messy end. O monger of the broken records. O monger of the early birthday present.

Throughout this collection, the queer Vietnamese-American poet examines how race and family, trauma and longing have shaped his (often conflicted) sense of self: What is the cost of loving, he wonders — of living, even? In “Notes on Staying,” quoted above, he seeks that which, in spite of it all, continues to “[propel] us through the door of tomorrow.” (In this case the selected passage appears on page 73.) To be published by Coffee House Press on April 10.

The Sparsholt Affair
A novel by Alan Hollinghurst

‘Well …’ said Evert, astonished, and grateful, but caught at once in the maze of impossible replies. David’s own beauty was the unspoken context, and of course his incalculable modesty and vanity shaded any such compliment. ‘As I say, my mother’s very pretty,’ he said.

‘There,’ said David, almost reproachfully, and for the first time, miraculously, he blushed.

For a close-knit group of male friends at Oxford in 1940, where the war is rarely out of mind, a handsome newcomer named David Sparsholt becomes a pleasantly distracting object of fixation. But while many things seem to come easily to Sparsholt, he is unable to live as he pleases — something that, only decades later in a changed England, is altogether more feasible for his painter son. Hollinghurst’s novel is a history of postwar Britain told through a queer lens. Published by Knopf on March 13.

Circe
A novel by Madeline Miller

He left and I considered the tapestry again. Its groom was goggle-eyed, the bride buried in her veils, and behind them the family gaped like idiots. I had always hated it. Let it stay and rot.

When she is born, Circe’s nymph mother (Perse) hopes she might someday attract a son of Zeus, but she is destined for more than a mere marriage plot. Banished to the island Aiaia, she takes up potion-making — most famously turning Odysseus’ crew into swine — but this telling, in the sorceress’s own words, is not the version we think we know. To be published by Little, Brown and Company on April 10.

The Chandelier
A novel by Clarice Lispector (translated by Benjamin Moser and Magdalena Edwards)

“What had existed in her life was an indistinct and infinite power, really infinite and pallid. But she could never have demonstrated the existence of that power as it would be difficult to prove that she felt like going on, that the color of the rose was pleasing to her, that she was feeling strength, that she was connected to the stone in the garden. What had existed in her life, untouched and never lived, had raised her through the world like the bubble that rises.”

Originally published in 1946, Lispector’s second novel follows Virginia, who as a child shapes little forms out of river clay. In time, she and her beloved brother, Daniel, move to the city, where Virginia falls in with an artistic crowd but remains deeply solitary, most comfortable in the company of her own meandering thoughts. To be published by New Directions on March 27.

About the artist: Sam McKinniss studied at N.Y.U. and is based in New York. His paintings, which focus on figuration, draw from popular culture and explore themes of romance, tragedy and celebrity. He is represented by Team Gallery, whose Venice Beach, L.A., location showed a solo exhibition of his work, ‘‘Daisy Chain,’’ earlier this year. This month, McKinniss’s work can be seen in the group exhibition ‘‘The Lure of the Dark: Contemporary Painters Conjure the Night’’ at MASS MoCA in North Adams, Mass., and at Art Basel Hong Kong.
