    10. Metro Briefing | New York: Bronx: Man Dies In Shooting

      An 18-year-old Bronx man was fatally shot about 5 p.m. yesterday at the Melrose Houses in the South Bronx, the police said. The man, Carlos Arauz, of East 156th Street, was taken to Lincoln Medical and Mental Health Center, where he was pronounced dead upon arrival. Two men wearing red bandannas were seen fleeing the shooting. No arrests had been made last night. JENNIFER 8. LEE (NYT)
      Dec. 30, 2005

    2. Metro Briefing | New York: Albany: Phase Of Adirondack Land Deal Is Complete

      The first phase of a deal preserving more than a quarter-million acres in the heart of the Adirondack Mountains is complete, state officials announced yesterday. The state's Department of Environmental Conservation will pay the property's owner, International Paper, about $5.5 million in exchange for public access rights for more than 41,500 acres in Hamilton and Franklin Counties. Under the agreement, International Paper will retain the right to harvest wood, with some limitations. The announcement closes the first of three agreements in the largest land protection deal in state history. Gov. George E. Pataki first announced the plan in April 2004. The terms of the two remaining phases are expected to be completed in 2006. JENNIFER MEDINA (NYT)
      Dec. 30, 2005

    3. Metro Briefing | New York: Bronx: Teenager Attacked On Roof

      A 16-year-old Bronx girl was sexually assaulted at knifepoint yesterday evening on the roof of her apartment building and then tied to a satellite dish, the police said. The girl was approached by a man in his late 20's about 5:30 p.m. as she was entering her apartment, on Aqueduct Avenue near Bronx Community College. After the attack, the man took her jewelry. The girl freed herself and was taken by her mother to North Central Bronx Hospital, where she was in stable condition last night. The man was described as Hispanic, with a medium build, and wearing a white baseball cap, light blue jeans and a light-colored jacket with a dark, hooded sweatshirt underneath. The police said he was seen on videotape. JENNIFER 8. LEE (NYT)
      Dec. 30, 2005

    4. Metro Briefing | New Jersey: Springfield: Woman Killed By Truck

      A 26-year-old woman from Haledon, N.J., was killed on Route 78 when a truck changed lanes and ran over her sports car yesterday, the police said. The woman, Carrie A. Johansson, was taken to Overlook Hospital in Summit, where she died of severe internal injuries at 4:39 p.m., about an hour after the accident. Charges against the driver, James H. Porter, 67, of Alpha, Ohio, are pending. For three hours, only lane of traffic was open as the accident was being investigated. Jennifer 8. Lee (NYT)
      Dec. 29, 2005

    5. Smell Was Noticed, Not Neighbor's Absence

      Strikingly handsome and tall, James Fallon, 87, had once caught the eye of many of his elderly neighbors in the Plaza Tower, a luxury high-rise in Midtown. But he and his wife, Carole, kept mostly to themselves. So no one grew curious or concerned when he was no longer seen in the building's hallways or elevators. The neighbors did notice a rancid odor coming from the couple's 11th-floor co-op -- ''fish gone bad'' was how one described it. The smell crept so high in the building, at 118 East 60th Street, that it reached a woman on the 27th floor who said she thought someone had spilled garbage in the incinerator room across the hall.
      Dec. 28, 2005

    6. Firefighter on Bicycle Hurt In Crash With Charter Bus

      An off-duty New York City firefighter was critically injured yesterday when the driver of a charter bus hired to carry workers stranded by the transit strike slammed into him at a Midtown intersection as he was bicycling to work, police and fire officials said. The firefighter, Matthew Long, 39, was going north in the right lane of Third Avenue when the bus driver, who was also traveling north on Third Avenue, turned right onto 52nd Street and struck Mr. Long, dragging him under the bus, officials said.
      Dec. 23, 2005

    7. TRANSIT STRIKE ENDS: OVERVIEW

      TRANSIT STRIKE ENDS ON 3RD DAY; FRAMEWORK FOR DEAL REACHED

      Thousands of New York City transit workers put down their picket signs and streamed into bus depots and railyards last night to restart the nation's largest transit system, after leaders of their union agreed to a tentative framework for a new contract and ended a 60-hour strike that hobbled the city. After three frenetic and frustrating days of carpooling, biking, roller skating and trudging to work in chilly temperatures, New Yorkers reacted joyfully to the news that the city's sprawling network of subways and buses would soon be running again.
      Dec. 23, 2005

    8. TRANSIT STRIKE: PICKETS

      Monetary Losses Starting To Be Felt by Workers

      Early on Dec. 12, two days after bus and subway workers voted to authorize a strike, Muriel Scott borrowed $2,500 from her 401(k) plan and set it aside in a savings account. With her husband sick at home, a mortgage to pay, Christmas gifts to buy and two teenage daughters to raise, Mrs. Scott, a bus driver, felt she needed some cushion in case transit employees staged a walkout. ''That money isn't much, but I'm glad that I have it,'' Mrs. Scott, 50, said yesterday at the picket line outside the Michael J. Quill bus depot on 11th Avenue and 41st Street. ''I'm just scared to death because I don't know what might happen if this strike drags on for a while.''
      Dec. 22, 2005

    9. TRANSIT STRIKE: DAY 2

      Tolls Start to Mount: Financial, Medical, Emotional

      For a day, maybe, it felt like a communal challenge, a dare to New Yorkers to demonstrate their pluck and ingenuity. But by yesterday, the reality of a transit strike had wiped away any sense that this was an adventure. It was an annoyance, a hardship, a source of suffering. Every apartment block, office, store or sidewalk had its tales of people who were unable to get to work, of businesses that had trouble functioning or were able to operate only at a daunting cost, of workers and employers who reached their jobs and found there had been little point in trying because the customers were missing.
      Dec. 22, 2005

    10. TRANSIT STRIKE: OVERVIEW

      STAKES CLIMB ON DAY 2 OF STRIKE; JAIL THREAT FOR UNION LEADERS

      As New York City plunged into Day 2 of the transit strike, the stakes for those on the picket line, businesses and riders spiraled upward yesterday: Gov. George E. Pataki said that there would be no negotiations until the strikers return to work; a judge raised the possibility of sending union leaders to jail; businesses languished and weary New Yorkers grappled with the harsh realities of life without buses or subways. The war of rhetoric surrounding the strike entered a louder and more contentious phase, with the head of the transit union demanding that thorny pension issues be removed from the table before the strikers returned to work. But Governor Pataki joined Mayor Michael R. Bloomberg in saying that the transit workers must end the strike before negotiations could resume, contradicting the Metropolitan Transportation Authority's earlier position that it would talk anytime. The authority took the highly unusual step of running television advertisements urging individual workers to return to their jobs.
      Dec. 22, 2005

    11. An Actor, a Thief and a Shared Path Downhill

      The first time that young Lillo Brancato Jr. is remembered setting foot on the quiet street of homes on Arnow Place in the Bronx was around 1993. Then, he was a cocksure teenager starring opposite his big-screen idol, Robert De Niro, in ''A Bronx Tale.'' He and a 9-year-old actor, Francis Capra, played the same character at different stages of his life in the film, and Francis lived on Arnow Place. On the film's set, Mr. Brancato met Francis's older sister, Chanel, and courted her, thereby becoming a regular visitor to the block.
      Dec. 16, 2005
