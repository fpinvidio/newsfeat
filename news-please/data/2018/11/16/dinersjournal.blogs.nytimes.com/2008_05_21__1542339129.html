    Ed’s Rant on Ramps

    By Edward Schneider
    May 21, 2008 3:37 pm May 21, 2008 3:37 pm
    A porgy fillet with ramp and mushroom hash (Edward Schneider)

    Regular Bitten contributor Ed Schneider found ramps the other day — often the first real sign of spring here in New York — and was off and running. –MB

    Last week, for the first time this year, there were ramps in our little farmers‚Äô market. I love to cook ramps, not so much because of the bulbs (which cook very much like spring onions or scallions) but because of the greens, which collapse to a fraction of their original size even faster than young spinach leaves. It‚Äôs fun to watch, a bit like one of those pop-up sponges in reverse. And, of course, ramps, tops and bottoms alike, are delicious, with a delicate onion taste.

    On my new theory that any seasonal ingredients go together, it would have been lovely to cook these with morels, but getting fresh ones would have involved overcoming my lethargy and leaving the neighborhood, while using the dried kind didn‚Äôt seem quite right for my first ramps of spring. But I‚Äôd gotten myself fixated on fungi, so I just bought a package of regular white mushrooms from the supermarket. After staring at these for a while, I made crisp porgy fillets with ramp and mushroom hash. The ramps and mushrooms and the polenta would have made a nice dinner on their own, by the way, so you can forget about the fish if you like.

    First start the hash, much of which you can do an hour in advance: for two or three people, get a generous bunch of ramps or two small ones. Cut off the bulbs including an inch or an inch and a half of the purple part of the stalk; clean these by pulling the microscopically thin outer layer off under water, and trim off the root. Cut the bulbs into one-inch lengths and cut the thicker ones in half on the bias. With the leaves, tear out any particularly tough bits of stem, and tear each leaf in half lengthwise; the fibrous structure of the leaves makes them easy to tear. Wash and drain everything carefully.

    Cut half a pound of mushrooms into . . . well, to say 3/8-inch dice is a little silly with something the shape of a mushroom, but that‚Äôs the kind of size you‚Äôre looking for. In a 10-inch skillet, saut√© them, seasoned with salt and plenty of black pepper, in two tablespoons of butter over medium heat until a lot of their liquid has come out and they‚Äôre lightly browned; this took me six minutes.

    Drop the heat to low, stir in the ramp bulbs and cover the skillet. Stir every now and again and continue to cook until the ramps are tender, about 10 or 15 minutes. Check for salt and pepper, and leave aside until dinner time.

    Meanwhile, you‚Äôve got some soft polenta cooking, made with a mixture of milk and water if you like. If you are going the fish route, you‚Äôve also taken some tasty fillets (like porgy), seasoned them and breaded them using an egg wash and crumbs made from decent bread. Japanese panko would work too, even though it is made from bread that is just plain weird.

    When you‚Äôre ready to eat, turn on the heat under the ramp bulbs and mushrooms to medium-high. Add half a cup of heavy cream and when it comes to the boil put in the ramp greens and stir around with tongs; in no time, they‚Äôll wilt, but they‚Äôll remain slightly chewy, which is part of the point. Simultaneously, fry the breaded fish in clarified butter or a neutral-tasting oil.

    To serve, check the seasoning of the ramp-mushroom hash and put some of it on each plate. Top it with fried fish (or don‚Äôt) and plop some polenta down next door. A spritz of lemon juice on the fish is a good idea. The parsley you see in the photograph was pretty but entirely unnecessary.

    Today’s column reminded me of a persistent question I’ve had for a long time: how to prepare duck breasts that are cooked no more than medium rare in the center yet have crispy skin. Simply grilling them straight away with no prior preparation always yields constant “roaring flame ups” from the amount of fat that drips onto the flavor bars (I have a Weber gas grill), and although the meat is okay the breasts end up with lots of fat remaining, and with scorched but soft skin.

    I have tried to cut slits in the skin and fat and then boil the breasts for a time to ‚Äúcook away‚Äô some of the fat before grilling the breasts. That never quite reduces the amount of fat sufficiently before the meat itself has reached a point of almost being done.

    The closest I have come is to cut slits in the skin and fat in several places and then sear the breasts skin side down in a hot cast iron frying pan to render the fat. This manages to reduce the amount of fat substantially and fairly quickly, without ‚Äúpre-cooking‚Äù the meat too much. But I still cannot get the skin crisp, whether I follow the searing by broiling the breasts in the oven or by grilling them.

    Gary May 21, 2008 · 5:06 pm

    Al — you’re on the right track with the cast iron skillet. Score the skin on the duck breasts and cook them skin side down over medium or so heat. Let them cook on the skin side until they’re almost as done as you want them, then turn them for a few minutes to cook the rawness out of the other side.

    As they’re cooking on the skin side, you’ll probably need to pour out the accumulated fat several times — hold the duck breasts in place with tongs as you pour. (Pour the fat into a heatproof container and save for future use frying other things, or making confit, etc.) It will take some time to render out the fat, and crisp up the skin. The secret is to cook 90% of the time on the skin side, and just briefly on the other side to finish the cooking.

    Charlene (#9): A ramp is a variety of wild onion; it has a small bulb, and the green part is not cylindrical like a scallion or leek but rather has broad leaves. It is as much a symbol of “Appalachian Spring” as is the Copland tone poem of that name. To my mind, its earthy taste, together with time of year when it becomes available, is a reminder that the earth itself is awakening after its winter hibernation, and a harbinger of the sweet corn and tomatoes that are only a few months away.
