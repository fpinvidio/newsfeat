    A Barbecue Sauce That Transforms Chicken

    By Annemarie Conte
    June 5, 2009 2:16 pm June 5, 2009 2:16 pm
    Emily Weinstein/The New York Times

    Not long ago, I had a delightful conversation with Chris Lilly, barbecue champ and author of the new cookbook “Big Bob Gibson’s BBQ Book,” where he extolled the virtues of gathering friends and family together for great barbecue. So when Andy and I had a few friends over for a meal in the sunshine, I decided to try out his formula for a good time.

    Mr. Lilly is executive chef of Big Bob Gibson Bar-B-Q in Decatur, Ala., and I was particularly intrigued by the restaurant’s tangy mayonnaise-based white sauce, which he said was great with poultry. I chose chicken partly because I only had two hours before guests arrived, and that didn’t leave me enough time to slow-cook his Eight-Time World Championship Pork Shoulder for the required 14 to 16 hours.

    I’m not normally a huge fan of chicken, but the simple one-hour brine helped the meat retain some moisture and imparted enough flavor to keep it from being such a snore. But once the grilled breasts (over charcoal, not wood) were dipped in the white sauce, the magic truly happened. The creamy, peppery sauce had a slight edge of heat that was transformational. By the end of the meal, we were pouring white sauce on everything we could find—grilled mushrooms, corn, bread, whatever — just so we could get some more of it.

    Print Recipe

    Brined Chicken with White Sauce

    Yield Serves 8

    Time 1 hour brining, 12 minutes cooking

    Chris Lilly

    Summary

    The cooking method here is direct heat. Suggested wood: hickory, pecan, oak.

    Ingredients
    • For the brine:
    • 1 cup apple juice
    • 1 cup water
    • 1 tablespoon salt
    • 1/4 tablespoon garlic powder
    • 1 tablespoon honey
    • 1/2 tablespoon dark brown sugar
    • 1/2 tablespoon soy sauce
    • 1/2 tablespoon fresh lemon juice
    • 8 boneless skinless chicken breasts
    • Big Bob Gibson Bar-B-Q White Sauce (see recipe below)
    Method
    • In a medium bowl, combine the brine ingredients and mix well. Add the chicken breasts, making sure they are completely covered. Cover the bowl and refrigerate for 1 hour. Remove the chicken breasts from the brine and wipe off the excess salt.
    • Preheat an outdoor grill to 400 degrees. Place the chicken breasts on the grate directly over the heat and grill for 5 to 6 minutes on each side, or until golden brown and firm to the touch. The internal temperature of the chicken breasts should be 160 degrees.
    • Submerge each chicken breast into a bowl of Big Bob Gibson Bar-B-Q White Sauce. Remove from the sauce and serve.

    Source: Adapted from "Big Bob Gibson’s BBQ Book" by Chris Lilly

    susan June 9, 2009 · 2:15 am

    Living in the south for the first 30 years of my life and tripping for barbeque in every direction, I had never come across the concept of a white sauce. Thankfully, serendipity presented itself in the form of this article after I had thawed the languishing contents of a freezer bag of several assorted cuts of meat – two expensive bone-in frenched pork chops, two ends of a boston butt and finally something I could only identify as maybe, top round of beef. Why not, I ventured, after all, the author stated they were searching for any vehicle to consume the sauce . . . I made the brine exactly as stated in the recipe and tossed all of it in together. I brined the meat for at least 10 hours and made a charcoal fire. Tossing together the white sauce, I realized I did not have horseradish . . . instead I added ample cayenne and a good tablespoon of habanero style hot sauce. I didn’t think the sauce was all that earthshaking and I didn’t quite know how it would all come together in the end. . . I thought it would make good cole slaw if nothing else and I turned some of it into a ranch dressing before the addition of the hot sauce with sweet herbs and the addition of some buttermilk. The remainder I applied as instructed, grillside to the assorted, charred chops and slices of beef. . . Eureka !!! The application of the heat to the sauce was transformative. It definitely can’t be judged simply from the bowl alone. Between the brine and the sauce, this is simply a recipe for if nothing else, resurrecting things from the freezer that need a little divine intervention. It was delicious.
