    Ask About Brooklyn Architecture

    By The New York Times
    April 14, 2008 3:45 pm April 14, 2008 3:45 pm

    This week, through Friday, Diana Lind will be answering questions about architecture and the interior design of Brooklyn brownstones.

    • Read Ms. Lind’s answers to selected questions posted April 16, April 17 and April 18.

    We are no longer accepting comments on this feature.

    Ms. Lind is the author of “Brooklyn Modern: Architecture, Interiors & Design,” which takes a look at the innovative architecture and interiors from Bedford-Stuyvesant to Brooklyn Heights and Cobble Hill to Ditmas Park. She is also the editor in chief of Next American City magazine, a quarterly examination of urban issues, community activism, culture and politics. She was a freelance architecture and design writer for Architectural Record, Art + Auction, Plenty and other magazines.

    Ms. Lind edited “Designing the Hamptons: Portraits of Interiors” (Edizioni Press, 2006) and founded the short-lived magazine, Work. She received her B.A. in English from Cornell and an M.F.A. in creative writing from Columbia. Ms. Lind was born and raised in Manhattan, lived in Fort Greene for three years, and moved to Philadelphia this month.

    K.V. Olesen April 14, 2008 · 5:15 pm

    My wife and I lived on the top floor of a row house on Bay Ridge Parkway (75th Street) between 5th and 6th Avenues in Bay Ridge for the first 10 years of our marriage. Our building was apparently a part of a development that was built around 1910 or so and was quite lovely – pocket doors, inlaid parquet floors, built in details, with a limestone-clad front, these were very much upper middle class homes. It is my understanding that one house on the block (near 5th Avenue, the only one with some extra width and a garage) was both the office of the developer and the “show house” for the development and was subsequently built first. Prospective buyers could tour that house and choose the various items that could be customized (build in cabinets, decorative detailing, tile work, etc).
    If this is (roughly) true, was this a common way for sections of row houses to be developed at that time?

    Andrew April 14, 2008 · 10:33 pm

    I’ve talked with a lot people who live around the Williamsburg/Greenpoint area about all the new construction going up. It seems you can’t go a single block on any street without noticing at least one new residential building going up. About 95% of these buildings are being built in the pseudo-modernist style (ie. colored brick, cheap metallic panels, and large glass windows). The general consensus is that these buildings are incredibly tacky, overpriced, and generally poor excuses for modern architecture. Because of this, many people feel like they are destroying the old neighborhoods’ character and lifestyle. However, if you look at the existing architecture, you’ll notice that most of it in this area is cheap frame housing with little to no aesthetic significance or warehouses. Do you believe this new wave of construction is undermining the classic Brooklyn neighborhoods or just another shift in the continually evolving urban environment that is Brooklyn? Could these new buildings perhaps be looked back upon in 30-50 years with that same admiration that we now reserve for the classic brownstone?

    Rob April 15, 2008 · 9:05 am

    Can you comment on the trend of renovating brownstones in ways that are not historically appropriate?

    We recently purchased a run-down brownstone in Prospect Heights, and began a renovation of the interior that can in no way be called a restoration: changing the floorplan dramatically, removing original woodwork and details (to be sold and reused, not trashed), and massively upgrading the systems. While from the street, you will always see a conforming brownstone, the interior is being radically modified to fit our lifestyle.

    Some people regard this as heresy, and say that if you purchase a brownstone you have a duty to maintain if not restore it: otherwise why buy a brownstone? We obviously do not agree.
